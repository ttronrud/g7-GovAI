**Detailed Summary of the *Online News Act* (Canada)**

---

### **Overview**

The *Online News Act* is a landmark piece of Canadian legislation enacted by the Parliament of Canada to regulate **digital news intermediaries**â€”online platforms such as search engines, social media services, and content aggregatorsâ€”and to ensure **fairness, sustainability, and journalistic independence** in the Canadian digital news marketplace. The Act is designed to address growing concerns about the **power imbalance** between large tech platforms and independent, local news outlets, particularly in terms of how news content is accessed, monetized, and distributed.

Its primary objective is to **enhance the financial sustainability of Canadian news businesses**, especially small, independent, and local onesâ€”including Indigenous and official language minority community news outletsâ€”by establishing a structured, transparent, and equitable framework for compensation and access to digital news content.

## **Key Provisions and Structure**

### **1. Definitions**

The Act establishes precise definitions essential to understanding its scope and application:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------|
| **Digital News Intermediary** | An online platform (e.g., Google, Facebook, YouTube) that makes news content available to users in Canada. Excludes private messaging services (e.g., WhatsApp, Telegram). |
| **News Business** | Any individual or entity operating a news outlet in Canada. |
| **News Content** | Any contentâ€”audio, video, textâ€”that reports on, investigates, or explains current public issues. Includes Indigenous storytelling. |
| **News Outlet** | An entity whose primary purpose is to produce news content. Includes Indigenous and official language minority community outlets. |
| **Indigenous News Outlet** | An outlet operated by an Indigenous person or community, primarily producing news for Indigenous peoples. |
| **Official Language Minority Community** | English-speaking communities in Quebec and French-speaking communities outside Quebec. |
| **Operator** | The individual or entity that runs or controls a digital news intermediary. |
| **Eligible News Business** | A news business designated by the Commission as meeting specific criteria for participation in bargaining. |

> *Note: The Act emphasizes fairness, journalistic independence, freedom of expression, and inclusion of diverse voicesâ€”especially Indigenous and minority communities.*

### **2. Scope and Application**

The Act applies to a **digital news intermediary** if there is a **significant bargaining power imbalance** between its operator and news businesses, based on three key factors:

1. **Size of the intermediary or operator**
2. **Market advantage** (e.g., dominance in search or social media)
3. **Prominent market position**

> If such imbalance exists, the operator must **notify the Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC)**.

**Exclusions:**
- Does **not apply** to broadcasting undertakings (as defined in the *Broadcasting Act*).
- Does **not apply** to telecommunications service providers acting solely in that capacity.

---

### **3. The Role of the CRTC**

The **CRTC (Commission)** is central to the Actâ€™s implementation and enforcement:

- Must maintain and **publish a public list** of digital news intermediaries to which the Act applies.
- Must conduct **public consultations** before making exemption or interim orders.
- Must **publish reasons** for decisions (e.g., granting or denying exemption, repealing orders).
- Must **publish all exemption and interim orders** on its website.
- Is **not subject to the Statutory Instruments Act** for these orders or lists (ensuring faster, more flexible decision-making).

### **4. Exemption Orders (Main Mechanism for Fairness)**

An operator may apply for an **exemption from the Actâ€™s requirements** (e.g., mandatory compensation, bargaining obligations) **if** the following conditions are met:

#### âœ… Criteria for Exemption:
The agreements between the operator and eligible news businesses must:
1. Provide **fair compensation** for news content.
2. Ensure **a significant portion of compensation is used to support local, regional, and national news production**.
3. **Protect journalistic independence and freedom of expression** from corporate influence.
4. **Contribute to the sustainability** of the Canadian news marketplace.
5. **Benefit a significant portion of independent local news businesses**, including those in underserved regions and communities.
6. Include **diverse business models** (non-profit and for-profit), serving all provinces, territories, anglophone and francophone communities, and racialized communities (e.g., Black, Indigenous).
7. Ensure **significant benefits to Indigenous news outlets** and **official language minority community news outlets**.

### **5. Interim Orders (Emergency Pathway)**

If the Commission **cannot grant a full exemption** but believes the operator is taking **good-faith steps** to meet the criteria within **one year**, it may issue an **interim order**.

#### Conditions for Interim Order:
- Operator has entered into agreements with eligible news businesses.
- Commission finds the agreements **do not yet meet all exemption criteria**.
- Commission believes the operator will **achieve compliance within one year**.
- Operator must take **measurable actions** to improve the agreements.

#### Features:
- Includes **conditions on actions being taken**.
- May require **public consultations** to be held in Canada.
- Also subject to **Treasury Board approval** if it exempts the operator from section 82 or related provisions.
- Expires at the end of the **one-year period**.

> This provides a **flexible, time-bound pathway** for operators to transition to full compliance without immediate penalties.

### **6. Bargaining Process (Mandatory Negotiation Framework)**

When a news business initiates the process, the Commission oversees a **structured negotiation process**:

#### ðŸ” Stages:
1. **Negotiation Period**: 90 days.
2. **Mediation Period**: 120 days (if no agreement is reached).
3. **Final Offer Arbitration**: 45 days (only if mediation fails and at least one party requests it).

> All stages are **limited to issues related to the making available of news content**â€”not copyright, advertising, or other unrelated matters.

#### ðŸ“Œ Key Rules:
- Only **eligible news businesses** (as defined) can initiate the process.
- The operator **must participate** in the process.
- All parties must act in **good faith**.
- The process **excludes copyright disputes** if:
  - Payments have already been made under a license or approved tariff.
  - The content is already covered by a legal license.

> This ensures that **copyright issues are not used as a barrier** to fair negotiation.

#### ðŸš« Exclusion of Copyright in Mediation:
- If the operator has paid or offered to pay under a valid license or tariff, **copyright use is not subject to mediation or arbitration**.

- If a digital news intermediary has a **covered agreement** (e.g., a negotiated compensation deal), it is **not liable for copyright infringement** related to that content.

### **7. Designation of Eligible News Businesses**

The Commission determines which news businesses are **eligible to participate** in bargaining and are entitled to fair compensation.

#### Criteria for Eligibility:
- Must be a **qualified Canadian journalism organization** (e.g., under Income Tax Act) or a licensed broadcaster (e.g., community station, native station).
- Produces **news of public interest**, primarily focused on **general issues and current events**, including democratic institutions.
- **Regularly employs two or more journalists in Canada** (including partners or non-armâ€™s-length employees).
- Operates **in Canada**, with editing and design done in Canada.
- Does **not focus on narrow topics** (e.g., sports, lifestyle, entertainment).
- Follows **journalistic ethics** (e.g., fairness, independence, rigor) or has a code of ethics aligned with professional standards.
- **Indigenous news outlets** are eligible if they:
  - Operate in Canada.
  - Produce news on matters of general interest, including Indigenous rights (e.g., self-government, treaty rights).

#### Exclusions from Eligibility:
- A business **cannot be designated** if:
  - It was previously designated and revoked under section 59(1)(c).
  - It is subject to **UN, Special Economic Measures, or Sergei Magnitsky sanctions**.
  - Its **headquarters are in a sanctioned foreign state**.

> The Commission must **revoke eligibility** if a business falls into a sanctions category.

#### Public List:
- The Commission maintains and **publishes a public list** of eligible news businesses.
- Businesses must **consent** to being listed.
