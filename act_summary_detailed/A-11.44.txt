### **Detailed Summary of the *Antarctic Environmental Protection Act***  

The *Antarctic Environmental Protection Act* (AEPA) is a comprehensive piece of Canadian legislation designed to uphold Canada’s international commitments to the protection of the Antarctic environment, particularly through the implementation of the **Antarctic Treaty System**. The Act establishes a legal framework to ensure that human activities in Antarctica are conducted in a manner that preserves the region’s unique and fragile ecosystems, in accordance with the principles of peace, scientific research, and environmental sustainability.

---

## **I. Background and Purpose**

### **1. International Obligations**
Canada is a party to several key international agreements related to Antarctica:
- The **Antarctic Treaty** (1959), which designates Antarctica as a scientific preserve and bans military activity.
- The **Convention for the Conservation of Antarctic Seals** (1978).
- The **Convention on the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources** (1980).

These agreements emphasize the importance of protecting Antarctica as a natural reserve devoted to **peace and science**, and the AEPA ensures that Canada fulfills its obligations under these treaties.

### **2. Core Purpose**
The primary purpose of the Act is:
> *"To protect the Antarctic environment, particularly by implementing the Protocol on Environmental Protection to the Antarctic Treaty."*

This means that the Act translates international environmental commitments into enforceable domestic law, ensuring that all Canadian activities in Antarctica are environmentally responsible and scientifically justified.

---

## **II. Key Definitions**

The Act provides clear definitions of key terms used throughout the legislation:

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Antarctic** | Includes the continent, islands south of 60°S, adjacent continental shelves, and all sea and airspace south of 60°S. |
| **Canadian** | A Canadian citizen or permanent resident, or a Canadian corporation (provincial or federal). |
| **Canadian vessel/aircraft** | As defined under the *Canada Shipping Act, 2001* and the *Aeronautics Act*. |
| **Canadian expedition** | Organized in Canada or departing from Canada. |
| **Master** | Anyone in command of a vessel (excluding pilots). |
| **Minister** | The designated minister of the Queen’s Privy Council for Canada responsible for administering the Act. |
| **Permit** | A permit issued by the Minister under Section 21. |
| **Place** | Includes platforms, shipping containers, or conveyances. |
| **Protocol** | The *Protocol on Environmental Protection to the Antarctic Treaty* (1991), as amended. |
| **Registered owner** | As defined in the *Aeronautics Act*. |
| **Treaty** | The original *Antarctic Treaty* (1959), as amended. |
| **Vessel** | Any boat, ship, or craft used on or above water (excluding fixed platforms). |

> **Note:** Unless otherwise stated, terms have the same meaning as in the Treaty or Protocol.

## **III. Prohibitions and Restrictions on Activities in Antarctica**

The Act imposes strict limitations on what Canadians and Canadian vessels may do in Antarctica, with the goal of minimizing environmental disturbance.

### **1. Entry into Antarctica**
- **No Canadian person** may enter the Antarctic **without a permit or written authorization from another Party** to the Protocol.
- Exceptions:
  - Travelling through or on the high seas to an immediate destination outside Antarctica.
  - Fishing for profit (sole purpose).

### **2. Canadian Stations**
- No Canadian individual may be present at a Canadian station in Antarctica without a permit.

### **3. Canadian Vessels**
- No Canadian vessel may enter Antarctica without a permit or authorization from another Party.
- Exceptions:
  - Traveling through or on the high seas to an immediate destination outside Antarctica.
  - Fishing for profit.

### **4. Canadian Aircraft**
- No Canadian aircraft may operate in Antarctica without a permit or authorization from another Party.
- Exception: Traveling to an immediate destination outside Antarctica.

### **5. Mineral Resource Activities**
- **Prohibited**: Any activity related to mineral resources (exploration, recovery, exploitation).
- **Exception**: Scientific research conducted under a permit or authorization from another Party.

### **6. Disturbance of Native Species and Habitats**
The Act strictly prohibits activities that could disturb native wildlife or degrade habitats:

| Prohibited Activity | Explanation |
|--------------------|-----------|
| Killing, injuring, capturing, or molesting native mammals or birds | Applies to all stages of life (e.g., eggs, chicks). |
| Removing or damaging native plants | Must not significantly affect local distribution or abundance. |
| Flying or landing aircraft in a way that disturbs birds or seals | Especially during breeding or moulting seasons. |
| Using vehicles or vessels that disturb birds or seals | Includes hovercrafts and small boats. |
| Using explosives or firearms that disturb wildlife | Any use that causes disturbance is prohibited. |
| Wilfully disturbing breeding/moulting birds or seal concentrations on foot | Even minor disturbances are prohibited. |
| Significantly damaging terrestrial plants via landing, walking, or driving | Applies to all forms of ground contact. |
| Engaging in activities causing significant adverse modification of habitats | Applies to all native species (mammals, birds, plants, invertebrates). |

> **Definitions of "Native" Species**:
> - **Native bird**: Any bird indigenous to Antarctica or present seasonally via migration.
> - **Native invertebrate**: Any terrestrial or freshwater invertebrate indigenous to Antarctica.
> - **Native mammal**: Any mammal indigenous or seasonally occurring in Antarctica.
> - **Native plant**: All terrestrial or freshwater vegetation (including lichens, fungi, algae) at any life stage.

### **7. Introduction of Non-Indigenous Species**
- **Prohibited**: Introducing any animal or plant species not indigenous to Antarctica.
- **Exceptions**:
  - Seasonal migrants (e.g., birds or mammals that naturally migrate).
  - Food items (excluding poultry or live animals).

### **8. Introduction of Substances**
- **Prohibited**: Introducing into Antarctica any substance or product specified in regulations (e.g., chemicals, pollutants).

### **9. Access to Specially Protected Areas**
- No Canadian or vessel may enter **Antarctic Specially Protected Areas (ASPA)** without a permit or authorization from another Party.

### **10. Protection of Historic Sites**
- No Canadian or vessel may damage, destroy, or remove any part of an **historic site or monument** designated under regulations.

### **11. Waste Management**
- **Prohibited**: Disposing of waste in Antarctica without a permit or authorization.
- **Strict Prohibitions**:
  - Burning waste in open air.
  - Disposing of waste in ice-free areas or freshwater systems.
- **Vessel Discharges**:
  - **Oil, oily mixtures, or food waste**: Only allowed with a permit or authorization.
  - **Garbage, plastic, or harmful substances**: Prohibited from being discharged into the sea.
  - **Sewage**:
    - Vessels with >10 passengers **must not discharge untreated sewage within 12 nautical miles of land or ice shelves**.
    - **No instantaneous discharge** of sewage from holding tanks.

> **Note**: "Garbage" includes all domestic, operational, and victual waste, excluding fresh fish and parts.

## **IV. Permit System**

The Act establishes a robust permit system to regulate all activities in Antarctica.

### **1. Who Can Apply?**
- Any individual or entity (including Canadian vessels) may apply for a permit.

### **2. Application Requirements**
- Must be in the form and contain information prescribed by regulations.
- The Minister may require additional information.

### **3. Conditions of Permits**
- The Minister may include any conditions deemed appropriate.
- Conditions may apply to individuals, vessels, or expeditions.

### **4. Permit Authority**
- The Minister may:
  - Issue, renew, suspend, or cancel permits.
  - Refuse a permit if public interest requires it.

### **5. Environmental Assessments**
The Minister must conduct environmental evaluations before issuing a permit:

| Evaluation Level | Trigger |
|------------------|--------|
| **Preliminary Evaluation** | Required for all applications. |
| **Initial Evaluation** | If activities likely to have **minor or transitory** environmental impact. |
| **Comprehensive Evaluation** | If activities likely to have **more than minor/transitory** impact. |

> **Final Rule**: The Minister **cannot issue a permit** if a comprehensive evaluation shows that the activity will cause a **significant adverse environmental impact** that cannot be justified.

### **6. Required Plans**
A permit authorizing an expedition, vessel, or aircraft must include:
- A **waste management plan**.
- An **emergency plan**.

### **7. Security Requirements**
- Applicants must provide security (financial guarantee) to cover costs related to environmental damage.
- Security may be used to reimburse Canada for reasonable costs incurred in preventing, mitigating, or remediating environmental harm.
- Security is required for vessels or expeditions with high risk or large scale.

---

## **V. Regulatory Authority**

The **Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal Cabinet) may make regulations to implement the Act, including:

| Regulatory Powers |
|-------------------|
| Form and content of permit applications |
| Who may apply for permits |
| Issuance, renewal, cancellation, suspension of permits |
| Types of activities permitted |
| Specific substances/products banned (Section 14) |
| Designation of **Antarctic Specially Protected Areas (ASPA)** |
| Designation of **historic sites and monuments** |
| Environmental impact assessments (Section 23)
