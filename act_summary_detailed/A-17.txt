### **Detailed Summary of the Auditor General Act**

---

#### **1. Introduction and Citation**
The Act is formally titled the **Auditor General Act** and establishes the structure, powers, responsibilities, and protections of the **Auditor General of Canada**. It serves as a foundational piece of legislation that ensures accountability, transparency, and integrity in the financial operations of the federal government.

---

#### **2. Key Definitions**
The Act defines several key terms used throughout:

- **Appropriate Minister**: As defined in Section 2 of the *Financial Administration Act* (FAA), this refers to the minister responsible for financial administration (currently the Minister of Finance).
- **Auditor General**: The individual appointed to serve as the federal auditor of Canada, as per subsection 3(1) of the FAA.
- **Department**: As defined in Section 2 of the FAA, refers to federal government ministries or agencies.
- **Crown Corporation**: Defined under Section 83 of the FAA — a corporation owned by the Crown.
- **Designated Entity**: Same meaning as in the *Federal Sustainable Development Act* — entities designated for sustainability reporting or oversight.
- **Funding Agreement**: As defined in subsection 42(4) of the FAA — formal agreements between the government and recipients for financial support.
- **Recipient**: As defined in subsection 42(4) of the FAA — any entity receiving public funds under a funding agreement.
- **Registrar**: The Bank of Canada and a registrar appointed under Part IV of the FAA; responsible for managing securities and financial instruments.
- **Sustainable Development**: Defined as development that meets present needs without compromising future generations’ ability to meet theirs — a core principle of environmental and fiscal responsibility.

> **Note**: Several outdated definitions have been repealed:
> - *Category I Department* (repealed 2019)
> - *Not-for-profit corporation* (repealed 2006)
> - *Recipient Corporation* (repealed 2006)
> - *Sustainable Development Strategy* (repealed 2008)

---

#### **3. Appointment and Tenure of the Auditor General**

- **Appointment Process**:
  - The **Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal Cabinet) appoints the Auditor General by commission under the Great Seal.
  - Appointment requires **consultation** with:
    - Leader of the Government in the Senate or Government Representative in the Senate
    - Leader of the Opposition in the Senate
    - Leader or Facilitator of every other recognized party or parliamentary group in the Senate
    - Leader of every recognized party in the House of Commons
  - The appointment must be **approved by resolution** of both the **Senate and House of Commons**.

- **Term of Office**:
  - The Auditor General serves for **10 years** during good behaviour.
  - Can be **removed only for cause** by the Governor in Council on an address (formal motion) from both the Senate and House of Commons.

- **Re-appointment Prohibition**:
  - Once a person has served as Auditor General, they are **not eligible for re-appointment**.

- **Interim Appointment**:
  - In case of absence or incapacity, the Governor in Council may appoint a **qualified auditor** to serve temporarily for up to **six months**.
  - The interim auditor receives the same salary and expenses as the Auditor General.

- **Salary**:
  - The Auditor General is paid a salary **equal to that of a puisne judge of the Supreme Court of Canada**, reflecting the high level of independence and authority.

---

#### **4. Legal and Pension Provisions**

- **Public Service Superannuation Act** applies to the Auditor General, **except for tenure of office**.
- A person appointed from outside the public service may, within **60 days of appointment**, opt to join the **Diplomatic Service (Special) Superannuation Act** pension plan instead.
  - This allows flexibility in retirement benefits while preserving independence.

---

#### **5. Core Responsibilities of the Auditor General**

##### **A. Auditing Financial Statements**
- The Auditor General is the **auditor of the accounts of Canada**, including those of the **Consolidated Revenue Fund**.
- Must examine:
  - Financial statements required under **Section 64 of the Financial Administration Act** (included in Public Accounts).
  - Any other financial statements presented by the **President of the Treasury Board** or the **Minister of Finance** for audit.
- Must express an opinion on whether the statements:
  - Present fairly information in accordance with stated government accounting policies.
  - Are consistent with the prior year’s financial reporting.
  - Include any **reservations** if material misstatements or inconsistencies are found.

##### **B. Annual Reporting to the House of Commons**
- The Auditor General must submit **one annual report** to the House of Commons, which must be laid before the House by the **Speaker**:
  - By **December 31** of the fiscal year.
  - If the House is not sitting, laid on **any of the first 15 days** after it resumes.
- The report must highlight:
  - Any instances where:
    1. Accounts were not faithfully maintained or public funds were not properly accounted for or paid into the Consolidated Revenue Fund.
    2. Essential records were not kept or controls were inadequate (risking misuse of public property or revenue).
    3. Funds were spent outside the purposes for which they were appropriated.
    4. Spending lacked economy or efficiency.
    5. No effective procedures exist to measure and report program effectiveness.
    6. Environmental effects of expenditures were ignored in the context of **sustainable development**.

##### **C. Additional Reports (Up to Three per Year)**
- The Auditor General may make **up to three additional reports** in any year to the House of Commons.
- These reports can cover:
  - General work of the office.
  - Whether the Auditor General received all necessary information and explanations.
- **Notice Requirement**:
  - Must send a **written notice** to the Speaker of the House of Commons at least **30 days before** submitting the report.
  - If a longer period is specified, that time applies.
- The report is submitted within **30 days** (or as specified) after notice is sent and laid before the House as per the annual report rules.

##### **D. Special Reports**
- May issue a **special report** on any matter of **pressing importance or urgency** that cannot wait until the next annual report.
- Must be submitted to the Speaker and laid before the House **immediately upon receipt**, or on the **first day after the House resumes sitting**.

---

#### **6. Inquiries into Recipients of Public Funds**

The Auditor General may inquire into recipients under **funding agreements** regarding:

1. Failure to meet obligations under the funding agreement.
2. Misuse of funds — lack of economy or efficiency.
3. Failure to establish procedures to measure and report on program effectiveness.
4. Inadequate accounting or record-keeping.
5. Failure to consider environmental effects in line with **sustainable development**.

- Findings from such inquiries may be included in:
  - The annual report.
  - Any of the three additional reports.
- The Auditor General may also highlight any significant findings that should be brought to the attention of Parliament.

---

#### **7. Access to Information and Powers**

- **Free Access to Information**:
  - The Auditor General has the right to **free access at all convenient times** to information relevant to his duties.
  - May **require and receive** reports, explanations, and records from any federal public administration officer.

- **Examination on Oath**:
  - May examine any person on oath about matters related to accounts.
  - Has the same powers as a **commissioner under Part I of the Inquiries Act**.

- **Reliance on External Audits**:
  - May rely on the audit reports of **duly appointed auditors** of Crown corporations or their subsidiaries.

- **Request for Information from Crown Corporations**:
  - May request information from directors, officers, employees, agents, or auditors of a Crown corporation or its subsidiaries.
  - If the corporation fails to provide sufficient information, the Auditor General may advise the Governor in Council, who may then **direct officers to provide access** to records, books, vouchers, and documents.

---

#### **8. Staffing and Administrative Authority**

- **Appointment of Staff**:
  - Officers and employees necessary to carry out duties are appointed under the **Public Service Employment Act (PSEA)**.
  - The PSEA applies to these staff, **except** for specific provisions related to:
    - Hearing allegations by candidates (Sections 118–119).
    - Making regulations.

- **Employer and Deputy Head Powers**:
  - The Auditor General may exercise the powers of:
    - **Employer** and **deputy head** under the PSEA.
    - Specifically, for **human resources management**, including:
      - Determining terms and conditions of employment.
      - Managing employer-employee relations.
      - Setting salaries and benefits.
  - These powers are **not subject to Treasury Board approval**.

- **Delegation of Authority**:
  - The Auditor General may delegate powers and functions to any employee in the office.
  - Any authorized person may further delegate authority to others under their supervision.

---

#### **9. Appointment of the Commissioner of the Environment and Sustainable Development**

- The Auditor General appoints a **Commissioner of the Environment and Sustainable Development**.
- This person reports **directly to the Auditor General**.
- The Commissioner assists the Auditor General in fulfilling duties related to **environmental protection and sustainable development**.
