### **Detailed Summary of the Referendum Act, Canada**

The **Referendum Act** is a foundational piece of legislation in Canada that establishes the legal framework for conducting referendums—direct votes by the people on constitutional or other significant national issues. Enacted by Her Majesty, with the advice and consent of both the Senate and the House of Commons, this Act provides comprehensive rules governing the **initiation, approval, conduct, financial regulation, and oversight** of referendums in Canada.

Below is a detailed, structured summary of the key provisions and their implications:

---

## **1. Purpose and Scope of the Act**

- **Title**: The Act is titled the *Referendum Act*.
- **Primary Objective**: To enable the federal government to obtain the public’s opinion on constitutional or other major questions through a formal, transparent, and democratic referendum process.
- **Applicability**: Applies specifically to referendums called by proclamation under the authority of the Governor in Council (the federal Cabinet), with strict procedural safeguards to ensure fairness, public interest, and democratic legitimacy.

> **Key Provision**:  
> *"Where the Governor in Council considers that it is in the public interest to obtain by means of a referendum the opinion of electors on any question relating to the Constitution of Canada, the Governor in Council may, by proclamation, direct that the opinion of electors be obtained by putting the question to the electors of Canada or of one or more provinces specified in the proclamation at a referendum called for that purpose."*

This grants the federal government discretion to initiate a referendum on constitutional matters, but only when it deems such a process in the public interest.

---

## **2. Definitions (Key Terms in the Act)**

The Act defines critical terms used throughout:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------|
| **Chief Agent** | The person recorded in the official registry as the principal representative of a registered referendum committee. Responsible for administrative and financial compliance. |
| **Contribution** | Money or commercial value of goods/services (excluding volunteer labor) provided to support a referendum. Excludes normal business loans or costs incurred at market value. |
| **Electoral District** | Any territorial area entitled to return a Member of Parliament (MP) in a general election. Used to define geographic boundaries for referendum voting. |
| **Federal Party / Provincial Party** | Political parties with elected members in the House of Commons or provincial legislatures on the day writs are issued. Includes parties that were elected immediately before dissolution. |
| **Oppose / Support** | To vote "no" or "yes" on a referendum question, respectively. |
| **Polling Day** | The day specified in the writs when voting occurs. |
| **Proclamation** | The official government directive initiating a referendum. |
| **Referendum Committee** | Any individual or group intending to spend over **$5,000** on referendum-related activities. |
| **Referendum Expenses** | All costs directly related to supporting or opposing a referendum question, including: <br> - Advertising (broadcasting or publications) <br> - Hiring personnel (paid services) <br> - Meeting space, refreshments, promotional materials <br> - Goods/services from government (with commercial value) <br> ❌ Excludes costs incurred by MPs in their official duties. |
| **Referendum Finances Return** | A mandatory financial report filed by the chief agent of a registered committee, including receipts, invoices, and vouchers. |
| **Referendum Period** | Begins when the referendum question is approved and ends on polling day. |
| **Referendum Question** | The specific question put to voters (must allow a "yes/no" vote). |
| **Registered Referendum Committee** | A committee formally registered under section 13. |
| **Support / Oppose** | Being in favour of voting "yes" or "no" to the question. |

> **Note**: Unless otherwise stated, all terms have the same meaning as in the *Canada Elections Act*.

## **3. Process for Approving a Referendum Question**

This section outlines the **democratic and procedural steps** required to approve a referendum question before a proclamation is issued.

### **Step-by-Step Approval Process**:

1. **Initiation by Government (Governor in Council)**  
   - The Governor in Council may issue a proclamation to hold a referendum on a constitutional or significant issue.

2. **Requirement for Public Consultation**  
   - A motion for approval of the referendum question must be introduced in the House of Commons **in accordance with procedures** under section 4.1 of the *Salaries Act* (a member of the Queen’s Privy Council may propose it).

3. **Consultation with Political Parties**  
   - The **Leader of the Opposition** and the leaders of all federal parties with **12 or more members** in the House of Commons must be consulted **at least three days before** the motion is introduced.

4. **House of Commons Session Rules**  
   - After consultation begins, the House must sit **beyond normal adjournment hours** each day until no further debate occurs (but not later than midnight).  
   - This ensures adequate time for discussion and scrutiny.

5. **Motion Considered and Decided**  
   - The motion is debated and considered on the next sitting day after notice is given.  
   - After **three days of sitting**, or earlier if ready, the Speaker **immediately puts the motion to a vote without further debate or amendments**.

6. **Senate Concurrence Required**  
   - If the House adopts the motion (with or without amendment), it sends a message to the Senate requesting concurrence.  
   - The Senate reviews the motion and may propose amendments.  
   - If amended, the House must consider the amendment on the next sitting day, and the process repeats in the Senate.

7. **Final Approval**  
   - Only after **both Houses agree** (with or without amendments) can a proclamation be issued.

> ⚠️ **Critical Restrictions**:
> - No proclamation can be issued:
>   - When the House of Commons is dissolved.
>   - Before the referendum question is approved under section 5.
>   - More than **45 days after** the question is approved.

> ✅ **Multiple Questions**: A single proclamation may include **more than one referendum question**.

---

## **4. Conduct of the Referendum (Writs, Polling, and Voting)**

### **Issuance of Writs**
- The **Chief Electoral Officer (CEO)** issues writs of referendum in accordance with the proclamation.
- Writs are sent to the **returning officers** (electoral officials) for each electoral district.
- Writs must be issued:
  - **No earlier than 36 days before polling day**.
  - **Not during a general election period**.
- If general election writs are issued during the referendum period, the referendum writs are **automatically withdrawn**.

> **Withdrawal Procedure**:
> - Upon withdrawal, the CEO publishes a notice in the *Canada Gazette*.

### **Ballot Paper and Language Access**
- The CEO may **modify the ballot paper** to ensure clarity and ease of understanding.
- The text of the referendum question must be available in **Indigenous languages**, in locations and formats determined through consultation with Indigenous groups.

## **5. Financial Regulations and Spending Limits**

This section establishes **strict financial rules** to prevent undue influence, ensure transparency, and maintain fairness.

### **Spending Limits**
- **Individuals or groups (non-registered)**:
  - May not incur referendum expenses totaling more than **$5,000** during the referendum period.

- **Registered Referendum Committees**:
  - May spend **up to a maximum amount** calculated as:
    > **(Number per elector) × (Total number of names on preliminary voter lists)**  
    where:
    - For the year beginning April 1, 1992: **56.4 cents per elector**
    - For any subsequent year: **30 cents × the fraction published under subsection 39(2) of the Canada Elections Act**

> This means spending is **proportional to the size of the electorate** in the districts the committee is active in.

### **Sources of Contributions**
- **Prohibited Sources**:
  - Contributions from:
    - Non-Canadian citizens or permanent residents
    - Corporations not carrying on business in Canada
    - Trade unions without bargaining rights in Canada
    - Foreign governments or their agents

> This ensures that funding comes from Canadian residents and entities with local ties.

### **Financial Accountability**
- All registered committees must:
  - Keep detailed records of **all contributions and expenses** as prescribed by the CEO.
  - Maintain accounts with:
    - A member of the **Canadian Payments Association** (COPA)
    - Or a **local cooperative credit society** that is part of a central cooperative in COPA

> **Financial Reporting**:
> - A **Referendum Finances Return** must be filed with the CEO by the chief agent, including all bills, vouchers, and receipts.

---

## **6. Appointment of Agents and Witnesses at Polling Stations**

To ensure fair and transparent voting, the Act establishes clear roles for agents and witnesses.

### **Agent Appointment**
- A registered committee may appoint an agent (in writing) to be present at each polling station in districts where it supports or opposes the question.

### **Witness Appointment**
- If **no agent** from a supporting or opposing committee is present, the returning officer must appoint a **witness** who supports or opposes the question to act as a witness at the polling station.

> **Rights and Obligations**:
> - Each agent and witness has **all the rights and responsibilities** of a candidate
