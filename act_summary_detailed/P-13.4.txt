### **Detailed Summary of the *Physical Activity and Sport Act***  

The **Physical Activity and Sport Act** is a comprehensive federal legislation enacted by the Government of Canada to promote, support, and develop physical activity and sport across the country. It reflects a national commitment to improving public health, fostering social cohesion, enhancing cultural diversity, and advancing economic opportunities through active living and participation in sport. Below is a detailed and structured summary of the Act, covering its background, objectives, key provisions, governance structure, and accountability mechanisms.

---

## **I. Background and Policy Foundations**

The Act begins with several **"Whereas" clauses** that establish the foundational rationale for the legislation:

1. **Cultural and Societal Importance**:  
   Physical activity and sport are recognized as integral parts of Canadian culture and society, contributing to:
   - Health and well-being  
   - Social cohesion  
   - Linguistic duality (English and French)  
   - Economic activity  
   - Cultural diversity  
   - Quality of life  

2. **Policy Goals**:  
   - Increase awareness of the health and societal benefits of physical activity.  
   - Encourage Canadians to adopt active lifestyles and participate in sport.  
   - Remove barriers—especially for marginalized or underrepresented groups—that prevent participation.  

3. **Ethical Principles in Sport**:  
   The Government’s policy on sport is grounded in **high ethical standards**, including:
   - Doping-free sport  
   - Fairness, respect, and inclusion for all participants  
   - Equitable access and participation  
   - Transparent, fair, and timely dispute resolution  

4. **Inter-Governmental and Cross-Sector Cooperation**:  
   The Act emphasizes collaboration among:
   - Federal, provincial, and territorial governments  
   - Non-governmental organizations (NGOs) and community groups  
   - The private sector  

This framework ensures that physical activity and sport are promoted not only as health initiatives but also as tools for **social development, equity, and national identity**.

---

## **II. Core Objectives of the Act**

The primary **objects** of the Act are to:
- Encourage, promote, and develop physical activity and sport in Canada.

To achieve these objectives, the **Minister of the Crown** (designated by the Governor in Council) is empowered to take a wide range of actions, including:

| Key Powers of the Minister |
|---------------------------|
| Conduct or support research and studies on physical activity and sport |
| Organize national and regional conferences |
| Recognize achievements through certificates, citations, or awards |
| Prepare and distribute public information about physical activity and sport |
| Collaborate with stakeholders (e.g., communities, NGOs, private sector) |
| Coordinate federal initiatives related to: |
| – Promotion of physical activity and sport |
| – Hosting major sporting events (e.g., Canada Games) |
| – Anti-doping measures |
| Support projects and programs related to physical activity and sport |
| Assist in promoting Canadian participation in international sport |
| Provide training for coaches and other sport professionals |
| Offer bursaries or fellowships to individuals pursuing excellence in sport |
| Promote sport as a tool for individual and social development, both domestically and internationally |
| Encourage private sector financial contributions to sport development |
| Facilitate inclusion of underrepresented groups (e.g., Indigenous peoples, persons with disabilities, racialized communities) |
| Encourage provincial and territorial governments to promote sport |
| Coordinate federal efforts around the staging of the **Canada Games** |
| Support **alternative dispute resolution (ADR)** in sport |

## **III. Establishment of the Sport Dispute Resolution Centre of Canada (the "Centre")**

A key innovation of the Act is the creation of a **not-for-profit corporation** called the **Sport Dispute Resolution Centre of Canada**.

### **Key Features of the Centre**:

#### 1. **Legal Status and Independence**
- Not an agent of Her Majesty  
- Not a departmental or Crown corporation under the *Financial Administration Act*  
- Not considered a federal tribunal under the *Federal Courts Act*  
- Operates independently of government bureaucracy  

#### 2. **Mission**
To provide:
- A **national alternative dispute resolution (ADR) service** for sport-related disputes  
- Expertise and support in ADR practices  

#### 3. **Scope of Sport Disputes Covered**
Includes:
- Disputes between sport organizations and their members  
- Disputes among sport organizations  

#### 4. **Legal Capacity and Powers**
Despite being a non-profit, the Centre has the **capacity of a natural person**, allowing it to:
- Use funds provided to it (subject to terms)  
- Enter into contracts in its own name  
- Conduct studies  
- Carry out activities conducive to its mission  

#### 5. **Restrictions on Powers**
The Centre **cannot**:
- Acquire or construct real property for valuable consideration (except for head office)  
- Procure shares in a corporation where those shares would be held on behalf of or in trust for the Centre  
- Acquire shares that would be held in trust for the Centre  

#### 6. **Governance Structure**
- **Board of Directors**:
  - Maximum of **12 directors**, including the chairperson  
  - Executive director (who does **not vote**)  
  - Terms: Up to **3 years**, with no more than **half of directors** having overlapping terms in any one year  
  - Directors may serve **up to two consecutive terms**  
  - Can be removed by the Minister for cause  

- **Appointment Process**:
  - Appointed by the Minister  
  - Must be chosen in accordance with **Ministerial guidelines** that ensure:
    - Diversity in gender, background, and experience  
    - Representation of the sport community  
    - Reflection of Canada’s bilingual and diverse society  

- **By-Laws**:
  - Can be established by the board to govern:
    - Committees (including an executive committee)  
    - Roles and responsibilities of chairperson and executive director  
    - Appointment and remuneration of officers  
    - Delegation of powers  
    - Dispute resolution procedures (including selection of arbitrators/mediators)  
    - Language options (English/French) based on parties’ needs  
    - Code of ethics for directors, officers, and mediators  
    - Personnel management policies  
  - By-laws are **not statutory instruments** under the *Statutory Instruments Act*  

#### 7. **Chairperson**
- Designated by the Minister (for up to 3 years, renewable once)  
- Presides over board meetings  
- Can be replaced if absent/incapacitated; temporary replacement allowed for up to **90 days** with Minister’s approval  

#### 8. **Executive Director**
- Chief executive officer of the Centre  
- Responsible for day-to-day operations  
- Can delegate powers to others  
- If absent, the chairperson may appoint a temporary replacement (up to 90 days with board approval)  

#### 9. **Employees and Advisors**
- The Centre may hire employees and technical advisers as needed  
- Directors, officers, and employees are **not considered public servants** under the *Public Service Superannuation Act*  
- They are **not employees of the federal public administration**

---

## **IV. Financial and Administrative Oversight**

### **Funding and Financial Assistance**
- The Minister may provide **grants and contributions** to individuals or organizations, in accordance with:
  - *Parts IV and VII of the Official Languages Act*  
  - Federal funding rules for language and cultural programs  

### **Agreements with Provinces/Territories**
- The Minister may enter into agreements with provinces or territories to:
  - Share costs of programs promoting physical activity or sport  
  - Implement the federal policy on sport  
- Agreements require **Governor in Council approval**

### **International Agreements**
- The Minister may enter into agreements with **foreign governments** to promote physical activity and sport abroad  

---

## **V. Accountability and Transparency**

To ensure transparency and public trust, the Act includes strong **accountability mechanisms**:

### **Corporate Planning**
- The Centre must prepare a **corporate plan annually**, covering:
  - Objectives  
  - Strategies (operational, financial, human resources)  
  - Operating and capital budgets  
- Must deliver the plan to the Minister **at least 30 days before the fiscal year begins**  
- After delivery, the plan is made **public**  
- A copy is **tabled in both Houses of Parliament** within 15 days of receipt  

### **Annual Reporting**
- The **chairperson** submits an annual report to the Minister within **four months after the fiscal year ends**, including:
  - Financial statements and auditor’s report  
  - Summary of the corporate plan  
  - Performance against objectives  
- The report is made **public**  
- A copy is **tabled in both Houses of Parliament** within 15 days of receipt  

### **Public Engagement**
- Within **60 days of submitting the annual report**, the Centre must hold a **public meeting** in a Canadian city of its choice  
- Must provide **at least 30 days’ notice** of the meeting  
- The meeting allows public discussion of performance and future directions  

### **Audit and Financial Controls**
- The Centre is audited annually by an **independent auditor** appointed by the board  
- The auditor’s report is submitted to the board and reviewed by the **audit committee**  
- The **audit committee** (minimum of 3 directors) has the authority to:
  - Review internal controls  
  - Evaluate financial statements  
  - Meet with the auditor and management  
  - Report concerns directly to the Minister if necessary  
  - Call special board meetings if needed  
- The committee may engage experts temporarily to assist in its duties
