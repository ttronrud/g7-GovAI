## üîç **Detailed Summary of the Canada-United States Tax Convention Act, 1984**

### üìú **Overview**
The *Canada-United States Tax Convention Act, 1984* (commonly referred to as the **Canada-United States Tax Convention Act**) is a Canadian statute that implements the **Canada-United States Income Tax Convention of 1984**‚Äîa bilateral agreement designed to:
- Avoid **double taxation** of income and capital gains,
- Prevent **fiscal evasion** (tax avoidance or fraud),
- Promote **tax fairness and certainty** for individuals and businesses operating across both countries.

This Act gives legal force and domestic effect to the Convention in Canada, ensuring that its provisions are enforceable within Canadian tax law.

## üîë Key Provisions and Structure

### 1. **Citation and Definitions**
- **Title**: The Act may be cited as the *Canada-United States Tax Convention Act, 1984*.
- **"Convention"** refers to the tax treaty between Canada and the U.S., as set out in **Schedule I**, and amended by Protocols in **Schedules II‚ÄìVI**.
- The Convention is **approved and declared to have the force of law** in Canada during the period it is in force.
- In case of **conflict**:
  - The Convention **prevails over any conflicting domestic law**.
  - The *Income Tax Conventions Interpretation Act* prevails over the Convention **to the extent of inconsistency**.
- The **Minister of National Revenue** may issue regulations to implement the Convention.
- The **Minister of Finance** must publish a notice in the *Canada Gazette* within 60 days of the Convention‚Äôs entry into force or termination.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è Note: The Convention entered into force on **August 16, 1984** (SI/84-233).

### 2. **Purpose and Scope of the Convention**
The Convention aims to:
- Prevent double taxation of income and capital gains.
- Prevent fiscal evasion through tax avoidance strategies.
- Provide clarity and consistency in tax treatment for residents of either country.

#### üåç **Applicability**
- Applies to **persons who are residents** of one or both countries.
- Covers:
  - **Taxes on income** (e.g., income from employment, business, investments).
  - **Taxes on capital** (e.g., gains from sale of assets).
- Applies to:
  - Existing taxes (as defined below),
  - Any **identical or substantially similar** taxes introduced after the Convention's signing,
  - Taxes on capital imposed after the Convention‚Äôs date of signature.

#### üìö Specific Taxes Covered:
- **Canada**:
  - Taxes under **Parts I, XIII, and XIV of the Income Tax Act** (e.g., income tax, capital gains tax).
- **United States**:
  - Federal income taxes under the **Internal Revenue Code (IRC)**.

> Special exceptions allow limited application of certain U.S. taxes:
- **Accumulated earnings tax** and **personal holding company tax** ‚Äì only to the extent needed to implement **Article X (Dividends)**.
- **Excise taxes on private foundations** ‚Äì only to implement **Article XXI (Exempt Organizations)**.
- **Social security taxes** ‚Äì only to implement **Article XXIX (Miscellaneous Rules)**.

### 4. **Residency Determination (Key Principle)**

Determining residency is critical for determining where income is taxed.

#### For Individuals:
1. **Permanent home**:
   - Resident of the country where they have a permanent home available to them.
2. **Centre of vital interests**:
   - If home is in both or neither country ‚Üí resident of the country with **closer personal and economic ties**.
3. **Habitual abode**:
   - If centre of vital interests cannot be determined ‚Üí resident of the country with **habitual abode**.
4. **Citizenship**:
   - If no habitual abode ‚Üí resident of the country of **citizenship**.
5. **Mutual agreement**:
   - If citizenship is shared or neither ‚Üí competent authorities resolve via **mutual agreement**.

> ‚úÖ Exception: An individual who is employed by a government entity in one country (e.g., working in the U.S. for a Canadian government body) and is subject to similar income tax obligations in that country **shall be deemed a resident** of the country where employed.

> üìå Spouse and dependent children of such individuals are also deemed residents of that country.

#### For Companies:
- If created under the laws of a Contracting State ‚Üí deemed a **resident of that State**.

#### For Estates, Trusts, or Other Entities:
- If resident of both countries ‚Üí **competent authorities must resolve** via mutual agreement.

> üîç **Note**: Residency determines which country has taxing rights over income.

### 5. **Permanent Establishment (PE) ‚Äì Key Concept**

A **permanent establishment (PE)** is a fixed place of business through which a resident conducts business in another country.

#### Included:
- Place of management
- Branch
- Office
- Factory
- Workshop
- Mine, oil/gas well, quarry
- Building site or construction project **if lasting more than 12 months**
- Drilling rig or ship used for exploration/exploitation of natural resources **for more than 3 months in any 12-month period**

#### Excluded:
- Use of facilities for **storage, display, or delivery** of goods
- Maintenance of stock for storage, display, or delivery
- Maintenance of stock for processing by another party
- Purchase of goods or collection of information
- Advertising, research, or auxiliary activities (preparatory or auxiliary)

> üö´ **Important**: A resident‚Äôs business through a **broker, agent, or commission agent** (acting independently) **does not** constitute a PE.

> ‚úÖ **Control relationships do not create a PE**:
- If a Canadian company controls a U.S. company, or vice versa, **neither is a PE of the other**.

> üí° The PE concept is used to determine where business profits are taxed.

### 6. **Taxation of Business Profits**

#### General Rule:
- A resident‚Äôs business profits are taxable **only in their home country**.
- **Exception**: If the resident operates through a PE in the other country ‚Üí profits attributable to that PE may be taxed in the foreign country.

#### How Profits Are Attributed:
- The profits are attributed to the PE using the **arm‚Äôs length method**:
  > ‚ÄúThe profits which the PE would make if it were a separate entity, engaged in the same or similar activities under the same or similar conditions, dealing independently with the resident and other related parties.‚Äù

#### Deductions:
- All **expenses incurred** for the PE (including executive and administrative costs) are allowed as deductions.
- **No deduction** required for expenses that are not generally allowed under domestic tax law.

#### Exclusions:
- Profits from **mere purchase of goods**, **provision of managerial/administrative services**, or **use of facilities** are **not attributable** to a PE.

> üìÖ Profits are determined **year by year**, unless there is good reason to vary.

> üö´ Profits that are already addressed in other Articles (e.g., royalties, dividends) are **not affected** by this rule.

> ‚úÖ Business profits attributed to a PE **include only those derived from the PE‚Äôs assets or activities**.
