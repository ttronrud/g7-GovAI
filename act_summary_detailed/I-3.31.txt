### 2. **Tax Agreements with Foreign Countries (Section 3)**

> *If a bilateral tax treaty or convention between Canada and another country limits the tax rate on payments to residents of that country, any reference in Part XIII of the amended Act to a higher rate shall be read as the specified rate. Additionally, the taxpayer shall be deemed to have no permanent establishment in Canada for the purpose of the treaty, unless the amount is attributable to business activity in Canada.*

#### Purpose:
To **respect international tax treaties** and prevent double taxation or excessive taxation of cross-border payments.

#### Key Points:
- If a treaty sets a **maximum tax rate** (e.g., 10%), any reference in the Income Tax Act to a higher rate (e.g., 25%) is **read down** to the treaty rate.
- The taxpayer is **deemed to lack a permanent establishment in Canada** unless the payment is reasonably attributable to business operations in Canada.
- This avoids tax liability in Canada for income from foreign sources that are already taxed under a treaty.

#### Practical Implication:
Foreign investors receiving income from Canada may be taxed at a reduced rate under a treaty, and Canadian businesses may avoid being taxed on foreign income if the income is not linked to Canadian operations.

### 3. **Continuity of Old Law Provisions (Section 4)**

> *A reference in any enactment to a provision of the amended Act shall be construed, as regards any transaction or matter to which the old law applied, to include a reference to the corresponding provision in the old law.*

#### Purpose:
To ensure **legal continuity** and avoid gaps in tax treatment when transitioning from old to new law.

#### Key Points:
- If a transaction existed before the 1970 amendments, the rules from the **old law** (e.g., 1948 Act or pre-1972 rules) are **presumed to apply** unless otherwise excluded.
- The old law provisions are interpreted as being **"reasonably related"** to the same subject matter as the current law.

#### Practical Implication:
Taxpayers can rely on pre-existing deductions, exemptions, or rules from earlier years when computing income or tax liability, even if those rules were not directly mirrored in the new Act.

### 4. **Continuation of Parts IV and VIII of the Former Act (Section 5)**

> *Part IV of the former Act (gifts) continues in force but does not apply to gifts made after 1971.*  
> *Part VIII (business and property rules) continues in force, with references to the old Act read as references to the current Act (except for amendments via Section 1 of Chapter 63).*

#### Purpose:
To preserve certain **legacy rules** for specific types of transactions while limiting their scope.

#### Key Points:
- **Part IV (gifts)**: Applies only to gifts made **before or in 1971**. No tax on gifts after that date.
- **Part VIII**: Continues in force, but references to old provisions are **reinterpreted** to refer to the current Act (1952, as amended), unless the amendment was via Section 1 of Chapter 63.

#### Practical Implication:
Taxpayers must ensure that gifts made after 1971 are not subject to gift tax under the old rules. However, pre-1971 gifts remain subject to the old rules.

### 5. **Clarification of Provisions by Number (Section 6)**

> *A reference by number to a provision of the Income Tax Act that refers to Part IV, Part VIII, or a provision with the same number in the amended Act shall be read as referring to the provision in the old law, unless otherwise stated.*

#### Purpose:
To prevent **misinterpretation or misapplication** of provisions due to numbering inconsistencies.

#### Key Points:
- If a provision in the new Act is numbered the same as one in the old Act, it is **interpreted as referring to the old provision**.
- This avoids confusion when a provision in the new Act has a different meaning or scope.

#### Practical Implication:
Taxpayers and assessors must check whether a numbered reference points to a provision in the old law or the current law, especially when dealing with legacy cases.
