**Detailed Summary of the *Canada–European Union Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement Implementation Act***  

---

### **Overview**  
The *Canada–European Union Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement Implementation Act* (CETA Implementation Act) is the primary Canadian legislative instrument enacted to implement the **Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement (CETA)** between Canada and the European Union (EU), signed in Brussels on October 30, 2016. This Act provides the legal framework for Canada to fulfill its obligations under CETA, including establishing trade rules, creating institutional mechanisms, and ensuring compliance with the Agreement’s objectives.

The Act was passed by the Parliament of Canada with the advice and consent of both the Senate and the House of Commons and came into force in stages, with key provisions activated in 2017 and later updated through amendments and transitional provisions.

---

## **Key Provisions and Detailed Summary**

---

### **1. Definitions and Scope**
The Act begins by defining key terms used throughout:
- **Agreement**: Refers to the CETA signed on October 30, 2016, between Canada and the EU and its Member States.
- **CETA Joint Committee**: The body established under Article 26.1 of CETA to oversee implementation and resolve disputes. It is a key institutional mechanism for cooperation.
- **Federal law**: Includes any Act of Parliament or regulatory instrument made under a federal law.
- **Minister**: Refers specifically to the **Minister for International Trade**, who serves as Canada’s principal representative in the CETA Joint Committee.

> *Note:* The Act emphasizes that all provisions must be interpreted in a manner consistent with the Agreement, reinforcing its primacy.

---

### **2. Exclusions and Limitations**
To clarify scope and intent:
- **No application to natural surface or ground water**: Nothing in the Act applies to natural surface, ground, liquid, gaseous, or solid water resources. This excludes environmental regulation of water bodies from the scope of CETA implementation.
- **Parliament’s legislative authority is preserved**: The Act does not limit Parliament’s power to pass laws to implement CETA or fulfill Canada’s obligations under it. This ensures that Parliament retains full authority to legislate in response to evolving trade needs.

---

### **3. Purpose and Objectives of the Agreement**
The Act explicitly outlines the **core objectives** of CETA, which are:
1. **Establish a free trade area** between Canada and the EU.
2. **Promote reciprocal trade** to foster economic development and harmonious economic relations.
3. **Ensure fair competition** in trade between Canada and the EU.
4. **Increase investment opportunities** while preserving each party’s right to regulate for legitimate policy goals (e.g., public health, safety).
5. **Eliminate trade barriers** in goods and services to expand world and regional trade.
6. **Provide adequate protection and enforcement of intellectual property rights** (IPR) in the territories covered by the Agreement.
7. **Protect, enhance, and enforce basic workers’ rights**, strengthen labor cooperation, and uphold international labor commitments.
8. **Strengthen environmental laws and regulations**, promote environmental cooperation, and support sustainable development.

These objectives reflect the broader vision of CETA as a comprehensive trade agreement that goes beyond simple tariff reductions to include investment, labor, environment, and IP protections.

---

### **4. Legal Enforceability and Judicial Access**
A significant provision limits access to legal remedies:
- **No individual action without Attorney General’s consent**: No person may bring legal proceedings or enforce rights arising solely from:
  - Sections 9 to 14 of the Act (which deal with dispute resolution and enforcement mechanisms).
  - The Agreement itself (except for specific exceptions).

> This provision is designed to prevent individuals from challenging CETA provisions in courts, which could undermine the stability and enforceability of the Agreement.

#### **Exception**:
- The restriction does **not apply** to:
  - **Section F of Chapter Eight** of CETA (related to dispute settlement and investor-state mechanisms).
  - **Article 13.21** of the Agreement (which deals with specific investment protection or dispute resolution provisions).

This exception allows for greater access to dispute resolution mechanisms for investors or parties involved in specific investment disputes.

---

### **5. Institutional Roles and Representation**
The Minister for International Trade is designated as the **principal representative of Canada** on the **CETA Joint Committee**.

The Minister has authority to:
- Propose individuals to serve on **tribunals** established under Section F of Chapter Eight (dispute resolution).
- Propose individuals to be included in **sub-lists** under:
  - **Paragraph 1 of Article 29.8** (related to dispute settlement and transparency).
- The **Minister of Finance** can propose individuals for sub-lists under **paragraph 3 of Article 13.20** (investment-related matters).
- The **Minister of Labour** can:
  - Propose individuals for the list under **paragraph 6 of Article 23.10** (labor-related expert panels).
  - Propose a **chairperson** for a Panel of Experts on labor issues.
- The **Minister of the Environment** can:
  - Propose individuals for the list under **paragraph 6 of Article 24.15** (environmental expert panels).
  - Propose a **chairperson** for an environmental expert panel.

> These provisions ensure that Canada’s key ministries have a role in shaping the composition of expert bodies responsible for reviewing compliance and resolving disputes.

---

### **6. Government Responsibility for Costs**
The Government of Canada is responsible for paying its **appropriate share** of:
- **Tribunal expenses** (including member remuneration and operating costs).
- **Arbitration panel and Panel of Experts** costs (including fees for arbitrators, mediators, and experts).
- **CETA Joint Committee and related bodies** (e.g., working groups, bilateral dialogues, specialized committees), including remuneration for members and representatives.

> This ensures that Canada bears its fair share of the administrative and operational costs of implementing CETA.

---

### **7. Suspension Powers (Governor in Council)**
The **Governor in Council** (i.e., the Cabinet or federal executive) may, under **Article 29.14 of CETA**, issue an order to **suspend obligations** under the Agreement if necessary. The powers include:
- Suspending rights or privileges granted to EU countries, goods, service suppliers, or investors.
- Modifying or suspending the application of federal laws related to the EU.
- Extending the application of federal laws to the EU.
- Taking any other measure deemed necessary.

> This power allows Canada to respond to unforeseen risks (e.g., public health emergencies, national security threats, or economic shocks) by temporarily suspending certain obligations under CETA.

> **Duration**: Unless repealed, such orders remain in effect for the period specified in the order.

---

### **8. Effective Dates and Transitional Provisions**
The Act does **not come into force immediately**. Instead, it is implemented in stages via orders made by the Governor in Council:

| Provision | Effective Date Condition |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **General provisions (excluding sections 133–137)** | To be fixed by order of the Governor in Council. |
| **Sections 8(3), 11(1)(a), 11(2), 13(a), and 90** | Must come into force on or after the general date (but not earlier). |
| **Sections 32(1), (4)** | To be activated by order of the Governor in Council. |
| **Sections 32(2), 34(1), (2), 36, 38–42** | Must come into force on or before the general date. |
| **Sections 45 to 58** | Come into force on a date not earlier than the general date. (Note: These were activated on **June 30, 2021**, per SI/2021-25.) |

> **Note**: The Act was initially in force on **September 21, 2017**, for most provisions (SI/2017-47), with some key sections (like those related to dispute settlement and investment) activated later.

---

### **9. Repeals and Amendments**
Several provisions have been **repealed or amended**:
- Sections **133 to 137** were **repealed before coming into force** (2017, c. 6, s. 137).
- Certain provisions were **repealed in 2018** (c. 27, s. 209).
- Amendments were made over time to refine roles, clarify responsibilities, and align with evolving policy priorities (e.g., labor, environment, investment).

> These changes reflect ongoing legislative refinement and political review of CETA’s implementation.
