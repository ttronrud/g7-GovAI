**Detailed Summary of the *Fall Economic Statement Implementation Act, 2022***

The *Fall Economic Statement Implementation Act, 2022* is a legislative instrument enacted by the Parliament of Canada—by and with the advice and consent of the Senate and House of Commons—to implement specific measures and amendments arising from the 2022 Fall Economic Statement. While the Act does not introduce new broad economic policy, it provides a series of targeted legal changes to support fiscal policy, regulatory adjustments, and administrative reforms. Below is a detailed and structured summary of the key provisions, grouped by thematic areas.

---

### **1. Implementation of the Underused Housing Tax Regulations**

This section establishes the legal framework for the **Underused Housing Tax (UHT)** regime, which aims to encourage the use of underutilized housing stock (e.g., vacant or underoccupied properties) by imposing a tax on owners who fail to use their properties appropriately.

#### Key Provisions:
- **Section 116 Amendments**:
  - **Section 1, subsection 2(1), and Section 3 of the Underused Housing Tax Regulations** are brought into force or deemed to have come into force on **December 31, 2022**.
  - **Subsections 2(2) and (3)** of the regulations apply to the **2022 calendar year and all subsequent years**, ensuring continuity and long-term application of the tax rules.
  
- **Legislative Authority and Validity**:
  - The regulations are **deemed to have been made under section 84 of the Underused Housing Tax Act**, which authorizes the Minister to create regulations for the UHT.
  - For **statutory instrument compliance**, the regulations are deemed to have been:
    - Transmitted to the **Clerk of the Privy Council** for registration (as required under the *Statutory Instruments Act*).
    - Met the **publication requirements** under subsection 11(1) of that Act.

> **Purpose**: To ensure that the UHT regime is legally sound, properly authorized, and transparently implemented, with immediate effect from December 31, 2022.

---

### **2. Canada Growth Fund and the Canada Development Investment Corporation (CDIC)**

This section outlines the financial and governance mechanisms for the **Canada Growth Fund**, a public investment vehicle designed to support private sector growth and innovation in Canada.

#### Key Provisions:
- **Section 118**:
  - The **Minister of Finance** may acquire and hold **non-voting shares** in a **wholly-owned subsidiary** of the **Canada Development Investment Corporation (CDIC)**.
  - This subsidiary is responsible for **administering the Canada Growth Fund**.

- **Funding Authority**:
  - The Minister may draw funds from the **Consolidated Revenue Fund** (up to **$15 billion** in total, or any greater amount specified in an appropriation Act) to **acquire shares** in the subsidiary.
  - The total amount available is capped at **$15,000,000,000** (or more if authorized by Parliament via appropriation).

- **Governance Clarification**:
  - The subsidiary is **not considered an agent** of His Majesty in right of Canada. This clarifies that the subsidiary operates independently and does not act as a direct representative of the Crown in financial or administrative matters.

> **Purpose**: To provide a clear, legally robust mechanism for the federal government to invest in private sector growth through the Canada Growth Fund, while maintaining fiscal accountability and avoiding direct agency involvement.

---

### **3. Repeal of the First Nations Land Management Act (FNLM Act)**

This section marks a significant structural change in Indigenous land governance policy.

#### Key Provisions:
- The **First Nations Land Management Act (chapter 24 of the Statutes of Canada, 1999)** is **repealed**.
- This repeal is not a standalone policy change but part of a broader transition to a new land management framework.

#### Transitional Safeguards:
- **Definition of "Former Act"**:
  - In sections 123 and 124, "former Act" refers to the **1999 First Nations Land Management Act**.
  - Terms such as *First Nation law*, *Framework Agreement*, *interest*, *land code*, *licence*, and *right* are defined to have the **same meaning as in subsection 2(1) of the former Act**, immediately before the day section 143 comes into force.

- **Continuity of Existing Rights and Obligations**:
  - The Act **does not affect**:
    - Any land codes, individual agreements, First Nation laws, or documents issued, granted, or entered into **before the Act comes into force**.
    - Actions or decisions made prior to the Act’s implementation, including:
      - Registration of interests or rights in the **First Nation Land Register**.
      - Establishment of **priorities or rankings** among interests or rights (especially in Quebec).
  - Any reference to the former Act in existing legal instruments is **read as a reference to the Framework Agreement** or relevant provisions of that Agreement.

> **Purpose**: To legally transition from the 1999 FNLM Act to a new land management framework without disrupting existing Indigenous land rights, agreements, or administrative processes. It ensures continuity and legal certainty for First Nations and stakeholders.

---

### **4. Apprentice Loans Regulations – Transition and Repayment Schedule**

This section addresses the implementation of a revised apprenticeship loan program, with a focus on **phasing in new repayment obligations**.

#### Key Provisions:
- **Definitions**:
  - *Apprentice loan*: Same meaning as in subsection 1(1) of the *Apprentice Loans Regulations*.
  - *Borrower*: Same meaning as in the regulations.
  - *Transition period*: The period from **April 1, 2023**, to the day when a regulation amending or repealing **section 4 of the Apprentice Loans Regulations** comes into force.

- **Repayment Requirements During Transition**:
  - During the transition period, borrowers must begin repaying:
    - The **principal amount** of their apprentice loan.
    - Any **interest** that would have accrued under the previous regulations **but for subsection 8(1) of the Apprentice Loans Act** (as amended by section 164).
  - This effectively shifts repayment responsibility from a deferred or deferred-interest model to a more immediate repayment schedule.

- **Effective Dates**:
  - This Division (the transition provisions) comes into force or is deemed to have come into force on **April 1, 2023**.
  - Other sections (146, 147, 150–152, 157, 158, subsections 159(2) and (3), and sections 160 and 166) come into force on a **day to be fixed by Governor in Council**, but **after March 31, 2023**.
    - *Note*: These were later implemented on **December 9, 2023**, via **Statutory Instrument SI/2023-78**.

- **Section 165**:
  - Deemed to have come into force on **April 1, 2021** (a retroactive effective date, likely for historical consistency or implementation purposes).

> **Purpose**: To modernize the apprentice loan program by introducing a clear, enforceable repayment timeline, aligning it with fiscal responsibility and financial sustainability, while minimizing disruption to existing borrowers.

### **Summary of Key Themes and Impacts**

| Theme | Key Impact |
|------|-----------|
| **Housing Policy** | Introduces and operationalizes the Underused Housing Tax, targeting underutilized properties to improve housing supply and affordability. |
| **Fiscal Investment** | Enables the federal government to invest in private growth via the Canada Growth Fund through a structured, transparent, and accountable mechanism. |
| **Indigenous Land Governance** | Repeals the 1999 First Nations Land Management Act, transitioning to a new framework while protecting existing rights, agreements, and administrative records. |
| **Apprenticeship Financing** | Imposes a transition to immediate repayment of apprentice loans, enhancing financial accountability and sustainability. |

---

### **Conclusion**

The *Fall Economic Statement Implementation Act, 2022* is a **pragmatic and targeted legislative tool** designed to operationalize key elements of Canada’s 2022 fiscal policy. It does not introduce sweeping new economic measures but rather provides the **legal foundation** for:

- A new housing tax regime,
- A more structured approach to public investment in growth,
- A major policy shift in Indigenous land governance,
- And a phased transition in apprenticeship financing.

Each provision is carefully drafted to ensure **legal validity**, **fiscal responsibility**, and **continuity of rights and obligations**—particularly in sensitive areas like Indigenous land rights and financial obligations for borrowers.

While the Act is relatively concise in form, its implications are wide-ranging, touching on housing, investment, Indigenous self-governance, and workforce development. It reflects a balanced approach to economic policy implementation—grounded in both fiscal prudence and legal rigor.
