**Detailed Summary of the *Director of Public Prosecutions Act***  

---

### **Overview**  
The *Director of Public Prosecutions Act* establishes a formal office of the **Director of Public Prosecutions (Director)** within the Canadian justice system. This Act creates a distinct, independent, and accountable body responsible for overseeing federal prosecutions, ensuring consistency, transparency, and public interest in the conduct of criminal proceedings. It operates under the authority of the **Attorney General of Canada**, but with specific powers and responsibilities designed to enhance prosecutorial independence and professionalism.

The Act is structured to ensure accountability, public transparency, and effective coordination between the federal government, law enforcement, the judiciary, and the public.

---

## **Key Provisions and Detailed Summary**

---

### **1. Citation and Definitions**

- **Title**: The Act may be cited as the *Director of Public Prosecutions Act*.
- **Key Definitions**:
  - **Attorney General**: Refers to the Attorney General of Canada — the principal legal advisor to the government and the head of the prosecution function.
  - **Prosecution**: Defined broadly as:
    - Prosecutions under the jurisdiction of the Attorney General;
    - Any proceeding (including appeals) related to such prosecutions;
    - Prospective prosecutions falling under the Attorney General’s jurisdiction.
    - *Exclusion*: Matters referred to in subsection 3(8) are excluded from this definition (likely related to specific sensitive or political matters).

---

### **2. Appointment of the Director**

- The **Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal Cabinet) appoints the Director **on the recommendation of the Attorney General**, in accordance with **Section 4**.
- The Director holds the **rank and status of a deputy head of a department**, reflecting their seniority and importance in the federal government structure.

---

### **3. Powers and Duties of the Director**

The Director has extensive powers and responsibilities, acting **on behalf of the Attorney General** but with a degree of independence. These include:

#### a) **Initiation and Conduct of Prosecutions**
- Initiates and conducts prosecutions for the Crown, **except where the Attorney General personally assumes control** (under Section 15).
- Conducts prosecutions specifically under the **Canada Elections Act** and related appeals or proceedings.

#### b) **Intervention in Matters of Public Interest**
- May intervene in any matter that raises a **question of public interest** affecting prosecution or investigation.
- **Exception**: Cannot intervene where the Attorney General has already decided to intervene (under Section 14).

#### c) **Issuance of Guidelines**
- Issues **guidelines for federal prosecutors** on the general conduct of prosecutions.
- These guidelines are **not statutory instruments** under the *Statutory Instruments Act*, meaning they do not require parliamentary approval or formal notice procedures.

#### d) **Advising Law Enforcement**
- Advises law enforcement agencies and investigative bodies on prosecution strategies or specific investigations.

#### e) **Public Communication**
- Communicates with the **media and public** regarding prosecution decisions and conduct — enhancing transparency and public trust.

#### f) **Private Prosecutions**
- Exercises the Attorney General’s authority in **private prosecutions** (e.g., where a private individual brings a criminal action).
- Can intervene, assume control, or direct a stay of such prosecutions.

#### g) **Other Powers and Duties**
- May perform any other duties or functions assigned by the Attorney General that are compatible with the office of Director.
- **All assignments must be in writing and published in the *Canada Gazette***.

#### h) **Agreements with Provincial Governments**
- May enter into **agreements or arrangements with provincial governments** on behalf of the Attorney General to coordinate prosecutorial efforts.

#### i) **Legal Authority**
- For the purpose of exercising these powers, the Director is considered the **Deputy Attorney General of Canada**.

---

### **4. Appointment Process – Selection Committee**

To ensure diversity, fairness, and public accountability, the appointment process includes a **selection committee** composed of:

1. A person named by the **Federation of Law Societies of Canada**;
2. A person named by **each recognized political party in the House of Commons**;
3. The **Deputy Minister of Justice**;
4. The **Deputy Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness**;
5. A person selected by the **Attorney General**.

#### Process:
- The Attorney General submits a list of **no more than 10 candidates**, each with **at least 10 years of bar membership**.
- The selection committee evaluates the candidates and **recommends three** to the Attorney General.
- The Attorney General selects one of the three as most suitable.
- The **appointment is referred to a parliamentary committee** designated by Parliament for approval.
  - If approved → the Attorney General recommends the appointment to the Governor in Council.
  - If not approved → the Attorney General refers to the committee for another candidate.

This process ensures **independence, public input, and democratic legitimacy** in the selection of the Director.

---

### **5. Term and Removal**

- The Director serves a **seven-year term during good behaviour**.
- **May be removed at any time by the Governor in Council** for cause, **only if supported by a resolution of the House of Commons**.
- The Director is **not eligible for reappointment** after the initial term.
- At the end of the term, the Director **continues in office until a successor is appointed**.

---

### **6. Conflicts of Interest and Employment Restrictions**

- The Director **must engage exclusively** in duties related to the office.
- **Cannot hold any other office or employment for reward**.
- This ensures **professional integrity and avoids conflicts of interest**.

---

### **7. Succession and Delegation**

- In case of **incapacity or vacancy**, the Governor in Council may appoint a **Deputy Director of Public Prosecutions (Deputy Director)** to act as Director.
- No person may act as Director for **more than 12 months without Governor in Council approval**.

---

### **8. Remuneration and Funding**

- The Director is paid **by the Governor in Council**.
- Once fixed, **remuneration cannot be reduced** — ensuring job security and stability.
- The Director may **retain barristers or advocates (in Quebec)** to act as federal prosecutors, with fees and expenses approved by the **Treasury Board**.

---

### **9. Deputy Directors**

- The Attorney General appoints one or more **Deputy Directors of Public Prosecutions** (must have at least 10 years of bar experience).
- **Appointment requires consultation** with a selection committee including:
  - The Director;
  - A representative of the Federation of Law Societies of Canada;
  - The Deputy Minister of Justice.
- Deputy Directors:
  - Operate **under the supervision of the Director**;
  - May exercise the same powers and duties as the Director;
  - Act as **lawful deputies of the Attorney General**;
  - May act on behalf of the Director in various matters.

---

### **10. Federal Prosecutors and Staff**

- Prosecutors required to carry out duties are **appointed under the Public Service Employment Act**.
- The Director may **retain barristers/advocates** (with Treasury Board approval) to assist in prosecutions.
- The Director may also engage **technical or specialized experts** (e.g., forensic scientists, cybercrime analysts) with approval from the Treasury Board.

---

### **11. Authorization and Delegation**

- The Director may **authorize federal prosecutors or other persons** to act on their behalf in the exercise of powers or performance of duties.
- This delegation is **not limited to the power to delegate** — it covers all powers and functions under the Act or other laws.
- Any person so authorized is considered an **agent of the Director** and does not need to prove such authorization.

---

### **12. Designation as Agent of the Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness**

- The Director, Deputy Directors, and authorized persons may be **designated as agents** of the Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness under **Section 185 of the Criminal Code**.

---

### **13. Directives from the Attorney General**

- The Attorney General may issue **written directives** to the Director regarding:
  - Specific prosecutions;
  - General prosecution practices.
- All directives must be **published in the *Canada Gazette***.
- The publication may be **delayed** if the Attorney General or Director considers it in the **interests of justice**.
- **Delay cannot exceed** the completion of the relevant prosecution or related proceedings.
- Directives are **not statutory instruments** under the *Statutory Instruments Act* — meaning they do not require parliamentary approval or go through the formal SI process.

---

### **14. Reporting and Accountability**

- The Director must submit an **annual report** to the Attorney General by **June 30** of each year, covering the previous fiscal year’s activities — **except in matters referred to in subsection 3(8)**.
- The Attorney General must **lay a copy of this report before each House of Parliament** within the first 15 days of a sitting session.

This ensures **transparency and parliamentary oversight**.
