**Detailed Summary of the Alternative Fuels Act (Canada)**

---

### **Overview**

The *Alternative Fuels Act* is a Canadian federal legislation enacted to advance environmental sustainability by reducing greenhouse gas emissions and air pollution from transportation. The Act mandates a phased transition of federal government motor vehicles from conventional petroleum-based fuels to **alternative fuels**, with the goal of minimizing environmental damage caused by internal combustion engines.

It was introduced in response to Canada’s broader commitment to environmental reform and climate action, particularly in addressing the harmful impacts of carbon dioxide and other air pollutants emitted by vehicles using fossil fuels. As a major user of such vehicles, the federal government is positioned to lead by example through policy-driven adoption of cleaner transportation technologies.

---

### **Key Provisions and Interpretation**

#### **1. Definitions**

The Act establishes key definitions that shape its scope and application:

- **Alternative Fuel**:  
  A fuel that:
  - Is used directly for propulsion in motor vehicles,
  - Is less damaging to the environment than conventional (petroleum-based) fuels,
  - Is prescribed by regulation.  
  *Examples include*: ethanol, methanol, propane gas, natural gas, hydrogen, and electricity (when used as a sole source of direct propulsion).

- **Motor Vehicle**:  
  Any motor vehicle of a class prescribed by regulation. Includes automobiles, passenger vans, and light-duty trucks.

- **Federal Body**:  
  Any entity listed in Schedules I, I.1, or II to the *Financial Administration Act*. This includes departments, agencies, and government offices.

- **Crown Corporation**:  
  A corporation listed in Schedule III to the *Financial Administration Act*, unless excluded by regulation.

> Note: The Act allows the Treasury Board to exclude specific Crown corporations from its provisions after consulting with their board of directors.

---

#### **2. Core Purpose and Objectives**

The primary objective of the Act is to **promote the replacement of petroleum-based fuels in government transportation** through a clear, measurable, and phased transition to alternative fuels.

Specifically, the Act mandates that:

> For each fiscal year beginning on April 1, **2004 and thereafter**, **at least 75% of all motor vehicles operated by federal bodies and Crown corporations** must be powered by alternative fuels — **provided that such transition is both cost-effective and operationally feasible**.

This reflects a long-term environmental policy goal: to reduce the environmental footprint of government transportation operations.

---

#### **3. Phased Implementation Targets**

To ensure a gradual and realistic transition, the Act sets out **specific, time-bound targets** for federal bodies and Crown corporations:

| Fiscal Year Commencing | Target Percentage of Vehicles Using Alternative Fuels |
|------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| April 1, 1997           | 50%                                                    |
| April 1, 1998           | 60%                                                    |
| April 1, 1999 and onward| 75%                                                    |

These targets apply to the **aggregate acquisition** of new motor vehicles by federal bodies and Crown corporations. This means that over time, the total fleet must meet or exceed these percentages, based on the number of vehicles being purchased.

> Importantly, the targets are not mandatory for individual departments but apply to the **collective vehicle purchases** of all federal bodies and Crown corporations.

---

#### **4. Operational Requirements**

Where it is **cost-effective and operationally feasible**, a federal body or Crown corporation must:

- **Use an alternative fuel** in the operation of any motor vehicle capable of running on such fuel.

This ensures that even if a vehicle is purchased as a conventional model, it should be operated using a cleaner fuel when possible — promoting real-world environmental benefits beyond just fleet composition.

---

#### **5. Regulatory Authority**

The **Treasury Board** holds broad powers to implement and adapt the Act through regulations, including:

- **Prescribing specific fuels** as "alternative fuels" (e.g., expanding or narrowing the list to include hydrogen or biofuels).
- **Defining classes of motor vehicles** eligible under the Act.
- **Establishing criteria** for determining what constitutes "cost-effectiveness" and "operational feasibility."
- Making general provisions for carrying out the Act’s purpose.

> The Treasury Board must consult with **industry representatives and environmental groups** before making any such regulations — ensuring stakeholder input and balancing economic and environmental concerns.

Additionally, the Treasury Board may take **other measures** (e.g., funding, procurement policies, or guidance) to support the Act’s implementation.

---

#### **6. Exemptions and Flexibility**

- The Act allows for **exclusion of specific Crown corporations** from its requirements, provided the Treasury Board consults with the corporation’s board of directors.
- The requirement to adopt alternative fuels is **conditional** on cost-effectiveness and operational feasibility — preventing mandates that would be impractical or financially burdensome.

This flexibility ensures that the policy remains realistic and adaptable to varying organizational needs and circumstances.

---

### **Environmental and Policy Rationale**

The Act is grounded in the following principles:

- **Environmental Protection**: Reduces emissions of greenhouse gases (especially CO₂) and harmful air pollutants from transportation.
- **Government Leadership**: The federal government, as a major consumer of motor vehicles, can set a precedent for private sector and public institutions to follow.
- **Market Incentive**: By mandating purchases of alternative-fuel vehicles, the Act stimulates demand, encouraging innovation, investment, and scaling of cleaner technologies.
- **Long-Term Sustainability**: Promotes a shift toward sustainable, low-carbon transportation systems.

---

### **Historical Context and Status**

- The Act was passed in the early 2000s (first enacted around 2003–2004), reflecting Canada’s early engagement with climate change and clean transportation policies.
- It was **repealed in 2012** (by *Statutory Instrument 19, c. 19, s. 221*) and replaced by more modern and comprehensive environmental and transportation policies, such as those under the *Clean Air Act* and the *Canada Climate Change Plan*.

> Despite its repeal, the *Alternative Fuels Act* remains historically significant as one of the first pieces of Canadian legislation to formally establish a **mandatory, time-bound, government-led transition to alternative fuels** in transportation.

---

### **Conclusion**

The *Alternative Fuels Act* was a pioneering piece of environmental legislation designed to:

- Reduce environmental damage from transportation,
- Drive innovation in alternative fuels and vehicle technologies,
- Position the federal government as a leader in sustainable practices,
- Create a measurable, phased transition toward cleaner mobility.

While the Act is no longer in force, its principles — particularly the emphasis on government-led action, cost-effectiveness, and environmental responsibility — continue to influence Canada’s current climate and transportation policies.

| **Applicable Entities** | Federal bodies and Crown corporations |
