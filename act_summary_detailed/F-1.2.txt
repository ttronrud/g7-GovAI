**Detailed Summary of the *Family Homes on Reserves and Matrimonial Interests or Rights Act***  

---

### **Overview**

The *Family Homes on Reserves and Matrimonial Interests or Rights Act* (commonly referred to as the **Family Homes Act**) is a landmark piece of federal legislation enacted by Parliament of Canada in 2023 to address critical gaps in family law on **First Nation reserves**. It recognizes that existing provincial and territorial family laws do not apply on reserves, and that the *Indian Act* fails to provide adequate protections for spouses or common-law partners regarding the **use, occupation, possession, and division of family homes and matrimonial interests** in or on reserve lands.

This Act grants **First Nations the legal authority to create their own family law**—specifically governing family homes and matrimonial rights—within the framework of constitutional principles and Indigenous self-governance. It is designed to be **culturally appropriate, community-driven, and respectful of Indigenous values**, while ensuring that decisions are made in the best interests of children and in alignment with First Nation traditions and governance.

---

## **Key Purposes and Principles**

1. **Addressing Legal Gaps**  
   - Provincial family laws (e.g., divorce, property division, spousal rights) are not applicable on First Nation reserves.  
   - The *Indian Act* does not cover spousal or common-law rights related to family homes or property division.  
   - This Act fills those gaps by enabling First Nations to develop **customized, culturally relevant family laws**.

2. **Recognition of Indigenous Self-Government**  
   - The Act affirms the **inherent right of self-government** as an Aboriginal right under Section 35 of the *Constitution Act, 1982*.  
   - It does **not define or prejudge** the scope of self-government or outcomes of negotiations.  
   - It supports the **exercise of First Nation law-making power** in matters of family law, consistent with constitutional principles.

3. **Best Interests of Children and Cultural Continuity**  
   - Decisions involving family homes must take into account:
     - The **best interests of children**, especially those who are First Nation members.
     - The **interest of children in maintaining a connection** to their First Nation community.
   - The decision-maker must be **informed by the First Nation** about cultural, social, and legal context.

4. **Cultural Relevance and Traditional Practices**  
   - Agreements between spouses or common-law partners may be reached through **traditional dispute resolution methods** (e.g., elders, councils, community mediation).  
   - This ensures that family law reflects Indigenous values and community practices.

## **Core Definitions**

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------|
| **First Nation** | A band as defined in subsection 2(1) of the *Indian Act*. |
| **First Nation Member** | A person whose name appears on the band list or who is entitled to be listed. |
| **Family Home** | A structure on reserve land where spouses or common-law partners habitually reside. Does not need to be affixed. Only the residential portion counts if used for other purposes. |
| **Interest or Right** | Includes: <br> - Rights to possession (with or without certificate), <br> - Permits under the *Indian Act*, <br> - Leases under sections 53/58, <br> - Rights under First Nation land laws, self-government agreements, or Kanesatake interim governance laws. |
| **Matrimonial Interests or Rights** | Interests acquired during or before a conjugal relationship (with specific contemplation or appreciation during the relationship), excluding gifts, legacies, or inheritances. |
| **Spouse** | Includes persons in voidable or void marriages. Also includes former spouses or common-law partners. |
| **Designated Judge** | A justice of the peace, court judge, or judge of a provincial court authorized by the lieutenant governor in council. |
| **Reserve** | Includes the **Kanesatake Mohawk interim land base** under the *Kanesatake Interim Land Base Governance Act*. |

> All terms are interpreted consistently with the *Indian Act* unless otherwise specified.

## **Key Provisions and Mechanisms**

### **1. First Nation Law-Making Power**

- **Power to Enact Laws**:  
  A First Nation with reserve land may enact **its own family laws** governing:
  - Use, occupation, and possession of family homes.
  - Division of the value of matrimonial interests or rights in structures or land on reserves.

- **Scope of Laws**:  
  Laws must include:
  - Procedures for **amending or repealing** them.
  - Provisions for **enforcing court orders** (despite *Indian Act* restrictions under subsection 89(1)).

- **Exclusions**:
  - Title to reserve land is **not affected** by this Act.
  - Reserve lands remain **set apart for the benefit of the First Nation** and are still classified as "lands reserved for Indians" under Class 24 of the *Constitution Act, 1867*.

---

### **2. Community Approval Process (Democratic Governance)**

To ensure legitimacy and community ownership, proposed laws must undergo a **community approval process**:

- **Eligibility to Vote**:  
  Any First Nation member aged 18 or over (regardless of residence) can vote.

- **Voter Outreach**:  
  The council must take **reasonable measures** (in accordance with First Nation practices) to locate and inform eligible voters.

- **Notice of Vote**:  
  A public notice must be published specifying the **date, time, and location** of the vote.

- **Approval Thresholds**:
  - A **majority of participants** must vote in favor of the law.
  - **At least 25% of eligible voters must participate** for the law to be approved.
  - The council may **increase the participation threshold** by resolution (e.g., 30%, 40%).

- **Notification Requirements**:
  - Upon approval, the council must:
    - Notify the **Minister of Indigenous Services** in writing.
    - Send a copy of the approved laws to:
      - The Minister,
      - The organization designated by the Minister (if any),
      - The Attorney General of any province where the reserve is located.

---

### **3. Legal Effect and Enforcement**

- **Coming into Force**:  
  Laws come into effect on the day of approval or a later date specified in the law.  
  Once in force, they are **binding in all legal proceedings** and take **judicial notice**.

- **Proof of Laws**:  
  A certified copy of the laws (by an officer of the First Nation) is **sufficient proof** of authenticity—no signature or official character is required.

- **Public Access**:  
  The council must maintain a **publicly accessible copy** of the laws (and amendments) at designated locations.

- **Amendments and Repeals**:
  - Amendments must be **notified to the Minister and Attorney General** immediately.
  - Repeals must be **notified in writing** to the same parties.

- **Minister’s Role**:
  - The Minister maintains a **public list** of First Nations whose laws are in force.
  - The list and amendments are published in a manner deemed appropriate.

---

### **4. Application of Provisions (Sections 13–52)**

These sections apply under specific conditions:

#### **(a) General Application**  
Sections 13 to 52 apply to a First Nation **only if**:
- The First Nation has **reserve land**.
- The First Nation laws under **Section 7** are **not yet in force**.

#### **(b) Framework Agreement Land Code**  
Sections 13–52 apply **only if**:
- On the day the Act comes into force, the **land code adopted under the Framework Agreement on First Nation Land Management Act** is **not in force**.
- The First Nation’s laws under Section 7 are **not in force**.

#### **(c) Self-Government Agreements**  
Sections 13–52 apply **only if**:
- The Minister, **by order**, makes a declaration that the sections apply to the First Nation.
- The First Nation’s laws under Section 7 are **not in force**.
- The declaration must specify that the sections apply **until the First Nation laws come into force**.

> The Minister maintains a **public list** of such declarations and their updates.

> Once a First Nation enacts its laws under Section 7 or a self-government agreement, it must **notify the Minister in writing** immediately.

---

### **5. Family Home Rights and Occupancy**

#### **Occupancy During Conjugal Relationship**
- Both spouses or common-law partners have the **right to occupy** the family home, regardless of whether they are First Nation members or Indians.

#### **After Death of a Spouse or Common-Law Partner**
- A surviving spouse or common-law partner **may occupy** the family home for **up to 180 days** after death, regardless of membership status.

---
