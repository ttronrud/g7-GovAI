### **Detailed Summary of the Veterans Insurance Act**

---

#### **1. Title and Citation**
The Act is titled the **Veterans Insurance Act** and may be cited as such. It establishes a framework for providing life insurance benefits to certain individuals who served in Canada’s armed forces during a defined wartime period, primarily for the benefit of their families and dependents.

---

#### **2. Key Definitions**

The Act provides clear definitions of key terms used throughout:

- **Amount of insurance**: The specific sum stated in the insurance contract.
- **Brother/sister**: Includes half-brothers and half-sisters.
- **Child**: Includes:
  - Legally adopted children,
  - Stepchildren designated by the insured as beneficiaries (by name or as "stepchild"),
  - Children acknowledged or maintained by the insured, or for whom the insured has been judicially ordered to provide support.
- **Common-law partner**: A person cohabiting with the insured in a conjugal relationship for at least one year.
- **Discharge from service**: Any termination of service (active or inactive).
- **Grandchild**: A child of a child (i.e., a grandchild of the insured).
- **Insured**: Any person with whom the Minister of Veterans Affairs enters into a contract of insurance under this Act.
- **Minister**: Initially the Minister of Veterans Affairs, but may be replaced by the Governor in Council.
- **Parent**: Includes biological, step, foster, or grandparental parents of the insured or their spouse/common-law partner.
- **Service**: 
  - Service in the naval, army, or air forces of Canada while receiving active service rates or Permanent Force rates, **or**
  - Active service in the naval, army, or air forces of His Majesty (UK) by a person domiciled in Canada at the start of the war.
- **Veteran**: Any male or female who served during the war and has been granted discharge from service.
- **War**: Defined as beginning in **September 1939** and ending on **September 30, 1947**.

> **Special Provision**: Service in Newfoundland forces or recruitment in Newfoundland is deemed equivalent to service in Canadian forces, and domicile in Newfoundland is deemed domicile in Canada.

---

#### **3. Scope of Insurance Coverage**

The Minister may enter into a life insurance contract with the following persons **on or before October 31, 1968**, without requiring medical examination or evidence of insurability:

1. **Veterans** who were engaged in service during the war and who were discharged.
2. **Widows/widowers** of veterans, **if no insurance was previously provided to the veteran**.
3. **Widows/widowers** of persons who died in service during the war.
4. **Members of the regular forces** who were engaged in service during the war and have not been released from service.
5. **Merchant seamen** who received or were eligible for a bonus under the *Merchant Seamen Special Bonus Order*.
6. **Seamen** who received or were eligible for a War Service Bonus under the *Merchant Seamen War Service Bonus Order, 1944*.
7. **Any person receiving a disability pension related to the war** under the *Pension Act*.

> **Limitation Clause**: If a person has life insurance under the *Returned Soldiers’ Insurance Act*, the total insurance coverage (under both Acts) cannot exceed **$10,000**.

---

#### **4. Payment Upon Death**

- The death of the insured triggers payment of the **full amount of insurance**, or **any lesser amount**, chosen by the insured.
- The remainder (if any) may be paid in one of the following forms:
  - An **annuity certain** for 5, 10, 15, or 20 years,
  - A **life annuity**, or
  - An **annuity guaranteed for 5–20 years**, with continuation thereafter as long as the beneficiary lives.

> **Flexibility**: The insured may **change the mode of payment** at any time after applying for insurance via a formal declaration.

> **Post-Death Change**: After the insured’s death, the beneficiary may **vary the mode of payment** with the **consent of the Minister**.

> **Lump-Sum Option**: At the beneficiary’s request, any annuity payments may be converted into a **lump sum**.

---

#### **5. Disability Provisions**

- If the insured becomes **totally and permanently disabled** before turning 60 and such disability is **not attributable to service** (i.e., does not qualify for a disability pension under the Pension Act), the **premiums during the disability period are waived**.
- Total disability is defined as continuous incapacity for gainful employment for **at least one year**.

---

#### **6. Beneficiary Designations**

##### **Primary Beneficiaries (if spouse, common-law partner, or child exists):**
- The spouse, common-law partner, or children of the insured are the primary beneficiaries.

##### **Future Beneficiaries (if no immediate dependents):**
- If the insured has no spouse, common-law partner, or children, the future spouse, future common-law partner, or future children may be designated as beneficiaries.

##### **Multiple Beneficiaries:**
- The insured may **apportion** the insurance money among beneficiaries and **re-apportion** it at any time.
- In the absence of apportionment, the proceeds are paid **equally** among surviving beneficiaries.

##### **Death of a Designated Beneficiary During the Insured’s Lifetime:**
- The insured may designate a new beneficiary to receive the deceased’s share.
- If no new designation is made, the share is **divided equally** among surviving designated beneficiaries.

##### **No Designated Beneficiary or All Die:**
- If no beneficiary is designated or all die before the insured, the proceeds go to:
  - The **spouse, common-law partner, and children** in equal shares.
  - If the insured survives them and there are **no contingent beneficiaries**, the proceeds go to the **estate or succession** of the insured.

---

#### **7. Contingent Beneficiaries**

- The insured may designate a **contingent beneficiary** (e.g., grandchild, parent, brother, sister, or other person prescribed by regulation) in case:
  - The insured has no spouse, common-law partner, or children at death.
- If no contingent beneficiary is named or all die during the insured’s lifetime, the proceeds go to the **estate or succession**.
- The insured may **re-apportion** the contingent share among beneficiaries.
- If a contingent beneficiary dies during the insured’s lifetime, the share may be reassigned or divided equally among surviving contingent beneficiaries.

> **Flexibility**: The insured may change beneficiary or contingent beneficiary designations at any time.

> **Contractual Flexibility**: Any changes (apportionment, beneficiary designation, mode of payment) not made in the initial application must comply with the **terms of the insurance contract**.

---

#### **8. Premiums and Payment Terms**

- Premiums may be paid:
  - For a period of **10, 15, or 20 years**, or
  - Until the anniversary of the policy nearest the **65th or 85th birthday** of the insured.
- Premium rates are detailed in **Schedule A** (not included in the text provided).

---

#### **9. Ministerial Discretion and Approval**

- The Minister may **refuse** to issue a contract if, in their opinion, there are sufficient grounds for refusal.
- However, the Minister must follow **Schedule B** (a set of guidelines) in making such decisions.
- The Minister may require:
  - A **medical examination**, or
  - Submission of additional information.

> **Note**: This discretion is not absolute and is subject to the rules in Schedule B.

---

#### **10. Unassignability of Benefits**

- The insurance proceeds are **unassignable** and **not subject to the claims of creditors** of the insured or the beneficiary.

---

#### **11. Death Before Contract Execution**

- If an application is made and the applicant dies **before** the contract is finalized, the contract is deemed **entered into** if:
  - The initial premium has been paid, and
  - The application would have been approved had the applicant not died.

---

#### **12. Death of a Beneficiary Before Full Payment**

- If a beneficiary or contingent beneficiary survives the insured but dies **before receiving all proceeds**, the **remaining unpaid amount** is paid to the **estate or succession** of the deceased beneficiary.

---

#### **13. Ministerial Payments (Provision for Unproven Death)**

- If the death of the insured **cannot be proven** (due to lack of documentation or location), but the Minister is satisfied after reasonable efforts that the person has died or is unlocatable, the Minister may apply the Act as if the insured had died on a date specified.
- Similarly, if a beneficiary or contingent beneficiary dies **unproven**, and the Minister is satisfied, the Act applies as if they had died within the insured’s lifetime.

> **Ministerial Payment Definition**: The amount paid due to the operation of this provision (i.e., when death is presumed).

> **Repayment Requirement**: The person receiving a Ministerial payment must **agree in writing** to repay the amount **if later determined** that the insured or beneficiary was alive.

> This provision ensures that payments are not made in error, with a safeguard for repayment.
