**Detailed Summary of the *Budget Implementation Act, 2019, No. 1***

---

### **Overview**

The *Budget Implementation Act, 2019, No. 1* is a federal legislative instrument enacted by Her Majesty in Right of Canada, with the advice and consent of the Senate and House of Commons. It serves as a vehicle to implement key provisions of the 2019 federal budget, particularly those related to fiscal policy, environmental regulation, infrastructure funding, and changes to bankruptcy and insolvency laws. The Act contains a series of amendments to existing statutes, with provisions that come into force on specific dates, often governed by orders in council or by reference to the date of royal assent.

---

## **Key Provisions and Detailed Summary**

---

### **1. Amendments to the Canada Pension Plan (CPP)**

- **Section 45 and 46** modify the Canada Pension Plan (CPP) to allow for certain adjustments to the CPP’s contribution and benefit structure.
- **Subsection 114(2) of the CPP Act does not apply** to these amendments — meaning that the usual rules that would restrict certain changes to CPP contributions or benefits are suspended.
- These amendments **come into force on a day to be fixed by order of the Governor in Council**.
- **Note**: Sections 45 and 46 came into force on **March 1, 2020**, as confirmed by Statutory Instrument SI/2020-21.

> **Purpose**: To enable adjustments to CPP contributions or benefits without triggering the usual regulatory barriers under subsection 114(2), allowing for more flexible fiscal responses to economic or demographic changes.

---

### **2. Funding Mechanisms from the Consolidated Revenue Fund**

This section establishes new mechanisms for the government to transfer funds from the **Consolidated Revenue Fund** (CRF) to provinces, municipalities, and specific institutions, primarily to support infrastructure and emergency services.

#### **A. General Requisition Powers for Specified Ministers**
- The **Minister of Finance** may designate specific ministers ("specified Ministers") who can request payments from the CRF.
- For each designated minister, the Minister of Finance may:
  - Specify the **amount** of funds that may be paid,
  - Define the **province** and **period** (specified period) for which the funds are available,
  - Include **terms and conditions** for disbursement.

> **Limitation on Total Funding**:
- The total amount available for a given province and period must not exceed a calculated formula based on:
  - The estimated **greenhouse gas (GHG) charges** levied under the *Greenhouse Gas Pollution Pricing Act* (GGPRA),
  - Less any **rebates, refunds, or remittances** under the GGPRA or other federal laws,
  - Minus the **amounts deemed as rebates** under subsection 122.8(6) of the *Income Tax Act*,
  - Plus adjustments for **past periods** (cumulative over time), including:
    - Past GHG charges,
    - Past rebates,
    - Past CRF payments,
    - Past distributions under the GGPRA’s *distribution provisions*.

> **Purpose**: To ensure that funding is not excessive and is directly tied to the GHG pricing system, providing a transparent and revenue-based cap on transfers.

#### **B. Infrastructure and Emergency Funding (Specific Allocations)**

1. **Municipal, Regional, and First Nations Infrastructure**  
   - The **Minister of Infrastructure and Communities** or **Minister of Indigenous Services** may request up to **$2.2 billion** from the CRF.
   - This funding is **in addition** to the amount provided under *section 161 of the Keeping Canada’s Economy and Jobs Growing Act*.
   - Must be approved by the **Treasury Board** and used for infrastructure projects in municipalities, regions, and First Nations.

2. **Green Municipal Fund (via Federation of Canadian Municipalities)**  
   - The **Minister of Natural Resources** may provide up to **$950 million** to the Federation of Canadian Municipalities.
   - Purpose: To fund the **Green Municipal Fund**, supporting municipalities in transitioning to low-carbon operations.
   - Requires a prior **agreement** with the Federation, which can be entered into before the Act comes into force.
   - Approval by the Treasury Board is required.

3. **Asset Management Fund**  
   - The **Minister of Infrastructure and Communities** may provide up to **$60 million** to the Federation of Canadian Municipalities.
   - Purpose: To support the **Asset Management Fund**, which helps municipalities manage aging infrastructure.
   - Requires a prior agreement with the Federation and Treasury Board approval.

4. **Shock Trauma Air Rescue Service (Emergency Helicopters)**  
   - The **Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness** may provide up to **$65 million** to the Shock Trauma Air Rescue Service.
   - Purpose: To acquire **new emergency ambulance helicopters** for rapid response in critical situations.
   - Requires a prior agreement with the service and Treasury Board approval.

> **Purpose**: To provide targeted, emergency-responsive, and environmentally aligned funding to local governments and critical services, with oversight and accountability through Treasury Board approval and specific agreements.

### **3. Amendments to Bankruptcy and Insolvency Laws**

- **Sections 133 to 140 and 142, and subsection 143(1)**:
  - Amend the *Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act* (BIA) and *Companies’ Creditors Arrangement Act* (CCAA).
  - These amendments apply **only to proceedings initiated on or after the date they come into force**.
  - The amendments are designed to modernize insolvency processes, improve creditor protections, and streamline procedures for debtors and stakeholders.

- **Effective Dates**:
  - Sections 133 to 140 come into force on **November 1, 2019** (SI/2019-90).
  - Subsections 143(2) and (3), and sections 144, 145(2) and (3), and 147 come into force on a **day to be fixed by order of the Governor in Council**, but **not before** the first day on which sections 142 and 143(1) are in force.

> **Purpose**: To modernize insolvency laws to better reflect current economic conditions, improve efficiency in debt restructuring, and enhance transparency and fairness in bankruptcy proceedings.

---

### **4. Amendments to the Food and Drugs Act (Clinical Trial Authorizations)**

- **Section 166** introduces a transitional provision for entities currently authorized to sell or import drugs or health products for **clinical trials**.
- **Key Provisions**:
  - A person who was authorized under:
    - Division 5 of Part C of the *Food and Drug Regulations* (drugs for clinical trials),
    - Division 3 of Part C (positron-emitting radiopharmaceuticals),
    - Part 4 of the *Natural Health Products Regulations* (natural health products for clinical trials),
    - Part 3 of the *Medical Devices Regulations* (Class II, III, or IV devices for investigational testing),
  - Is **deemed to hold an authorization** under **section 3.1 of the Food and Drugs Act** as of the day the section comes into force.

> **Purpose**: To ensure continuity of operations for entities involved in clinical research by preserving their legal status and authorizations during a regulatory transition, avoiding disruptions in research and development.

---

### **5. Transitional Provisions in the Hazardous Materials Information Review Act (HMIRA)**

- **Section 201** establishes a **commencement day** for changes to HMIRA.
- **Pending claims and appeals** that existed before the commencement day are:
  - Transferred from the **Chief Screening Officer** or a screening officer (under paragraph 12(1)(b)) to the **Minister of Health**.
  - Continued under the **current version of HMIRA** as it existed immediately before the commencement day.

- **Appeals** filed before the commencement day are continued under the updated provisions of HMIRA (sections 20 to 27 and 43–44) as they existed prior to the change.

- **Section 50 of HMIRA** continues to apply to members of appeal boards who are performing duties related to appeals continued under this Act.

> **Purpose**: To ensure legal continuity and fairness in the handling of hazardous materials information claims and appeals, preventing disruption due to regulatory changes.
