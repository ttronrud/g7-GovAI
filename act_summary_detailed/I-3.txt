**Detailed Summary of the *Importation of Intoxicating Liquors Act***  

---

### **1. Title and Citation**  
The Act is officially titled the *Importation of Intoxicating Liquors Act* and may be cited accordingly. This legislation governs the conditions under which intoxicating liquors may be imported into Canadian provinces.

---

### **2. Key Definitions (Interpretation Section)**  
The Act establishes precise definitions for key terms used throughout, drawing from other existing laws such as the *Excise Act, 2001* and the *Customs Tariff*. These definitions ensure consistency and clarity in enforcement:

| Term | Definition & Source |
|------|---------------------|
| **Beer** | As defined in Section 4 of the Excise Act; refers to alcoholic beverages with a standard alcohol content. |
| **Bulk** | As defined in Section 2 of the Excise Act, 2001; refers to unopened, large-volume liquors (e.g., in barrels or tanks). |
| **Chile / Costa Rica / Honduras / Panama / Ukraine / Israel / EU country / CUSMA country** | All defined in subsection 2(1) of the *Customs Tariff*, indicating specific foreign countries eligible for tariff or trade benefits under various agreements. |
| **CUKTCA beneficiary** | A beneficiary of the *Canada–United States–Mexico Agreement (CUSMA)* or related trade agreements (as defined in Customs Tariff). |
| **CUSMA country** | A country party to the *Canada–United States–Mexico Agreement*, as defined in Section 2 of the *Canada–United States–Mexico Agreement Implementation Act*. |
| **Denature** | As defined in Section 2 of the Excise Act, 2001; refers to rendering a liquor unfit for human consumption (e.g., by adding chemicals). |
| **Excise warehouse** | As defined in Section 2 of the Excise Act, 2001; a facility authorized to store excisable goods under government supervision. |
| **Intoxicating liquor** | Any liquor deemed intoxicating under provincial law and prohibited for sale or possession without a government permit. Includes beer, wine, and spirits. |
| **Licensed distiller** | A person holding a spirits licence under Section 14 of the *Excise Act, 2001*. |
| **Packaged** | As defined in Section 2 of the Excise Act, 2001; refers to liquors in sealed, finished containers (e.g., bottles). |
| **Province** | Any province *excluding Yukon, Northwest Territories, and Nunavut* where provincial laws regulate the sale of intoxicating liquor. |

> **Note:** The term *NAFTA country* has been **repealed** (2020), reflecting Canada’s transition from NAFTA to CUSMA.

---

### **3. Core Prohibition on Importation**  
**General Rule (Section 1):**  
Despite any other law (except the *Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act*), **no person may import or cause to be imported** intoxicating liquor into a province from outside Canada **unless**:

- The liquor is **purchased by or on behalf of**, and **consigned to**, either:
  - Her Majesty (the Crown), or  
  - The executive government of a province, or  
  - A provincial board, commission, officer, or agency authorized to sell intoxicating liquor.

This rule effectively restricts private individuals from importing liquor for personal use or resale.

---

### **4. Suspension of Paragraph (2)(f)**  
The operation of a specific provision (paragraph 2(f)) is **suspended during the period when paragraph (2)(c) is in force**, suggesting a temporary exception or adjustment in enforcement during transitional periods (likely related to trade agreements or policy changes).

---

### **5. Exceptions to the General Importation Ban**  
The Act provides several **important exceptions** where importation is permitted without violating the general prohibition. These are critical for industry operations and international trade:

#### **A. Transportation of Liquor Through Canada**  
- The transportation of intoxicating liquor **through a province to a territory or outside Canada** by:
  - The **producer**, or  
  - A **common carrier**  
  is allowed **if**:
  - The container remains **unopened or undamaged**, and  
  - No liquor is drunk or used during transport.

> This exception supports legitimate cross-border movement for distribution or delivery.

#### **B. Importation by Licensed Distillers**  
Licensed distillers (those with a spirits licence) may import liquor for **blending or flavouring** purposes, provided:

- The liquor is imported **solely for use in brewing or distilling**.
- It is **kept in compliance** with:
  - For **spirits or wine**: Excise Act, 2001 and provincial laws.
  - For **beer**: In a place or warehouse that meets all legal requirements.

> This enables distillers to source raw materials from abroad for production.

#### **C. Importation of Bulk Spirits from Specific Countries**  
Licensed distillers may import **bulk spirits** from the following countries **for packaging purposes**, if:

1. The spirits qualify under a **specific tariff** (as listed in the *Schedule to the Customs Tariff*), and  
2. They are stored in compliance with the *Excise Act, 2001* and provincial laws.

Specific countries include:
- **CUSMA countries** (e.g., U.S., Mexico)
- **Chile**
- **Costa Rica**
- **United States**
- Any country listed in **Column 1 of the Schedule**, provided the spirits qualify under the corresponding **Column 2 tariff**

> These provisions facilitate trade under tariff agreements and support the distilling industry’s access to raw materials.

#### **D. Transfer of Spirits Produced or Packaged in Canada**  
A licensed distiller may transfer spirits produced or packaged in Canada **if**:
- For **packaged spirits**: Stored in a licensed distiller’s **excise warehouse** and compliant with provincial laws.
- For **bulk spirits**: Stored in compliance with the *Excise Act, 2001* and provincial laws.

> This allows for internal movement and distribution of finished or intermediate products.

> **Note:** Several of these exceptions were **repealed** in 2002 and 2019 (e.g., 2002 c. 22, s. 395; 2019 c. 29, s. 186), indicating that some provisions have been removed or replaced due to policy or regulatory changes.

---

### **6. Burden of Proof**  
- The **burden of proving** a right to import or cause importation lies with the **accused person**.  
- This means the importer must demonstrate legal justification (e.g., a valid licence, consignment, or exception) to avoid liability.

---

### **7. Penalties for Violations**  
Violations of the Act constitute offences and carry **summary conviction penalties** based on the severity of the offence:

| Offence | Fine | Imprisonment |
|--------|------|-------------|
| **First offence** | Not exceeding $200 | Up to 3 months |
| **Second offence** | $200 to $1,000 | 3 to 6 months |
| **Each subsequent offence** | Not applicable (only imprisonment) | 6 to 12 months |

> Penalties are structured to deter repeated violations while allowing for first-time mistakes.

---

### **8. Jurisdiction for Prosecution**  
- A prosecution may be brought in:
  - The **city, town, or place** where the liquor is unlawfully imported, or  
  - The **residence of the accused**.
- **No prosecution** may be brought against a person **outside or not residing in a province** without the **written approval of the Attorney General** of that province.

> This limits jurisdictional reach and protects individuals from cross-provincial enforcement without oversight.

---

### **9. Search and Seizure Powers**  
If there is **reasonable suspicion** that:
- Intoxicating liquor is present in a premises, and  
- It has been dealt with in violation of the Act,  

Then an officer (recorder, two justices of the peace, provincial court judge, or Quebec Court of Quebec judge) may:
- Issue a **search warrant** for the premises (including government rail, vehicles, or ships), and  
- **Seize** the liquor and any containers (kegs, barrels, bottles, boxes, etc.) found.

> This provides law enforcement with tools to detect and prevent illicit importation.

---

### **10. Forfeiture and Destruction of Illicit Liquor**  
Upon conviction:
- The court may order the **forfeiture and destruction** of:
  - The illicit liquor involved in the offence, and  
  - All containers (kegs, bottles, packages) containing it.
- The order must be carried out by:
  - The constable or peace officer who executed the search warrant, or  
  - Another authorized person.

> This ensures that illegally imported liquor cannot be resold or reused.

---

### **11. Exceptions to the General Ban**  
The Act explicitly **permits** the importation of intoxicating liquor into Canada for the following **non-beverage purposes**:
- **Sacramental or medicinal purposes** (e.g., for religious or medical use)
- **Manufacturing or commercial purposes** (excluding the manufacture or use of intoxicating liquor as a beverage)
