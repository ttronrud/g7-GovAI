## üìö **Detailed Summary of the Trademarks Act**

---

### üîπ **Overview**
The *Trademarks Act* (commonly cited as the "Trademarks Act") is the primary federal statute in Canada that governs the registration, use, protection, and enforcement of **trademarks**, **trade names**, and **certification marks**. It establishes a legal framework to protect consumers from confusion, prevent deceptive practices, and ensure fair competition in the marketplace.

This Act defines what constitutes a trademark, sets out rules for use and registration, and includes provisions on **confusion**, **misrepresentation**, **prohibited uses**, and **geographical indications**.

## üîê Foundational Principles

### 1. **Deemed Use of a Trademark**
A person is deemed to have **adopted** a trademark when:
- They or their predecessor began using it in Canada, or
- Filed an application for registration in Canada (even without prior use).

A trademark is **deemed used in association with goods** if:
- It is marked on the goods or their packaging at the time of transfer of ownership or possession.
- It is otherwise associated with the goods in a way that gives notice to the recipient.

A trademark is **deemed used in association with services** if it is used or displayed during the performance or advertising of those services.

> ‚úÖ Exported goods with a Canadian-marked trademark are **deemed used in Canada**.

A trademark is **deemed made known in Canada** if:
- It is used in a country of the Union (other than Canada) in association with goods/services,
- And those goods/services are **distributed or advertised in Canada** via:
  - Printed publications circulated in Canada,
  - Radio broadcasts received in Canada by potential users/dealers.

> The mark must become **well known in Canada** through such distribution or advertising.

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Prohibited Acts (Section 12)

No person may:
1. **Make false or misleading statements** that discredit a competitor‚Äôs business, goods, or services.
2. **Direct public attention** to their goods/services in a way that causes or is likely to cause **confusion** with another business‚Äôs goods/services in Canada.
3. **Pass off** goods/services as belonging to someone else (e.g., falsely claiming they are from a known brand).
4. **Use false descriptions** in connection with goods/services that mislead the public about:
   - Character, quality, quantity, or composition,
   - Geographical origin,
   - Mode of manufacture, production, or performance.

> These rules protect against **passing off**, **deceptive advertising**, and **consumer deception**.

## üö´ Prohibited Marks (Section 9)

No person may adopt or use any mark that:
1. **Implies royal, governmental, or official patronage**:
   - Royal Arms, Crest, or Standard;
   - Arms or crest of a member of the Royal Family;
   - Arms of the Governor General;
   - Any word or symbol suggesting royal or governmental approval.

2. **Implies connection with official institutions**:
   - Arms, crests, or emblems adopted by Canada or any province/municipality (if publicly notified by the Registrar);
   - Red Cross emblem (on white ground), ‚ÄúRed Cross‚Äù or ‚ÄúGeneva Cross‚Äù;
   - Red Crescent emblem;
   - Red Crystal (third Protocol emblem);
   - Red Lion and Sun (used by Iran);
   - International civil defence sign (blue triangle on orange ground);
   - Any territorial or civic flag, national arms, or emblem of a country of the Union ‚Äî **if listed under Article 6ter of the Paris Convention or Annex 1C of the WTO Agreement** and publicly notified by the Registrar.

3. **Is scandalous, obscene, or immoral**.

4. **Falsely suggests a connection with a living individual**.

5. **Contains a portrait or signature** of any individual who died within the last 30 years.

6. **Uses ‚ÄúUnited Nations‚Äù or its official seal**.

7. **Uses official marks** adopted by:
   - Universities;
   - Public authorities in Canada;
   - Her Majesty‚Äôs Forces (as defined in the *National Defence Act*);
   - Organizations granted armorial bearings by the Governor General.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Exception**: These prohibitions **do not apply** if:
> - The mark is used **with the consent** of Her Majesty or the relevant authority;
> - The mark is used **only in relation to goods similar to those for which the official mark was adopted**;
> - The mark is **not likely to mislead** the public about a connection to the organization.

> ‚úÖ **Public Notice**: The Registrar may give public notice that a mark is **not restricted** under this section if:
> - The entity requesting the notice is **not a public authority**, or
> - The entity **no longer exists**.

> üìù The Registrar may, at the request of a person paying a prescribed fee, **give public notice** that a mark is exempt from the restriction.

## üö´ Prohibited Use of Common or Descriptive Signs (Section 10)

No person may:
- Adopt a sign or combination of signs that has become **commonly recognized** in Canada as indicating:
  - Kind, quality, quantity, destination, value, place of origin, or date of production.
- Use such signs in a way that is **likely to mislead**.
- Use signs **so similar** that they are likely to be mistaken for the original.

> This prevents the use of **descriptive or functional signs** as trademarks, which could mislead consumers.

> Example: "Organic" or "Handmade" may be protected if they have become associated with quality or origin.

> **Exception**: If a denomination is required under the *Plant Breeders‚Äô Rights Act* (e.g., "Rosa chinensis"), it **cannot be used as a trademark** for that plant or similar plants ‚Äî nor can a similar mark be used.
