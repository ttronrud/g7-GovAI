### **Detailed Summary of the *Canada–Costa Rica Free Trade Agreement Implementation Act***

This Act, officially titled the **Canada–Costa Rica Free Trade Agreement Implementation Act**, is a legislative instrument enacted by the Government of Canada to give legal effect to the **Canada–Costa Rica Free Trade Agreement (FTA)**, signed on **April 23, 2001**. It provides the domestic legal framework necessary for Canada to implement the terms of the FTA, ensuring compliance with its objectives, establishing administrative structures, and creating mechanisms for enforcement, dispute resolution, and cooperation.

---

## **I. Background and Objectives (Whereas Clauses)**

The Act begins with a series of "Whereas" clauses that outline the **shared motivations and principles** behind the FTA. These include:

1. **Strengthening Bilateral Relations**:  
   The governments aim to deepen friendship and cooperation between Canada and Costa Rica, recognizing their shared values and mutual interests.

2. **Promoting Global and Regional Trade**:  
   The FTA supports the expansion of world and regional trade, contributing to the broader goal of establishing the **Free Trade Area of the Americas (FTAA)**.

3. **Economic Development and Employment**:  
   The agreement seeks to create new job opportunities and improve working conditions and living standards in both countries.

4. **Economic Equity and Market Access**:  
   Acknowledging differences in economic size and development levels, the agreement aims to create a secure and expanded market for goods and services.

5. **Trade Facilitation and Predictability**:  
   Clear, transparent, and predictable trade rules are essential to reduce costs, eliminate trade distortions, and support business planning and investment.

6. **Compliance with International Agreements**:  
   The FTA builds upon Canada’s obligations under the **World Trade Organization (WTO)** and other multilateral and bilateral frameworks.

7. **Sustainable Development and Environmental Protection**:  
   Trade liberalization must not come at the expense of environmental conservation or public welfare. Both parties recognize the importance of sustainable development.

8. **Cultural Preservation and Labour Cooperation**:  
   The agreement respects the right of each country to maintain and develop its cultural policies and promotes cooperation in labour and environmental matters.

9. **National Identity and Competitiveness (Canada-specific)**:  
   Canada reaffirms its commitment to preserving national identity while enhancing economic competitiveness as a global trading nation.

---

## **II. Key Definitions**

The Act defines several key terms used throughout:

- **Agreement**: Refers to the Free Trade Agreement signed on April 23, 2001, including any rectifications made prior to Canada’s ratification.
- **Commission**: Refers to the **Free Trade Commission** established under **Article XIII.1** of the Agreement.
- **Federal Law**: Encompasses any Act of Parliament, regulation, order, or instrument made under parliamentary authority.
- **Minister**: The designated member of the Queen’s Privy Council for Canada responsible for a specific provision of the Act (as defined in section 10).
- **Territory**:  
  - For **Canada**: Includes all areas under customs jurisdiction, including maritime zones beyond territorial seas where Canada has rights to seabed and subsoil resources.  
  - For **Costa Rica**: Includes land, airspace, and maritime areas (including seabed and subsoil) where it exercises sovereign rights over natural resources.

---

## **III. Purpose of the Act**

The primary purpose of the Act is to **implement the Canada–Costa Rica FTA** by achieving the following objectives:

1. Establish a **free trade area** with reduced or eliminated tariffs and non-tariff barriers.
2. Promote **regional integration** as part of the broader FTAA initiative.
3. Create **economic development opportunities** for both nations.
4. Eliminate **barriers to trade in goods and services** and facilitate cross-border movement.
5. Increase **investment opportunities** in both countries.
6. Develop **fair competition** within the free trade area.
7. Establish a **cooperative framework** for bilateral, regional, and multilateral collaboration.
8. Provide **effective procedures** for implementation, administration, and dispute resolution.

---

## **IV. Legal and Procedural Framework**

### **1. Binding Nature of the Act**
- The Act is **binding on Her Majesty in right of Canada**, meaning it carries full legal force across Canada.

### **2. No Private Litigation Against the Agreement**
- **No individual or entity** can bring legal proceedings to enforce or determine rights or obligations arising **solely under the Agreement or Part 1 of this Act**.
- This provision is reinforced by the requirement that **any such action must have the prior consent of the Attorney General of Canada**.

> *Rationale*: This protects the integrity of the FTA by preventing private lawsuits that could undermine the agreement’s stability or create uncertainty in commercial relationships.

### **3. Exclusions and Limitations**
- **Water Resources Excluded**: Nothing in the Act applies to **natural surface or ground water** in any state (liquid, gaseous, or solid).
- **Parliamentary Authority Preserved**: Parliament retains full power to pass legislation to implement the Agreement or fulfill Canada’s obligations under it — no limitation is imposed.

---

## **V. Institutional and Administrative Provisions**

### **1. The Free Trade Commission (Commission)**
- Established under **Article XIII.1** of the Agreement.
- The **Minister for International Trade** serves as the **principal representative of Canada** on the Commission.

### **2. Funding and Support for the Commission**
- The **Government of Canada** is responsible for paying its **appropriate share** of:
  - Remuneration and expenses of **panelists, committee members, and working group participants**.
  - Costs of **assistants**.
  - General operating expenses of panels, committees, and working groups.

### **3. Appointment of Members and Representatives**
- The **Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal Cabinet) may appoint members to:
  - The **roster of experts** established under Article XIII.9 of the Agreement.
- The **Minister** may appoint representatives to:
  - Committees, subcommittees, or working groups referenced in **Annex XIII.2.2** or established under **Article XIII.1.5**.

### **4. Regulatory Powers (Article III.8 Implementation)**
- If needed to implement **Article III.8** (which likely relates to trade in services or specific regulatory measures), the Governor in Council may make **regulations**:
  - To require or prohibit certain activities.
  - To prescribe penalties for non-compliance.

> **Conditions for Regulation**:
- Regulations **do not come into force** in a province if that province already has laws or practices in conformity with the relevant part of Article III.8.
- The **Minister must consult** with the provincial government before making a regulation affecting that province.
- A regulation may be **suspended or revoked** by order of the Governor in Council at any time.
- Once suspended, the regulation **ceases to apply** in a province on a date specified in the order.
- Such regulations are **binding on Her Majesty in right of that province**.

### **5. Suspension of Concessions (Article XIII.18)**
- In cases where Canada needs to suspend concessions or obligations under the Agreement (e.g., due to public health, safety, or environmental concerns), the Governor in Council may issue an order to:
  - Suspend rights or privileges granted to Costa Rica or its entities.
  - Modify or suspend the application of federal laws related to Costa Rica.
  - Extend the application of federal laws to Costa Rica.
  - Take other measures deemed necessary.

> **Conditions for Suspension**:
- The order must be **supported by evidence** that the Government of Costa Rica has taken **satisfactory steps** to implement the Agreement.
- Unless revoked, such orders remain in effect for the **period specified** in the order.

---

## **VI. Implementation Timeline and Enforcement**

- The provisions of the Act (or any amendments enacted by it) come into force on a date or dates **to be fixed by order of the Governor in Council**.
- This allows for phased implementation, ensuring readiness in administrative, legal, and economic systems.

> **Note**: The Act came into force on **November 1, 2002** (as noted in the SI/2002-146 statutory instrument).

---

## **VII. Key Features and Implications**

| Feature | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| **Trade Liberalization** | Eliminates tariffs and non-tariff barriers between Canada and Costa Rica. |
| **Investment Facilitation** | Encourages cross-border investment through predictable, transparent rules. |
| **Regulatory Harmonization** | Requires alignment on standards and procedures to reduce trade friction. |
| **Dispute Resolution Mechanism** | Establishes formal processes for resolving disagreements between the two countries. |
| **Environmental and Cultural Safeguards** | Ensures that economic growth does not compromise ecological or cultural integrity. |
| **Provincial Consultation** | Respects provincial autonomy by requiring consultation before regulating trade-related matters. |
| **Flexibility for Public Welfare** | Allows Canada to suspend obligations if public interest demands it (e.g., health, safety). |

---

## **VIII. Limitations and Exceptions**

- **No application to water resources** — groundwater, surface water, or geothermal water are explicitly excluded.
- **No restriction on Parliament’s legislative power** — Canada remains free to pass laws to meet FTA obligations as needed.
- **No private enforcement rights** — Private parties cannot sue over obligations under the Agreement.
