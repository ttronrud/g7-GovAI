### **Overview**

The *Free Trade and Labour Mobility in Canada Act* is a comprehensive legislative initiative introduced by the Government of Canada to **remove federal barriers** to the **interprovincial movement of goods, services, and workers**, thereby promoting **free trade and labour mobility** across Canada. The Act supports the broader goal of creating a **national system of mutual recognition**â€”where a good, service, or worker that meets the standards of one Canadian jurisdiction is recognized as meeting those standards in all other jurisdictions.

## **Key Provisions and Explanations**

### **1. Purpose of the Act**

The primary objectives of the Act are:

- To **remove federal regulatory barriers** to the movement of:
  - Goods and services between provinces and territories.
  - Workers across Canada.
- To **enhance labour mobility** by enabling workers to move and work in any province or territory without unnecessary restrictions.
- To **improve access to Canadian goods and services** for businesses and consumers by streamlining interprovincial trade.
- To **strengthen the Canadian economy** through greater efficiency, competition, and consumer choiceâ€”while upholding essential public protections.

> *This Act is not about eliminating all regulation; rather, it promotes consistency and mutual recognition of standards among jurisdictions.*

### **2. Definitions (Key Terms)**

The Act establishes clear definitions to guide interpretation:

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------|
| **Federal Regulatory Body** | A body empowered by federal law to regulate a good/service or issue authorizations for an occupation. May include designated bodies under regulations. |
| **Federal Requirement** | Any requirement established under federal legislation or by a federal regulatory body. |
| **Provincial or Territorial Regulatory Body** | A body empowered by provincial/territorial legislation or designated by regulation to regulate goods/services or issue occupational authorizations. |
| **Provincial or Territorial Requirement** | A requirement established under provincial/territorial law or by a provincial/territorial regulatory body. |

These definitions clarify the scope of federal vs. provincial jurisdiction and form the foundation for mutual recognition.

### **4. Mutual Recognition Principle**

#### **Section 8 â€“ Goods and Services**

> *Subject to regulations, a good or service produced, used, or distributed in accordance with a provincial or territorial requirement is considered to meet any comparable federal requirement.*

##### Conditions for "Comparability":
A provincial or territorial requirement is deemed **comparable** to a federal requirement if:
1. It applies to the **same aspect or element** of the good or service.
2. It is intended to achieve a **similar objective** (e.g., safety, quality, environmental protection).
3. Any conditions set out in regulations are met.

##### Process:
- The **federal regulatory body** responsible for enforcing the federal requirement has the authority to **determine whether a provincial/territorial requirement is comparable**.
- If comparable, the good/service is treated as meeting the federal standard.

> âœ… *Example:* A food product certified as safe and nutritious under a provincial health standard may be recognized as meeting federal food safety requirements, even if the federal standard is more stringent or less detailed.

#### **Section 9 â€“ Services**

> *A service provided in accordance with a provincial or territorial requirement is considered to meet any comparable federal requirement, provided the provincial/territorial requirement continues to apply to the service provider.*

##### Conditions for Comparability:
Same as for goods:
- Same aspect/element of the service.
- Similar objective.
- Compliance with regulatory conditions.

> âœ… *Example:* A licensed accountant in Ontario providing services in Quebec under Ontarioâ€™s licensing rules may be recognized as meeting federal standards, as long as the Ontario requirement remains in force.

### **5. Labour Mobility â€“ Section 10**

> *The federal regulatory body must recognize an authorization to practise an occupation issued by a provincial or territorial body as comparable to a federal authorization, and must issue a federal authorization upon application.*

##### Key Points:
- A person who holds a valid **provincial or territorial license** to practice a profession (e.g., doctor, teacher, engineer) is **automatically recognized** as meeting the federal standard.
- The federal body **must issue a federal authorization** upon request.
- This promotes **freedom of movement** for skilled workers across Canada.

## **Key Principles Underlying the Act**

| Principle | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Mutual Recognition** | Standards set by one jurisdiction are recognized in others if they are equivalent in scope and intent. |

## **Limitations and Exceptions**

- **Not universal**: Applies only to goods/services/occupations where there is a **federal requirement** and a **provincial/territorial requirement**.
- **No automatic recognition**: Recognition is conditional on comparability in terms of **aspect, objective, and conditions**.
- **Exceptions allowed**: Regulations can create exceptions where public safety or other interests require it.
- **Labour mobility not full freedom**: Workers still need to meet basic eligibility criteria (e.g., education, experience), but the process is streamlined.
