**Detailed Summary of the *Avoiding Complicity in Mistreatment by Foreign Entities Act***

---

### **Overview**

The *Avoiding Complicity in Mistreatment by Foreign Entities Act* is a Canadian legislative measure designed to **prevent the Canadian government from becoming complicit in the torture or other cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment (CIDT) of individuals by foreign entities**. The Act establishes a formal, transparent framework for federal departments, agencies, and officials to **avoid actionsâ€”such as disclosing, requesting, or using informationâ€”that could lead to or result in mistreatment of individuals abroad**.

It operates within the broader context of Canadaâ€™s commitment to **national security, the rule of law, human rights, and international obligations**, particularly those related to the prohibition of torture.

### **Key Background and Policy Rationale**

1. **National Security and Public Safety**  
   The Government of Canada has a fundamental duty to protect national security and the safety of Canadians. This responsibility requires the government to gather, share, and use intelligence and informationâ€”sometimes involving foreign entities.

2. **Need for Information Disclosure and Use**  
   While such information is necessary for national security, it must be handled responsibly to avoid contributing to human rights abuses.

3. **Rule of Law and Charter of Rights**  
   All government actions must comply with the *Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms*, ensuring that individual rights and freedoms are protectedâ€”even in sensitive security contexts.

4. **International Obligations**  
   Canada is a party to key international treaties that explicitly prohibit torture and cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment, including:
   - The **Geneva Conventions**
   - The **International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)**
   - The **Convention Against Torture and Other Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment (CAT)**

5. **Domestic Legal Prohibitions**  
   The *Criminal Code* criminalizes:
   - Torture
   - Aiding, abetting, counselling, conspiring to, attempting, or being an accessory after the fact to torture

6. **Moral and Value-Based Stance**  
   The Government of Canada explicitly opposes torture and CIDT, viewing such acts as contrary to Canadian values and principles of justice and human dignity.

### **Main Provisions and Key Features**

#### **1. Definitions**

The Act establishes clear definitions of key terms:

- **Appropriate Minister**:  
  The minister responsible for a specific department, division, or corporation. For example:
  - For departments (e.g., Department of Foreign Affairs), the Minister presiding over that department.
  - For Crown corporations, the minister designated by the Governor in Council.
  - For the Canadian Forces, the Minister of National Defence.

- **Deputy Head**:  
  The senior official responsible for day-to-day operations in a federal agency. Examples include:
  - Deputy Minister of Foreign Affairs
  - Chief of the Defence Staff
  - Director of CSIS
  - Commissioner of RCMP
  - President of CBSA
  - Chief of CSE  
  *Note: For other entities, the Governor in Council may designate a deputy head.*

- **Mistreatment**:  
  Defined strictly as **torture or other cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment or punishment**, as defined under the **CAT (Convention Against Torture)**.

> âš ï¸ *This definition is legally binding and aligns with international human rights standards.*

#### **2. The Core Mechanism: Directions from the Governor in Council**

The central mechanism of the Act is the power of the **Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal Cabinet) to issue **written directions** to deputy heads of key federal agencies.

These directions are issued **on the recommendation of the appropriate Minister** and apply to the following activities:

- **Disclosure of information** to any foreign entity that would result in a **substantial risk of mistreatment** of an individual.
- **Making requests** to any foreign entity for information that would result in a **substantial risk of mistreatment**.
- **Use of information** that was obtained through the **mistreatment of an individual** by a foreign entity.

> ðŸ” *The key trigger is a "substantial risk" of mistreatment â€” not mere possibility â€” which ensures that actions are only taken when there is a clear and serious threat.*
