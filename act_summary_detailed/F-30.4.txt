**Detailed Summary of the *Forgiveness of Debts (sub-Saharan Africa) Act***

---

### **Overview of the Act**

The *Forgiveness of Debts (sub-Saharan Africa) Act* is a Canadian legislative measure enacted by Her Majesty, with the advice and consent of the Senate and House of Commons of Canada. The Act formally forgives specific official development assistance (ODA) debts owed by governments in sub-Saharan Africa to the Government of Canada under a series of historical loan agreements.

This Act is notable for being part of Canada’s broader foreign policy and development strategy during the late 20th century, reflecting a commitment to debt relief for developing nations—particularly those in sub-Saharan Africa—to promote economic stability, reduce poverty, and foster sustainable development.

---

### **Key Provisions**

#### **1. Citation and Purpose**
- The Act is officially cited as the **Forgiveness of Debts (sub-Saharan Africa) Act**.
- Its primary purpose is to **forgive outstanding debts** owed by certain sub-Saharan African governments to Canada under specific loan agreements.

#### **2. Scope of Debt Forgiveness**
- The Act forgives **official development assistance (ODA) debts** owed by the governments of the following countries:
  - Cameroon  
  - Congo (People’s Republic of)  
  - Ivory Coast (Republic of)  
  - Gabon (Gabonese Republic)  
  - Ghana (Republic of)  
  - Kenya (Republic of)  
  - Madagascar (Democratic Republic of)  
  - Nigeria (Federal Republic of)  
  - Senegal (Republic of)  
  - Swaziland (Kingdom of)  
  - Zaire (Republic of)  
  - Zambia (Republic of)  
  - Zimbabwe (Republic of)

- The forgiveness applies only to **loan agreements** entered into between the respective governments and the Government of Canada.

#### **3. Specific Loan Agreements Covered**
The Act identifies and lists **127 individual loan agreements** across the listed countries. These agreements span from **1965 to 1985**, with the earliest dating back to 1965 and the most recent to 1985.

#### **4. Accounting and Budgetary Treatment**
- The amount of debt forgiven is **written off as a budgetary expenditure**.
- This means that the financial liability is **removed from Canada’s official accounts**, and the forgiveness is treated as a government expense in budgetary terms.
- The debt is **deleted from the accounts of Canada**, ensuring that no further accounting entries are required for these obligations.

#### **5. Effective Date**
- The Act does **not take effect immediately**.
- It comes into force on a **day to be fixed by order of the Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal Cabinet).
- This provision allows for administrative and financial review before implementation.

---

### **Historical Context and Significance**

- **Timing**: The Act was passed in **1990**, with the first reference to its coming into force noted as **March 25, 1990** (under SI/90-49).
- **Background**: This debt forgiveness occurred during a period of growing international awareness about the burden of external debt on developing nations. Many African countries had accumulated significant debt due to colonial-era infrastructure projects, post-independence economic challenges, and reliance on foreign aid.
- **Canada’s Role**: Canada had provided development financing to these countries through various loan programs, often under the framework of the **Official Development Assistance (ODA)** program.
- **Debt Relief Movement**: This Act aligns with broader global efforts in the 1980s and 1990s to forgive or restructure debt for poor and developing nations, including initiatives by the **International Monetary Fund (IMF)** and the **World Bank**.

---

### **Impact and Implications**

1. **Economic Relief for Borrowing Countries**:
   - The forgiveness provides immediate financial relief to governments in sub-Saharan Africa, allowing them to redirect funds toward essential services such as health, education, and infrastructure.

2. **Political and Diplomatic Benefits**:
   - Canada strengthens its diplomatic ties with African nations, reinforcing its role as a development partner.
   - It signals a commitment to **equitable international relations** and **development solidarity**.

3. **Domestic Budgetary Adjustment**:
   - Canada formally recognizes the forgiveness in its budgetary records, reflecting a policy of responsible foreign aid management.

4. **Precedent for Future Debt Relief**:
   - This Act serves as a precedent for future debt relief initiatives in Canada, particularly in relation to African nations.

   - The forgiveness applies only to **specific loan agreements** and not to all outstanding debts.
