**Detailed Summary of the Jules and Paul-Émile Léger Foundation Act**

---

### **Overview**

The *Jules and Paul-Émile Léger Foundation Act* is a legislative instrument passed by the Parliament of Canada that establishes a non-profit charitable foundation in honour of two prominent Canadian figures:  
- **Jules Léger**, who served as Governor General of Canada from 1974 to 1979,  
- **Paul-Émile Léger**, who served as Archbishop of Montreal (1950–1967) and Cardinal of the Roman Catholic Church since 1953.

This Act formally creates the **Jules and Paul-Émile Léger Foundation (the “Foundation”)**, recognizing their lifelong dedication to humanitarian causes, social welfare, and the alleviation of human suffering. The foundation is structured as a charitable, non-profit corporation with broad powers to promote education, religion, philanthropy, and benevolent activities both within Canada and internationally.

---

### **Key Provisions and Detailed Summary**

#### **1. Purpose and Objectives of the Foundation**

The Foundation’s primary mission is to advance humanitarian and charitable goals through the following specific objectives:

- **Relief of suffering**: To alleviate sickness, hunger, and poverty, and to provide assistance to vulnerable populations such as:
  - Orphans
  - The aged
  - The disabled (handicapped)
  - The ill-treated
  - The lepers (historically marginalized groups)

- **Support of institutions**: To fund and establish:
  - Hospitals
  - Rehabilitation centres
  - Refuges and shelters
  - Other charitable institutions aligned with its mission

- **Collaboration and support**: To support individuals and organizations that share similar humanitarian goals.

- **Fundraising and financial operations**:
  - To organize, promote, and participate in fundraising campaigns (both for itself and for other like-minded causes).
  - To solicit and receive donations, legacies, and grants.

- **Broad mandate**: To promote, coordinate, and support, both morally and financially, a wide range of:
  - Educational initiatives
  - Religious activities
  - Philanthropic efforts
  - Benevolent causes

> **Important Note**: The Foundation operates without regard to the religious beliefs of those it serves — ensuring inclusivity and secular access to its services.

---

#### **2. Financial and Operational Authority**

The Foundation is granted significant autonomy in managing its finances and operations:

- **Borrowing and debt instruments**:
  - May borrow money on its own credit.
  - May issue, reissue, sell, or pledge debt obligations (e.g., bonds).
  - May create security interests or hypothecate its property to secure obligations.

- **Investment flexibility**:
  - May invest its funds in any manner deemed suitable by its board, without restriction (subject to prudent management standards).

- **Establishment of charitable undertakings**:
  - May acquire, create, and manage any charitable or benevolent project necessary to fulfill its mission.

- **Incidental activities**:
  - May engage in any activity that is incidental or beneficial to achieving its objectives.

> **Capacity of a Natural Person**:
> The Foundation is granted the legal capacity of a natural person, meaning it can enter into contracts, own property, and be sued — enabling it to operate with full legal standing.

---

#### **3. Jurisdiction and Operations**

- **Geographic Reach**:
  - May carry out its activities throughout Canada.
  - May operate in any jurisdiction outside Canada, provided such operations are permitted under the laws of that jurisdiction.

- **Head Office**:
  - Initially located in **Montreal**, but may be relocated to any other place in Canada.
  - Any change in head office location must be **publicly announced in the Canada Gazette**.

---

#### **4. Founding Members and Initial Directors**

- The **first members** of the Foundation are:
  - Robert Comtois, Victor Goldbloom, Fernand Legault, Robert Morin, Jean Roquet, Marcel Vincent (all from Montreal)
  - John W. Pickersgill, Guy Roberge, Maxwell Yalden (all from Ottawa)

- These individuals are named as the **first directors** of the Foundation and serve until **a general meeting of members is held within three months** of the Act coming into force.

- These initial directors are **eligible for re-election** at the subsequent general meeting.

---

#### **5. Board of Directors**

- The board consists of **between five and twenty members**, elected by the members of the Foundation at a general meeting.

- **Term of office**:
  - Directors serve at the discretion of the members.
  - A director whose term has expired is eligible for **re-election**.

- **Leadership Officers**:
  - The **President** and other officers (e.g., vice-presidents, secretaries) are elected by the directors from among themselves as soon as practicable after the election of directors.

---

#### **6. Honorary Leadership**

- **Paul-Émile Léger and Gabrielle Léger** are named as **lifetime honorary co-presidents** of the Foundation and of all subsidiary corporations incorporated under this Act.

> This recognition underscores their personal legacy and enduring influence on Canadian society and charitable work.

---

#### **7. By-Laws and Governance**

- The board may adopt **by-laws** to govern internal operations, including:
  - Election procedures for directors
  - Appointment, compensation, and duties of officers and employees
  - Creation and functions of executive and special committees
  - Member classes, qualifications, dues, and rights
  - Governance of subsidiary corporations
  - Regulation of powers and director elections in affiliated entities

- **Approval Requirement**:
  - All by-laws must be **sanctioned by a general meeting of members** with **at least two-thirds of those present and entitled to vote**.

---

#### **8. Incorporation of Subsidiary Corporations**

- The Foundation may petition the **Minister of Consumer and Corporate Affairs** to incorporate **subsidiary corporations** for specific purposes (e.g., running a hospital or rehabilitation center).

- **Petition Requirements**:
  - Must include:
    - Proposed name
    - Objects and purposes of the subsidiary
    - Powers granted to the Foundation over the subsidiary
    - Location of head office
  - Must be accompanied by a copy of any relevant Foundation by-law governing the subsidiary’s operations.

- **Supplementary Letters Patent**:
  - A subsidiary may petition for **amendments or modifications** to its powers or objects, **with approval from the Foundation**.
  - However, **no amendment may allow the subsidiary to pursue an activity not authorized by the Foundation**.

- **Public Notification**:
  - Both initial and amended incorporations are published in the **Canada Gazette**.

- **Applicability of Laws**:
  - Sections 3 and 21 of this Act, and **Part 19 of the Canada Not-for-Profit Corporations Act**, apply to subsidiary corporations, with necessary adaptations based on context.

---

#### **9. Financial Management and Profits**

- **Profit Distribution**:
  - All profits, income, or appreciation in property value must be **reinvested** into the Foundation’s activities.
  - **No profit or property may be distributed** to any member of the Foundation — ensuring the organization remains strictly non-profit.

> This provision reinforces the Foundation’s commitment to transparency, accountability, and public service.

---

#### **10. Dissolution and Property Transfer**

- The Foundation or any subsidiary may be dissolved upon **petition** by the Foundation, following procedures specified in the petition.

- **Dissolution of a Subsidiary**:
  - After paying all debts and liabilities, any remaining assets are **transferred to the parent Foundation**.

- **Dissolution of the Foundation**:
  - After settling all obligations, any remaining assets are **transferred to the Procurer of the Roman Catholic Diocese of Montreal**.
  - These assets are to be used **exclusively for philanthropic purposes** — a tribute to Paul-Émile Léger’s ecclesiastical leadership and legacy.

- **Public Notice**:
  - All dissolution decisions are **published in the Canada Gazette**.

---

### **Historical and Symbolic Significance**

- The Act is not merely a legal instrument but a **symbolic tribute** to the shared values of compassion, service, and public duty embodied by Jules and Paul-Émile Léger.
- Their combined roles — one as a national representative (Governor General), the other as a spiritual leader (Cardinal) — reflect a unique blend of civic and moral leadership.
- The Foundation’s creation honors their **lifelong commitment to humanitarianism**, especially in addressing poverty, illness, and social marginalization.

---

### **Key Legal and Ethical Principles Embedded in the Act**

| Principle | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Inclusivity** | No preference for religious belief; services are open to all. |
| **Transparency** | Public notice of changes (e.g., office moves, dissolutions) in the Canada Gazette. |
| **Accountability** | Profits reinvested; no personal distribution to members. |
| **Autonomy** | Broad powers to operate, invest, borrow, and create subsidiaries. |
| **Sustainability** | Clear procedures for governance, dissolution, and succession. |

---

### **Conclusion**

The *Jules and Paul-Émile Léger Foundation Act* establishes a powerful, enduring, and values-driven charitable entity designed to promote social good across Canada and beyond. It reflects a deep respect for humanitarian principles, institutional integrity, and the legacy of two influential public servants.
