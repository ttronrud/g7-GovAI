### **Detailed Summary of the Spirit Drinks Trade Act**

---

#### **Overview**

The *Spirit Drinks Trade Act* is a Canadian federal law enacted to **protect international trade commitments** related to the **use of specific names for spirit drinks**, ensuring that such names are used only in accordance with agreed-upon standards and geographical or national production requirements. The Act balances the need to **uphold international trade agreements** with the **protection of Canadian consumers and the integrity of spirit drink branding**.

It was passed by the Parliament of Canada with the aim of complying with international obligations, particularly those under the **Agreement between Canada and the European Community on trade in wines and spirit drinks (2003)**, the **North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA)**, and Canada’s **1985 Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting commitments** regarding economic development in the Caribbean.

The Act comes into force on **June 1, 2006**.

---

## **Key Provisions and Structure**

---

### **1. Purpose and Background (Whereas Clauses)**

- Canada has international commitments (under trade agreements) to **protect the names of spirit drinks** from being misused or falsely applied to products not produced in the country or region associated with that name.
- These commitments are found in:
  - The **Canada–European Community Agreement on Trade in Wines and Spirit Drinks (2003)**,
  - **Side letters to NAFTA (1992)**,
  - Canada’s **1985 Commonwealth commitment** to support Caribbean economies through trade development.

These obligations require Canada to **prevent misleading or unauthorized use of protected names** for spirit drinks, especially those tied to specific regions or countries.

---

### **2. Definitions**

- **Minister**: Refers to the **Minister of Agriculture and Agri-Food**, responsible for administering the Act.
- **Sell**: Includes **offering for sale**, **exposing for sale**, and **having in possession for sale** — broadens the scope to cover any intent to market a product.

---

### **3. Prohibition on Unauthorized Use of Spirit Drink Names**

#### **Section 1–5 of the Schedule (e.g., Grappa, Ouzo, Cognac, etc.)**
- **No person may use the name of a spirit drink listed in Sections 1 to 5 of the Schedule** to sell a product as that spirit drink **unless it complies with the specific conditions** set out in those sections.
- This means:
  - A product cannot be labeled as "Grappa" or "Cognac" unless it meets the **geographical, production, and legal requirements** defined in the Schedule.

#### **Exceptions to the Prohibition**
- **Blending or modification in accordance with Canadian laws** is permitted. For example, a Canadian-made spirit can be blended with other spirits under Canadian regulations and still legally use a name if the blending is lawful.
- **Registered trademarks applied for before January 1, 1996** are protected and may continue to be used even if they conflict with the spirit drink name rules — this preserves existing brand rights.

#### **Section 6–14 of the Schedule (e.g., Scotch, Irish, Bourbon, Tequila, etc.)**
- Similar rules apply to spirit drinks listed in Sections 6 to 14 — **only use is permitted if the product meets strict origin and production criteria**.
- Again, **blending or modification under Canadian law** is allowed.

> **Key Principle**: The name of a spirit drink is not a free label. It must be tied to **authentic origin, production method, and legal compliance**.

---

### **4. Flexibility via Governor in Council Orders**

- The **Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal government, on ministerial advice) may issue **orders amending the Schedule** to implement **new or updated international trade obligations**.
- This allows the Act to remain **flexible and responsive** to changes in international trade agreements or global standards.

---

### **5. Enforcement: Designation of Inspectors and Analysts**

#### **Inspectors**
- The Minister may designate **persons or classes of persons** as inspectors to enforce the Act.
- Every inspector receives a **certificate** issued by the Minister, which must be shown upon entry into a place under inspection.
- Inspectors have broad powers to:
  - Enter any place (excluding **dwelling-houses**) where a spirit drink may be **manufactured, prepared, preserved, packaged, or stored**.
  - **Examine** the product and take **samples**.
  - **Open and examine** containers or packages.
  - **Review records** (books, documents) related to production or storage.
  - **Seize and detain** any article suspected of violating the Act.

#### **Duties of Persons in a Place**
- The owner or person in charge of a place must **provide reasonable assistance** and **furnish information** to an inspector.
- **Obstruction or false statements** are prohibited and constitute offences.

#### **Seizure and Storage**
- Seized articles may be:
  - Kept at the original location, or
  - Removed to another location at the inspector’s direction.
- An inspector may **release** an article if they are satisfied all provisions of the Act have been followed.
- If the **owner consents to destruction**, the article is **immediately forfeited** to Her Majesty and may be disposed of as directed by the Minister.

#### **Forfeiture by Court**
- If a person is convicted of a violation:
  - The **court may order forfeiture** of the offending product and any similar items found with it.
  - Forfeited items go to Her Majesty and may be disposed of as the Minister directs.
- A **superior court judge** may also order forfeiture **on application by an inspector**, after making an inquiry, if the product is found to violate the Act.

---

### **6. Role of Analysts**

- The Minister may designate **analysts** to conduct scientific or technical analysis of seized products or samples.
- An inspector may **submit a sample** to an analyst for testing.
- The analyst issues a **certificate or report** detailing the results.
- This certificate is **admissible in court** without needing to prove the signature or official status of the analyst — a significant procedural simplification.
- However, the **party against whom the certificate is presented may request cross-examination** of the analyst.
- **Notice is required** before a certificate is introduced in evidence — must be given reasonably in advance and a copy provided.

---

### **7. Regulatory Powers**

- The **Governor in Council** may make **regulations** (under the Minister’s recommendation) to:
  - Define powers and duties of inspectors and analysts,
  - Specify procedures for sampling,
  - Regulate seizure, detention, forfeiture, and disposal of articles.

> This gives the government flexibility to develop detailed operational rules without amending the main Act.

---

### **8. Offences and Penalties**

#### **Summary Conviction (e.g., minor or first-time offences)**
- Fine not exceeding **$50,000**, or
- Imprisonment for up to **six months**, or
- Both.

#### **Indictment (e.g., serious or repeat offences)**
- Fine not exceeding **$250,000**, or
- Imprisonment for up to **three years**, or
- Both.

#### **Defence of Due Diligence**
- A person is **not guilty** of an offence under the Act (except for section 7 offences) **if they can prove they exercised all due diligence** to prevent the violation.

> This encourages compliance and protects individuals who act reasonably and responsibly.

---

### **9. Specific Provisions on Spirit Drink Names (Schedule)**

The Act allows the use of certain names **only if the product meets strict origin and production criteria**. Below are the key protected names and their conditions:

| Spirit Drink Name | Conditions for Legal Use |
|------------------|---------------------------|
| **Grappa** | Must be produced **exclusively in Italy** |
| **Grappa di Ticino** | Must be produced in the **Ticino region of Switzerland** |
| **Jägertee, Jagertee, Jagatee** | Must be produced **exclusively in Austria** |
| **Korn or Kornbrand** | Must be produced **exclusively in Germany or Austria** |
| **Ouzo or Oύζo** | Must be produced **exclusively in Greece** |
| **Pacharán** | Must be produced **exclusively in Spain** |
| **Scotch Whisky** | Must be distilled in **Scotland** as Scotch whisky, in accordance with UK laws, for **domestic consumption in Canada** |
| **Irish Whisky** | Must be distilled in **Northern Ireland or Republic of Ireland** as Irish whisky, under applicable Irish laws, for domestic consumption |
| **Armagnac Brandy** | Must be manufactured in the **Armagnac district of France**, under French law, for consumption in France |
| **Cognac Brandy** | Must be manufactured in the **Cognac district of France**, under French law, for consumption in France |
| **Bourbon Whiskey** | Must be manufactured in the **United States**, under U.S. laws and regulations, for domestic consumption |
| **Tennessee Whiskey** | Must be manufactured in the **United States**, under U.S. laws and regulations, for domestic consumption |
| **Tequila** | Must be manufactured in **Mexico**, under Mexican laws and regulations |
| **Mezcal** | Must be manufactured in **Mexico**, under Mexican laws and regulations
