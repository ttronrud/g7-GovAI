**Detailed Summary of the *Canada–Honduras Economic Growth and Prosperity Act***

---

### **Overview**

The *Canada–Honduras Economic Growth and Prosperity Act* is a federal law enacted by the Parliament of Canada to implement the **Free Trade Agreement (FTA)** between Canada and the Republic of Honduras, signed on November 5, 2013, in Ottawa. This Act provides the legal framework necessary to operationalize the economic, environmental, and labor cooperation provisions of the Agreement and its related agreements.

It is not a standalone trade policy instrument but rather a **legislative enabler** that ensures Canada’s obligations under the FTA and associated cooperation agreements are met in a manner consistent with Canadian law, governance, and international commitments.

---

### **Key Provisions and Detailed Summary**

#### **1. Citation and Purpose**
- The Act is officially titled: **Canada–Honduras Economic Growth and Prosperity Act**.
- **Purpose**: To implement the Free Trade Agreement and the related environmental and labor cooperation agreements. The overarching objectives include:
  - Establishing a **free trade area** between Canada and Honduras.
  - Expanding **reciprocal trade** to foster economic development in both countries.
  - Promoting **fair competition** in trade.
  - Increasing **investment opportunities** in both nations.
  - Removing **barriers to trade in goods and services**, supporting global and regional trade expansion.
  - Strengthening **environmental cooperation** and enforcement of environmental laws.
  - Protecting and enhancing **basic workers’ rights** and labor standards.
  - Promoting **sustainable development** through balanced economic growth.

> These goals are not merely aspirational—they are embedded in the specific provisions of the Agreement and are legally binding under this Act.

---

#### **2. Definitions**
The Act establishes key definitions to ensure clarity and consistency in interpretation:

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Agreement** | The Free Trade Agreement between Canada and Honduras, signed on November 5, 2013. |
| **Commission** | The Free Trade Commission established under Article 21.1 of the Agreement. Responsible for overseeing implementation, dispute resolution, and cooperation. |
| **Federal law** | Any Act of Parliament or regulatory instrument (orders, regulations, etc.) enacted under parliamentary authority. |
| **Minister** | The Minister for International Trade (primary representative of Canada in the Commission). |
| **Related Agreement** | Two agreements tied to the FTA: <br> - Agreement on Environmental Cooperation <br> - Agreement on Labour Cooperation <br> Both signed on the same date as the FTA. |

> These definitions clarify jurisdiction, responsibilities, and the scope of legal obligations.

---

#### **3. Interpretation Principles**
To ensure coherence and consistency:
- This Act and any federal law implementing the Agreement or related agreements must be **interpreted in a manner consistent with the Agreement or related agreement**, as applicable.
- **Exclusions**: 
  - The Act does **not apply** to **natural surface or ground water** in any state (liquid, gaseous, or solid).
  - The Act **does not restrict Parliament’s authority** to pass legislation implementing the Agreement or fulfilling Canada’s obligations under it.
- This reinforces the principle that Parliament retains full legislative power to adapt and respond to evolving trade needs.

---

#### **4. Legal Enforceability and Jurisdiction**
A critical provision limits legal actions related to the Agreement:

> **No individual or entity may bring legal proceedings** to enforce or determine rights or obligations arising solely under:
- Part 1 of the Act, or
- Any order made under Part 1.

> **Exception**: This restriction **does not apply** if the matter falls under **Section C of Chapter 10** of the FTA or **Annex 3 of the Agreement on Labour Cooperation**.

This means:
- Individuals **cannot sue** over trade or investment disputes under the Agreement.
- Disputes must be resolved through **governmental or institutional channels** (e.g., the Commission).
- The **Attorney General of Canada** must give **consent** before any legal action is taken against the Government or its obligations under the Agreement.

> This reflects a common practice in trade agreements—**preventing private litigation** to avoid undermining the stability and predictability of trade relations.

---

#### **5. Approval of the Agreements**
- The **Free Trade Agreement** and the **two related agreements** (Environmental and Labour Cooperation) are formally **approved** by Canadian law under this Act.
- This formal approval gives them **legally binding status** within Canada and enables their implementation through domestic legislation.

---

#### **6. Role of the Minister and Government Representation**
The Minister for International Trade is the **principal representative of Canada** on the **Free Trade Commission**.

**Key powers granted to the Minister**:
- Appoint representatives to:
  - Committees, subcommittees, or working groups under **Article 21.1** of the FTA.
  - Panel members under **Article 21.11** of the FTA.
  - Propose or select chairs of panels under Article 21.11.
- The Minister is responsible for designating a **government agency** (e.g., a department or division) to:
  - Facilitate the operation of **Chapter 21** of the Agreement (which deals with dispute settlement and cooperation mechanisms).
  - Provide administrative support to panels established under the Agreement.

> This ensures Canada has a clear, structured, and accountable presence in the Commission.

---

#### **7. Role of Other Ministers**
Specialized ministerial roles are assigned based on subject matter:

| Minister | Responsibilities |
|--------|------------------|
| **Minister of Environment** | Appoints Canadian representatives to the **Environmental Cooperation Committee** under Article 13 of the Environmental Agreement; appoints panelists under Annex I; proposes or selects panel chairs. |
| **Minister of Labour** | Appoints Canadian representatives to labor-related committees, working groups, or expert groups under Article 7 of the Labour Cooperation Agreement; appoints panelists under Annex 2; proposes or selects panel chairs. |

> This reflects a **sector-specific approach** to cooperation, ensuring expertise and accountability in environmental and labor domains.

---

#### **8. Financial Obligations**
The Government of Canada is responsible for:
- Paying its **appropriate share** of:
  - **Remuneration and expenses** for:
    - Panel members
    - Committee members
    - Independent experts
    - Assistants to panel members
  - **General operating expenses** of:
    - Panels
    - Committees
    - Working groups
    - Expert groups

> This ensures the **financial sustainability** of cooperation mechanisms and prevents cost-shifting to private parties.

---

#### **9. Suspension Powers (Governor in Council)**
Under exceptional circumstances, the **Governor in Council** (i.e., the Cabinet or federal executive) may issue orders to **suspend benefits or modify laws** related to Honduras or Honduran goods, in accordance with **Article 21.18 of the Agreement**.

**Actions permitted under such orders**:
- Suspend rights or privileges granted to Honduras or its goods under the Agreement or federal law.
- Modify or suspend the application of any federal law with respect to Honduras or its goods.
- Extend the application of any federal law to Honduras or its goods.
- Take any other measure deemed necessary.

> This power allows Canada to respond to **non-compliance, public health risks, or trade disruptions** in a timely and flexible manner.

**Duration**: 
- Any such order is valid only for the period specified in the order (unless revoked).

> This is a **safeguard mechanism** to protect national interests while maintaining compliance with the Agreement.

---

#### **10. Commencement of the Act**
- **Most provisions** of the Act (excluding Sections 51 and 52) come into force on a date **to be fixed by order of the Governor in Council**.
- **Note**: The Act (excluding Sections 51 and 52) came into force on **October 1, 2014**, as per **Statutory Instrument SI/2014-74**.

> This allows for a phased and carefully managed implementation, giving time for institutions, agencies, and stakeholders to prepare.

---

### **Key Legal and Policy Implications**

| Implication | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **No private litigation** | Individuals or businesses cannot sue over trade disputes under the Agreement. This promotes stability and reduces legal uncertainty. |
| **Government-led dispute resolution** | All disputes are managed through the Commission and its panels, not courts. |
| **Cooperative governance** | Canada’s participation is structured through specialized ministerial appointments and dedicated administrative support. |
| **Environmental and labor standards** | The Act supports the enforcement of environmental and labor rights through formal cooperation mechanisms. |
| **Flexibility in enforcement** | The Governor in Council has emergency powers to suspend or modify laws, ensuring national interests are protected. |

---

### **Conclusion**

The *Canada–Honduras Economic Growth and Prosperity Act* is a comprehensive and carefully structured piece of legislation that:

- Legally enacts and operationalizes the Canada–Honduras Free Trade Agreement and its related environmental and labor cooperation agreements.
- Establishes clear roles, responsibilities, and financial obligations for Canadian government institutions.
- Ensures that trade relations are built on **fair competition, sustainable development, and mutual respect for human and environmental rights**.
- Provides **institutional mechanisms** (like the Free Trade Commission) and **emergency powers** to manage trade and compliance effectively.

While it does not create new rights for individuals, it significantly enhances Canada’s ability to **manage, monitor, and benefit from** a strategic trade relationship with Honduras—fostering
