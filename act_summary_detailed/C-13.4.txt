**Detailed Summary of the *Canadian Centre on Substance Abuse Act***  

---

### **Overview**  
The *Canadian Centre on Substance Abuse Act* (CCSA Act) establishes the **Canadian Centre on Substance Abuse (the "Centre")** as a federal corporation dedicated to addressing alcohol and drug abuse in Canada. Enacted on November 1, 1988 (S.I. 88-221), the Act provides a comprehensive legal framework for the Centre’s structure, mandate, operations, governance, and financial accountability. It reflects a public health-oriented approach to substance abuse, emphasizing awareness, research, policy development, intergovernmental cooperation, and knowledge exchange.

---

### **1. Establishment of the Centre**  
- The Act formally creates a **corporation** known as the *Canadian Centre on Substance Abuse*.  
- The Centre is established to promote awareness and reduce harm related to alcohol and drug abuse across Canada.  
- The Centre operates independently of government departments and is not considered part of the federal public administration.

---

### **2. Purpose and Mandate**  
The Centre’s core mission is to:  

1. **Increase public awareness** about alcohol and drug abuse and encourage greater Canadian participation in reducing associated harms.  
2. **Promote and support collaboration** among:  
   - Federal, provincial, and territorial governments  
   - The business community  
   - Labour, professional, and voluntary organizations  
   (in matters related to substance abuse)  

3. **Facilitate the effective exchange of information** on substance abuse policies, programs, and research.  

4. **Develop and apply knowledge and expertise** in the field of substance abuse.  

5. **Support the creation of realistic, evidence-based policies and programs** aimed at reducing harm.  

6. **Raise awareness of international efforts** to combat substance abuse and support Canada’s role in global initiatives.

---

### **3. Legal Capacity and Powers**  
The Centre has the **capacity of a natural person**, meaning it can act independently and engage in legal activities. Without limiting the scope of its powers, the Centre may:  

- Review and provide informed comments on policies and programs related to substance abuse, in consultation with stakeholders.  
- Facilitate cooperative efforts among governments, businesses, and organizations.  
- Institute and support programs and activities in partnership with stakeholders.  
- Organize and support conferences, seminars, and meetings.  
- Establish and operate **information systems** (e.g., databases, libraries, research platforms) to promote the sharing of information on substance abuse.  
- Initiate, support, and publish **studies, reports, and documentation** to advance knowledge and expertise.  
- Offer **scholarships and fellowships** and promote training in addiction-related fields.  
- Conduct and disseminate **research findings**.  
- Acquire, hold, invest, and dispose of property, money, or securities (subject to any conditions attached to their donation or bequest).

---

### **4. Jurisdiction and Operations**  
- The Centre may carry out its activities **throughout Canada**.  
- Its **principal office** is located in the **National Capital Region** (as defined in the *National Capital Act*), though it may be relocated by order of the Governor in Council.

---

### **5. Governance Structure**  

#### **Board of Directors**  
- The Centre is governed by a **Board of Directors**, consisting of:  
  - A **Chairperson**  
  - Up to **12 other directors**  
- The Board ensures diverse expertise relevant to substance abuse issues.

##### **Initial Appointment (Section 7)**  
- The first Board consists of the Chairperson and **five directors** appointed by the **Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal Cabinet), who serve at the pleasure of the government.

##### **Subsequent Appointments (Section 8)**  
- **Vacant positions** are filled as follows:  
  1. **Chairperson and up to four directors** may be appointed by the **Governor in Council**, on the **recommendation of the Minister of Health**, after consultation with the Board.  
  2. **Up to eight directors** may be appointed by the **Board itself**, after consulting with:  
     - Provincial governments  
     - Representatives of the business community, labour, professional, and voluntary organizations with a stake in substance abuse.  

##### **Term of Office**  
- Terms are **not more than three years** and are specified in the appointment instrument.  
- To ensure continuity, **no more than half of the directors’ terms expire in any given year**.  
- Directors may be **re-appointed up to three times**.  
- The Chairperson presides over Board meetings and may perform other duties assigned by the Board.  
- A **Vice-Chairperson** may be elected to serve in the Chairperson’s absence or in case of vacancy.

---

### **6. Leadership: Chief Executive Officer (CEO)**  
- The Board appoints a **Chief Executive Officer (CEO)** to manage the Centre’s day-to-day operations.  
- The CEO holds office at the pleasure of the Board and is responsible for supervising staff and activities.  
- The CEO may be **temporarily replaced** by a board member or officer if absent or incapacitated, but such authority is **limited to 180 days** unless approved by the Governor in Council.  
- The CEO is entitled to:  
  - Receive notice of all Board and committee meetings  
  - Attend and participate (without voting) in meetings  
  - Receive **reasonable travel and living expenses** as approved by the Board  
  - Be paid a **remuneration** set by the Board with Governor in Council approval  

---

### **7. Committees and By-Laws**  
- The Board may establish:  
  - An **Executive Committee** (from directors)  
  - **Advisory or other committees** (which may include non-directors)  
- These committees operate under by-laws that define:  
  - Meeting procedures and quorums  
  - Roles and responsibilities  
  - Expenses for members  
  - Terms of office  

- The Board may adopt **by-laws** governing all aspects of the Centre’s management, including:  
  - Meeting procedures  
  - Quorum requirements  
  - Payment of director and committee expenses  
  - Employment and compensation of officers and employees  

---

### **8. Financial and Accountability Provisions**  

#### **Tax Status**  
- The Centre is **deemed a registered charity** under the *Income Tax Act*, allowing it to receive tax-deductible donations and operate with charitable status.

#### **Funding and Profits**  
- Any **profits or appreciation in value** of Centre assets must be **used exclusively** to further its mission.  
- **No profits may be distributed** to any Board member or individual.  
- The Centre’s **financial year** runs from **April 1 to March 31**.

#### **Auditing and Reporting**  
- Annual audits are conducted by a **qualified independent auditor** appointed by the Board.  
- The **auditor’s report** is submitted to the Board.  
- Within **three months of each financial year-end**, the **Chief Executive Officer** submits a full annual report to the Board, including:  
  - Summary of activities  
  - Financial statements  
  - Auditor’s report  
- This report is **made publicly available** at the Centre’s principal office.  

#### **Reporting to Parliament and Provincial Governments**  
- Within **one month** of submission, the Centre must provide copies of its annual report to the **Minister of Health**.  
- The Minister is required to:  
  - Lay a copy of each report before **each House of Parliament** on one of the first 15 days after the House resumes sitting.  
  - Send a copy to the **lieutenant governor in council** of each province.  

#### **Public Dissemination of Research**  
- All **publishable research results** and initiatives supported or initiated by the Centre must be made available to the public **within a reasonable time** after becoming available.

---

### **9. Evaluation and Review**  
- **Four years after the Act comes into force**, the **Minister of Health** must evaluate the Centre’s:  
  - Activities  
  - Organizational effectiveness  
- This evaluation report, including any recommended changes, must be:  
  - Laid before **each House of Parliament**  
  - Sent to the **lieutenant governors in council** of each province  
- This evaluation ensures ongoing accountability and adaptability of the Centre’s mission.

---

### **10. Legal and Regulatory Framework**  
- The Centre is **not an agent of Her Majesty in right of Canada** and its personnel are **not part of the federal public service**.  
- Key provisions of the *Canada Business Corporations Act* apply to the Centre and its officers, with appropriate modifications:  
  - Validity of corporate acts  
  - Duty to disclose conflicts of interest  
  - Standard of conduct for directors and officers  
  - Authority to indemnify directors and officers  

---

### **Key Principles and Implications**  

| Principle | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| **Independence** | The Centre operates independently from government, allowing it to provide objective, evidence-based advice. |
| **Stakeholder Engagement** | Strong emphasis on collaboration across sectors (governments, business, labour, civil society). |
| **Public Accountability** | Regular reporting to Parliament and the public ensures transparency. |
| **Research and Knowledge Development** | Focus on generating and sharing knowledge to improve policy and practice. |
| **Charitable Status** | Enables fundraising and public trust. |
| **Long-Term Evaluation** | The four-year review ensures the Centre evolves with changing needs. |
