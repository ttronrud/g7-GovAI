**Detailed Summary of the Agreement Between the Dominion of Canada and the Province of Ontario (March 24, 1924)**

---

### **Overview**

The document in question is a **Memorandum of Agreement** between the **Government of the Dominion of Canada** and the **Government of the Province of Ontario**, dated **March 24, 1924**, which establishes a framework for the administration, disposition, and financial distribution of **Indian Reserves** in Ontario. While not an Act of Parliament itself, this agreement is **enacted as binding law** by the Canadian Parliament under the authority granted by the Canadian Constitution, meaning it has the force of statute and governs the rights and responsibilities of both federal and provincial governments concerning Indigenous lands.

The agreement resolves long-standing legal uncertainties arising from prior treaty surrenders of Indian lands and clarifies the division of authority and financial benefits between the Dominion (federal government) and the Province of Ontarioâ€”particularly in the context of **land sales, mineral rights, and water power**.

---

## **Key Background and Context**

1. **Historical Treaty Framework**  
   - Treaties were historically made between the Canadian Crown and Indigenous nations, whereby certain territories were surrendered in exchange for benefits such as funds, goods, or services.
   - These surrenders often involved the **release of Indian rights** to specific areas, which were then **set apart as Indian Reserves**â€”lands reserved for the benefit of specific Indigenous bands.
   - In most cases, **after surrender**, the land was no longer subject to Indigenous title and became available for provincial administration.

2. **Legal Uncertainty**  
   - The legal status of surrendered reserves, especially regarding **mineral rights** and **future disposals**, had been the subject of appeals to the **Judicial Committee of the Privy Council** (the highest court of appeal for Canada at the time).
   - There was ambiguity over whether the **federal government** retained rights to sell or lease land after surrender, and whether **provincial governments** had a legitimate claim to proceeds or mineral rights.

3. **Precedent Agreement (July 7, 1902)**  
   - Before the final agreement, counsel for both governments had agreed (in 1902) that:
     - The **federal government** could sell, lease, or convey land from Indian Reserves (after surrender) without violating constitutional or legal rights.
     - Any such sales would be **confirmed by the Province of Ontario**, and the **federal government would retain proceeds** until the Indian interest expired.
     - The proceeds would then be **shared** with the Province, depending on circumstances.

This 1924 agreement formalizes and expands upon that earlier understanding.

## **Core Provisions of the Agreement**

---

### **1. Administration of Indian Reserves**

> *"All Indian Reserves in the Province of Ontario heretofore or hereafter set aside shall be administered by the Dominion of Canada for the benefit of the band or bands of Indians to which each may have been or may be allotted."*

- The **federal government** (via the Superintendent General of Indian Affairs) is responsible for administering Indian Reserves **for the benefit of the Indigenous bands** to which they are allotted.
- This establishes a clear **federal administrative role** in reserve management.

---

### **2. Disposition of Surrendered Reserve Lands**

> *"Portions of Reserves may be sold, leased, or otherwise disposed of by letters patent under the Great Seal of Canada... proceeds applied for the benefit of the band or bands."*

- After a band surrenders a portion of its reserve, the **federal government may sell, lease, or otherwise dispose** of that land.
- The **proceeds** from such sales must be **applied to the benefit of the band**.
- However, if:
  - The band becomes **extinct**, or
  - The reserve is declared **no longer needed** by the Superintendent General of Indian Affairs,
  
  Then:
  - The reserve is **transferred to the Province of Ontario** for administration.
  - Any **remaining balance** of proceeds (after being used for the band) is **paid to the Province of Ontario**, **plus accrued simple interest**.

> âœ… This creates a **transition mechanism** from Indigenous benefit to provincial control.

---

### **3. Mineral Rights (Including Precious Metals)**

> *"Any sale, lease or other disposition may include or be limited to the minerals (including precious metals) contained in or under the lands..."*

- Mineral rights (especially **precious metals**) are **included in the disposal process**.
- However, **all mineral claims must comply** with the **Province of Ontarioâ€™s "Bed of Navigable Waters Act" (Revised Statutes of Ontario, 1914, Chapter 31)**.
  - This law governs access to waterbeds and mineral rights in navigable waters, and its application is **non-negotiable**.

> - For reserves established under the **1873 Treaty** (recited in Schedule), **precious metals are considered part of the reserve** and may be disposed of **in the same way as land**.
> - For other treaties, the **status of precious metals is left to be determined by law and circumstances**.

### **4. Prospecting and Mining Claims**

> *"Any person authorized under Ontario law to prospect for minerals may do so on an Indian Reserve upon obtaining permission from the Indian Agent and complying with conditions."*

- Prospecting is **only permitted** for individuals or entities **authorized under Ontario law**.
- Permission must be granted by the **Indian Agent** (a federal official).
- Conditions (e.g., environmental, safety, or financial) may be imposed.

> âŒ **No unauthorized prospecting** is allowed.

>   - The **Indian Act**, or
>   - Other relevant laws.

### **5. Financial Distribution of Proceeds**

> *"One-half of the consideration (purchase price, rent, royalty, etc.) from any sale, lease, or disposition involving mining claims or minerals on Indian Reserves shall be paid to the Province of Ontario immediately upon receipt."*

- **Half of all proceeds** from mineral or land sales involving **minerals** (including mining claims) are **automatically paid to the Province of Ontario**.
- The **other half** is retained by the **federal government** and treated as per **Paragraph 1** (i.e., applied to the band or later transferred to Ontario if the band is extinct).

> âœ… This creates a **federal-provincial revenue-sharing model** for mineral development on surrendered reserves.

> ðŸ” **Exception**  
> - This rule **does not apply** to:
>   - Sales/leases of mining claims or minerals on lands set aside under the **1873 Treaty**.
>   - Lands or minerals granted or conveyed by letters patent under the **Great Seal of Upper Canada, the Province of Canada, or Ontario**, or where minerals were vested by provincial statute.

> ðŸ“Œ This protects **historical federal grants** and **provincial mineral rights** from being overridden.

### **6. Water Power**

> *"No water-power with a capacity greater than 500 horsepower, in its natural condition at average low water, shall be disposed of by the Dominion of Canada without the consent of the Province of Ontario."*

- Water power (hydroelectric potential) is a **significant source of revenue**.
- Any water-power development exceeding **500 horsepower** requires **provincial consent**.
- A **special agreement** may be made between the federal and provincial governments regarding:
  - The **division of proceeds** from such development.

> âœ… This gives the **Province of Ontario a veto** over large-scale hydroelectric projects on Indian Reserves, ensuring provincial interests are protected.

---

### **7. Confirmation of Past Dispositions**

> *"Every sale, lease, or other disposition heretofore made under the Great Seal of Canada or under the direction of the Government of Canada of lands that were part of an Indian Reserve in Ontario is hereby confirmed."*

- All **prior federal land sales or leases** involving land that was once part of an Indian Reserve are **legally confirmed**.
- These sales are **subject to**:
  - The **Bed of Navigable Waters Act**.
  - The **provisions of Paragraph 1** regarding financial distribution (i.e., half proceeds go to Ontario).

> âš–ï¸ **Provincial Sales**  
> - Any land sold by the **Province of Ontario** that was previously part of a reserve remains under **provincial control** and is **not subject to federal redistribution rules**.

---
