The **Indian (Soldier Settlement) Act** is a historical piece of legislation that established a framework for the settlement of Indigenous soldiers—specifically, First Nations individuals—by enabling the government to provide land for their use, particularly in the context of post-Conflicts settlement. However, the version presented here is not a standalone act but rather a **provisional or supplementary enactment** that modifies or applies the original *Soldier Settlement Act* to Indian (First Nations) settlers under the jurisdiction of the **Minister of Indian Affairs and Northern Development**.

Below is a **detailed and structured summary** of the provided text:

---

### **1. Citation and Repeal Status**
- The Act is officially cited as the **Indian (Soldier Settlement) Act**.
- It was **repealed in 1951** by **Act No. 29, Section 123**.  
  → This indicates that the original Soldier Settlement Act (as it stood) was formally abolished in 1951, suggesting that the provisions described here are **not current law** and are likely **historical or transitional** in nature.

---

### **2. Scope and Applicability of the Act**
- The Act **applies only to "settlers who are Indians"**, as defined under this Act.
- The **administration** of the Act (with certain exceptions) is **transferred to the Superintendent General of Indian Affairs**.
- The **exceptions** include:
  - Sections 3, 4, 8, 9, 10, 11, 14, 29, subsection 2 of section 51, and section 61.
  - **Entire Part III** of the Act (which may relate to land rights or band governance).
- These excluded sections are **not subject to the new administration** and remain outside the scope of this provision.

### **3. Authority of the Deputy Superintendent General**
- The **Deputy Superintendent General of Indian Affairs** is granted **powers equivalent to those of the original Soldier Settlement Board** under the original Soldier Settlement Act.
- A key substitution is made in the text:  
  → The words **"The Soldier Settlement Board"** and **"The Board"** in the original Act are **replaced** with **"The Deputy Superintendent General of Indian Affairs"** for the purposes of administration.
- This substitution effectively **transfers the administrative authority** of the Board to the Deputy Superintendent General, allowing him to act as the governing body for soldier settlement matters involving Indigenous individuals.

### **4. Integration of the Act with This Part**
- The modified Soldier Settlement Act (with the above exceptions) is **read as one continuous part** with this section of the current Act.

### **5. Land Acquisition Powers**
- The Deputy Superintendent General has the **authority to acquire land** for an Indian settler:
  - **Within or outside** an Indian reserve.
- He may **set apart a portion of common band lands** (i.e., shared land of the band) **without requiring the consent of the band council**.
  - This is a significant power, as it allows **direct land allocation** without formal band approval, potentially bypassing traditional governance structures.

---

### **6. Security for Advances**
- If land is acquired or set apart **on an Indian reserve**, the Deputy Superintendent General may **take the land as security** for any financial advances (e.g., loans or funding) made to the settler.
- The **provisions of the original Soldier Settlement Act** apply to such transactions, subject to their relevance.
- **Crucial Limitation**:  
  → Only the **individual Indian settler’s interest** in the land is secured.  
  → The **band’s interest in the land is not affected** or diminished in any way.
- This ensures that while the settler may use the land as collateral, the band retains its underlying ownership rights.

---

### **7. Cooperation with the Soldier Settlement Board**
- The **Soldier Settlement Board and its officers/employees** are required to:
  - **Aid and assist** the Deputy Superintendent General in carrying out the purposes of this Act.
  - Assist to the **extent requested**.
- The Board may **sell, convey, or transfer property** (held for disposal) to the Deputy Superintendent General **at agreed prices** for the purpose of executing settlement plans.

---

### **8. Resolution of Doubts or Disputes**
- In the event of **uncertainty or difficulty** in administering the Act or in interpreting the powers of the Deputy Superintendent General:
  - The **Governor in Council** (i.e., the federal Cabinet, through the Minister) may issue an **order** to resolve the issue.

  - Define powers and procedures.
- **Limitation**:  
  → Such orders **cannot extend** the powers already granted under the original Soldier Settlement Act.
  → They can only **clarify or interpret**, not expand authority.

### **9. Reference to the Indian Act**
- In sections 187 to 190 of the original Soldier Settlement Act:

  - Any reference to the **Superintendent General** or **Deputy Superintendent General of Indian Affairs** is **deemed to be a reference to the Minister of Indian Affairs and Northern Development**.

## **Conclusion**
The **Indian (Soldier Settlement) Act** (as described in the text) is a **historical administrative measure** that enabled the federal government to settle Indigenous soldiers with land, particularly within or outside reserves. It was **repealed in 1951**, and its provisions are now obsolete. The key features—such as the transfer of authority to the Deputy Superintendent General, the power to allocate land without band consent, and the protection of individual settler interests—highlight a policy focused on individual settlement rather than community-based land governance.

While it may have served a practical purpose in the early 20th century, it reflects a **legacy of top-down governance** and **limited Indigenous agency**. Modern Indigenous rights frameworks emphasize **consent, self-determination, and shared land stewardship**, rendering this Act obsolete and inappropriate in contemporary Canadian law.
