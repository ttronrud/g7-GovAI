## üîç **Detailed Summary of the Preclearance Act, 2016**

### üìå **1. Purpose and Background**

The *Preclearance Act, 2016* implements the **Agreement on Land, Rail, Marine, and Air Transport Preclearance** between Canada and the United States, signed on **March 16, 2015**, in Washington, D.C. This agreement builds upon the earlier **Air Transport Preclearance Agreement** from 2001 (signed in Toronto).

#### Key Objectives:
- **Facilitate cross-border travel and trade** by allowing border inspections to occur *before* travelers or goods enter the destination country.
- **Enhance security** by enabling early detection of threats (e.g., contraband, terrorism, public health risks).
- **Ensure compliance with both national laws** ‚Äî while U.S. officers conduct preclearance in Canada, and Canadian officers do so in the U.S., **Canadian law remains supreme** in all matters involving rights, freedoms, and human dignity.

> The Act establishes a **reciprocal system** where:
> - U.S. preclearance officers may inspect travelers and goods **in Canada** before they enter the U.S.
> - Canadian border services officers may inspect travelers and goods **in the U.S.** before they enter Canada.

### üìå **3. Jurisdiction and Legal Framework**

#### ‚úÖ Canadian Law Applies in All Preclearance Zones
- **Canadian law governs all aspects** of preclearance operations, including:
  - The **Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms**
  - The **Canadian Bill of Rights**
  - The **Canadian Human Rights Act**
- No U.S. law can override Canadian constitutional rights or human rights protections.
- Canadian authorities have **full jurisdiction** to enforce Canadian law in preclearance areas and perimeters.

#### ‚öñÔ∏è Reciprocity and Oversight
- The **Minister** (designated by Governor in Council) must ensure that all preclearance officers receive **mandatory training** on Canadian law.
- The **Preclearance Consultative Group** (established under the Agreement) allows travelers to report concerns about preclearance activities (e.g., privacy, health, security) directly to Canadian senior officials.

### üìå **4. Designation of Preclearance Areas and Perimeters**

- The **Minister** may designate:
  - Specific **preclearance areas** (e.g., at airports or ports).
  - **Preclearance perimeters** near conveyances (e.g., planes, ships) preparing to leave Canada or enter the U.S.
- These designations can be **temporary or conditional**, based on operational needs or security requirements.
- If a conveyance is stationed outside a designated preclearance area, the following are also considered preclearance areas:
  - The conveyance itself
  - The corridor between the area and the conveyance
  - Any other conveyance used to transport travelers/goods between the area and the conveyance

> This ensures that security and inspection can be maintained even when conveyances are in transit or parked.

### üìå **5. Powers of Preclearance Officers**

#### ‚úÖ Powers Allowed (Under U.S. Law, but Subject to Canadian Law)
- **Question and examine travelers** (not interrogation or detention without cause)
- **Collect information** about travelers or goods
- **Examine, search, and detain goods** (including taking samples)
- **Direct travelers to report** or identify themselves
- **Direct unauthorized persons to leave** the preclearance area

#### ‚ùå Powers Prohibited
- **No questioning, interrogation, examination, search, seizure, forfeiture, detention, or arrest** under U.S. law that would violate Canadian law or constitutional rights.
- **No use of force** intended to cause death or grievous bodily harm ‚Äî **except** when self-preservation or protection of others is necessary.

### üìå **6. Traveler Rights and Obligations**

#### ‚úÖ Mandatory Requirements for Travelers Bound for the U.S.
- Must have **government-issued photo ID** (federal, provincial, local, or foreign).
- Must **immediately report to a preclearance officer** upon entry.
- Must:
  - Answer questions **truthfully**
  - Present goods, open packages, or unload conveyances as directed
  - Comply with all directions from preclearance officers, police, or border services officers
  - Follow any **regulatory requirements**

#### ‚ùå Withdrawal from Preclearance
- Travelers may **withdraw from preclearance** under section 29 (not detailed here), which would allow them to exit the preclearance area and avoid inspection.

#### üö´ No Right to Refuse Search (with Exceptions)
- Travelers **cannot refuse** a frisk or strip search **if the officer has reasonable grounds** to suspect concealed items.
- However, **searches must be conducted in accordance with Canadian law** and **cannot involve force or discrimination**.

### üìå **8. Right to Legal Review and Protection**

- Before any search under sections 21‚Äì23 (frisk, strip, bowel, etc.), the traveler must be **informed of their right to be taken before a senior officer**.
- The **search is only permitted** if the senior officer **agrees** that the officer is authorized to conduct it.

  - A suitable person of the same sex is authorized by the officer.

> This reflects a commitment to **privacy, dignity, and gender sensitivity** in border operations.
