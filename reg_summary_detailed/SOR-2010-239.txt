**Detailed Summary of the *Electronic Documents (Banks and Bank Holding Companies) Regulations***  
*Pursuant to the Bank Act (Bank Act), S.C. 2005, c. 54, s. 135 and s. 138; in force as of June 1, 2011*

---

### **1. Regulatory Background and Authority**
These regulations were made by **His Excellency the Governor General in Council**, on the recommendation of the **Minister of Finance**, under specific provisions of the *Bank Act*:
- **Section 978(1)a**
- Sections **993b, 995b, 996b, 998b, 1002b, and 1003b**

The purpose of these regulations is to **establish rules governing the use of electronic documents** by banks and bank holding companies in compliance with the Bank Act, particularly in relation to the **provision and receipt of notices, documents, and other information**.

---

### **2. Key Definitions and Interpretations**

- **"Act"** refers to the *Bank Act*.
- **"Prescribed notices, documents or other information"** (for section 993 of the Act) means the types of information specified in **sections 82 to 135 of the Bank Act**.
- All electronic documents provided under these regulations must be:
  - **Clear, simple, and not misleading** in language and presentation.
  - Delivered in a **language and format that is understandable** to the recipient.

---

### **3. When Electronic Documents Are Not Required to Be Sent to a Designated Information System**

Under **paragraph 995(1)(b)** of the Bank Act, an electronic document **does not need to be sent directly to a designated information system** if:
1. It is **posted on or made available through a generally accessible electronic source** (e.g., a public website); and  
2. The **addressee is given written notice** (in paper or electronic form) of:
   - The **availability** of the document; and  
   - Its **location** (e.g., URL or link).

### **4. Consent for Electronic Document Delivery**

#### **General Principle (Paragraph 995(1)(c))**
An addressee may give **consent** to receive electronic documents in:
- **Writing** (paper or electronic form), or  
- **Orally**

> This flexibility supports both formal and informal consent mechanisms.

---

#### **Requirements Before Written Consent Is Given**

Before an addressee gives written consent, the originator (or their agent) must provide the following **in writing** (paper or electronic form):

1. **Right to revoke consent at any time**

2. **Responsibility for informing the originator of changes**

   - The designated information system; or  
   - Contact information related to that system.

3. **Retention obligations**  
   â†’ The electronic document will be retained for a period specified in the notice.  
   â†’ The **addressee is responsible for retaining a copy** of the document.

4. **Effective date of consent**  
   â†’ The notice must specify **when the consent takes effect**.

#### **Consent in Writing â€“ Mandatory Information**
If consent is given in writing, it must include:
- The **name of the information system** designated by the addressee for receiving electronic documents; and  
- A **list** (in paper or electronic form) of the specific notices, documents, or information covered by the consent.

> This ensures that recipients are fully aware of what they are agreeing to receive.

---

#### **Consent Given Orally**
If consent is given **orally**, the originator must:
- Provide the addressee with a **written notice** (paper or electronic) containing the information required under subsection (2) above; and  
- **Confirm in writing** the details of the consent (e.g., effective date, designated system, etc.).

> This ensures that oral consents are legally binding and properly documented.

---

#### **One-Time Consent Exception**
If the consent is for a **one-time delivery** of an electronic document (e.g., a single report or notice), then **subsection (2)** (the pre-consent notification requirements) **does not apply**.

> This provides flexibility for temporary or ad hoc communications.

---

#### **Revocation of Consent**
An addressee may revoke consent:
- In writing,  
- In paper, or  
- Orally.

Upon revocation, the originator must:
- Provide the addressee with **written confirmation** (paper or electronic) of the revocation.  
- Specify **when the revocation takes effect**.

> This ensures that revocation is formalized and effective, protecting both parties.

### **5. Concurrent Delivery to Multiple Addressees**

Under **paragraphs 996(b) and 998(b)** of the Bank Act:
- If a notice, document, or information is required to be sent to **several addressees**, it must be provided to **all of them simultaneously** (concurrently).

> This prevents delays or inconsistencies in communication and ensures equal treatment of recipients.

---

### **6. Delivery to a Designated Information System**

If a notice or document is required to be sent to a **specific physical location** under the Bank Act, it may instead be delivered:
- To an **information system designated by the addressee** (e.g., a secure email or internal portal).

> This allows for digital substitution of physical delivery, provided the recipient has designated a system and consented to receive the document there.

---

### **7. When an Electronic Document is Considered "Provided"**

An electronic document is deemed to have been **provided** to an addressee when:
- It **leaves the control of the originator or their agent** (i.e., it is sent from their system); **or**
- It is **posted on or made available through the originatorâ€™s secure website**.

> This establishes a clear legal benchmark for when delivery is complete, even in digital form.

> âš ï¸ **Important**: This rule does **not affect** the timing of delivery under **section 9** of the Bank Act, which requires a **paper copy to be mailed** to the addressee if there is a reasonable belief that they did not receive the electronic version.

> So, even if an electronic document is delivered via a secure website, a **paper copy must still be mailed** if there is reason to believe the addressee missed it.

---

### **8. When an Electronic Document is Considered "Received"**

An electronic document is considered **received** when:
- It **enters the designated information system** of the addressee;  
- It is **posted on or made available through the originatorâ€™s secure website**; or  
- The **notice in paragraph 4(1)(b)** (the notice of availability) is received by the addressee.

> This defines the point at which the recipient is legally deemed to have received the document, which is critical for compliance with record-keeping and legal obligations.

---

### **9. Electronic Signatures**

Under **section 1002 of the Bank Act**, a signature may be **electronic** and must consist of:
- One or more **letters, characters, numbers, or symbols** in digital form that are:
  - Incorporated in, attached to, or associated with the electronic document.

> This allows for the use of digital signatures (e.g., digital certificates, email signatures, or electronic seals) to authenticate electronic communications.

---

### **10. Regulatory Timeline and Enforcement**

- **Coming into force**: **June 1, 2011**  
- These regulations apply to **banks and bank holding companies** operating under the *Bank Act*.
