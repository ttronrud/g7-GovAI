| Element | Description |

| **Personal Information Involved** | A description of the personal information that was accessed, disclosed, or lost. Only information known at the time of reporting must be included. |

| **Steps to Notify Affected Individuals** | A description of how the organization plans to notify affected individuals (in line with PIPEDA subsection 10.1(3)). |
| **Point of Contact** | The name and contact details of a designated person within the organization who can answer questions from the Commissioner about the breach. |

### 2. **Notification to Affected Individuals (Direct & Indirect)**

Under PIPEDA subsection 10.1(3), organizations must notify affected individuals of a breach. The regulations define two types of notification: **direct** and **indirect**.

#### âœ… Direct Notification (Preferred Method)
- Must be delivered **personally, by phone, mail, email, or any other form of communication** that a reasonable person would consider appropriate in the circumstances.
- This method is generally preferred because it allows individuals to receive timely, personalized information and ask questions.

> Example: A breach involving a phishing attack affecting employee email accounts would be best notified via email or phone to the affected employees.

#### ðŸš« Indirect Notification (When Direct Is Not Feasible)
Indirect notification applies when **direct notification would cause further harm or undue hardship** â€” or when **contact information is unavailable**.

##### When Indirect Notification Is Required:
- Direct notification would likely **cause further harm** to the individual (e.g., due to fear, stigma, or identity theft risk).
- Direct notification would impose **undue hardship** on the organization (e.g., due to lack of resources, geographic spread, or technical limitations).
- The organization **does not have contact information** for the affected individuals.

##### How Indirect Notification Must Be Delivered:
- Must be through **public communication or similar measures** that are reasonably expected to reach the affected individuals.
- Examples include:
  - Publicly posted notices on websites or social media.
  - Press releases or media alerts.
  - Notices in newsletters or community bulletins.
  - Posting on official government or organizational websites.

> âš ï¸ **Important**: Indirect notification is **not a substitute** for direct notification when possible. It is a fallback mechanism to protect individuals and organizations from disproportionate harm or burden.
