**Detailed Summary of the Regulation:**

---

### **Regulatory Instrument Overview**

This regulation is a **Privileges and Immunities Order** issued under the authority of the **Governor General in Council**, based on the recommendations of Canada‚Äôs **Minister of Foreign Affairs** and the **Minister of Finance**. It is enacted pursuant to **Section 5a of the *Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act*** (FMOA), which empowers the federal government to grant legal privileges and immunities to international organizations and their representatives when they conduct official business in Canada.

The specific instrument in question is the **19th Meeting of the Parties to the Montreal Protocol Privileges and Immunities Order**, effective from **September 10 to September 24, 2007**.

---

### **Legal Basis**

- **Primary Statute**: *Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act* (S.C. 1991, c. 41)
- **Authority Section**: Section 5a of the Act allows for the granting of privileges and immunities to international organizations and their representatives during specific events or periods.
- **Supporting Provision**: Sections 3 and 10 of S.C. 2002, c. 12 (the *Montreal Protocol and Related Measures Act*) provide procedural and interpretive support.

---

### **Key Definitions**

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Convention** | Refers to the *Convention on the Privileges and Immunities of the United Nations*, as set out in Schedule III of the FMOA. This is the legal framework governing privileges and immunities for UN-related entities. |
| **Experts performing missions for the Organization** | Individuals working under the Montreal Protocol whose identities are formally communicated to the Government of Canada by the Organization. These are technical or scientific experts deployed to support the meeting. |
| **Meeting** | The 19th Meeting of the Parties to the Montreal Protocol, scheduled to take place in **Montreal from September 17 to 21, 2007**. |
| **Montreal Protocol** | The *Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer*, adopted on September 16, 1987, by the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), and subsequently amended over time. It is a global treaty aimed at phasing out ozone-depleting substances. |
| **Organization** | The intergovernmental body comprising the Parties to the Montreal Protocol and its Secretariat, as defined in Article 1 of the Protocol. |

| **Relevant Period** | The time frame from **September 10, 2007**, to **September 24, 2007** ‚Äî this is the period during which the privileges and immunities apply. |

### **Core Provisions and Legal Effects**

#### **1. Legal Capacity of the Organization**
> *During the relevant period, the Organization shall have in Canada the legal capacity of a body corporate and the privileges and immunities set out in Articles II and III of the Convention.*

- The **Organization (the Parties to the Montreal Protocol and its Secretariat)** is recognized in Canada as a **body corporate** during the relevant period.
- This means it can:
  - Enter into legal agreements,
  - Own property,
  - Be sued or sue others,
  - Hold assets,
  - Conduct business operations in Canada.
- It is granted **privileges and immunities** under **Articles II and III of the UN Convention on Privileges and Immunities**, which include:
  - Immunity from legal process (e.g., lawsuits),
  - Immunity from taxation,
  - Immunity from search and seizure,
  - Freedom from interference in its operations.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Note**: This status is **temporary**, limited to the relevant period (September 10‚Äì24, 2007).

---

#### **2. Privileges and Immunities for State Representatives**
> *Representatives of member states of the Organization shall have in Canada, to the extent required for their functions, the privileges and immunities set out in Article IV of the Convention.*

- Diplomatic or official representatives of **member countries** participating in the meeting and preparatory meetings are granted **immunities and privileges** under **Article IV** of the UN Convention.
- These include:
  - Immunity from arrest or detention (except for serious crimes),
  - Immunity from civil and criminal jurisdiction,
  - Protection from search or seizure,
  - Freedom to conduct official business without interference.
- These rights are **limited to the functions required** in connection with the meeting and preparatory meetings.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è The immunity applies only to acts performed in the **official capacity** of their state and in relation to the Montreal Protocol activities.

---

#### **3. Privileges and Immunities for Experts**
> *Experts performing missions for the Organization shall have in Canada, to the extent required for their functions, the privileges and immunities set out in Article VI of the Convention.*

- Technical experts (e.g., scientists, environmental specialists) engaged by the Organization to support the meeting are granted **immunities and privileges** under **Article VI** of the Convention.
- This includes:
  - Immunity from legal proceedings related to their official duties,
  - Protection from interference in their work,
  - Immunity from liability for actions taken in the course of their mission.
- These privileges are **only applicable to the extent necessary** for their participation in the meeting and preparatory meetings.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è The experts must have their identities formally communicated to the Government of Canada by the Organization for these rights to apply.

---

#### **4. Exclusion of Tax Liability**
> *The privileges and immunities referred to in section 2 do not apply to Canadian citizens or permanent residents of Canada in respect of liability for taxes or duties imposed by any law in Canada.*

- This is a **critical limitation**:  
  - Even though representatives and experts enjoy immunities, **they are not exempt from Canadian tax obligations**.
  - Canadian citizens or permanent residents **cannot claim tax immunity** for income, property, or other taxable events.
  - This ensures Canada maintains fiscal accountability and revenue collection rights.

> üîç Example: An expert from the U.S. attending the meeting may not be subject to Canadian tax on income earned during the mission ‚Äî but a Canadian citizen working for the Organization would still owe taxes.

---

### **Effective Date and Jurisdiction**

- The Order **comes into force on the day it is registered** with the Statutes of Canada (i.e., published in the *Canada Gazette*).
- It is **temporary and time-bound**, effective only during the **relevant period (September 10‚Äì24, 2007)**.
- It applies **only to Canada** and does not extend to other countries or future events unless a new order is issued.

---

### **Purpose and Significance**

This Order enables the **smooth and effective operation** of the 19th Meeting of the Parties to the Montreal Protocol in Montreal by:

- Providing **legal recognition** of the Organization as a body corporate in Canada,
- Ensuring **diplomatic and technical personnel** can operate without fear of legal or administrative interference,
- Facilitating **international cooperation** on environmental protection by protecting the integrity of scientific and policy discussions,
- Respecting Canada‚Äôs sovereignty and fiscal responsibilities through clear limits on tax immunity.

---

### **Key Limitations and Conditions**

| Limitation | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Time-bound** | Applies only from September 10 to 24, 2007. |
| **Function-specific** | Privileges and immunities apply only to acts related to the meeting and preparatory meetings. |
| **No tax immunity** | Canadian citizens or residents remain liable for Canadian taxes. |
| **Requires formal communication** | Experts must have their identities communicated to the Government of Canada. |
| **Based on UN Convention** | All rights derive from the UN Convention on Privileges and Immunities, not from Canadian law. |

---

### **Conclusion**

This regulation is a **targeted, temporary measure** that grants legal and operational protections to the **Montreal Protocol Organization** and its participants during a major international environmental conference. It reflects Canada‚Äôs commitment to multilateral environmental governance while ensuring that the exercise of international privileges is balanced with national legal and fiscal interests.

It exemplifies how Canada manages its international obligations under the **Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act**, ensuring that diplomatic and scientific missions can function effectively within Canadian jurisdiction without compromising legal accountability or public revenue.
