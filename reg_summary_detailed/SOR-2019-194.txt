## **Detailed Summary of the Administration and Enforcement (Assisted Human Reproduction Act) Regulations**

### **1. Legal Basis and Adoption**
- These regulations were made under **subsection 65(1)b** of the *Assisted Human Reproduction Act (AHRA)*.
- The Governor in Council, on the recommendation of the Minister of Health, adopted the proposed regulations after they were laid before each House of Parliament under **subsection 66(1)** of the AHRA.
- The regulations are officially published in the *Statutes of Canada*, with references:
  - **S.C. 2004, c. 2** (original enactment)
  - **S.C. 2012, c. 19, s. 737** (amendment)

> **Purpose**: To provide detailed administrative and enforcement rules for implementing the *Assisted Human Reproduction Act*, ensuring compliance with legal standards related to assisted reproduction, embryo handling, donor consent, and court procedures.

---

### **2. Key Definitions**

#### **Act**
- Refers to the *Assisted Human Reproduction Act* (AHRA), the primary legislation governing assisted reproductive technologies in Canada.

#### **Common-Law Partner**
- A person who:
  - Cohabits with another individual in a conjugal relationship;
  - Has done so for **at least one year** at the relevant time.
- This definition establishes the legal status of cohabiting couples for the purposes of reproductive rights and consent.

> **Important Note**: The term "spouse" does **not** include someone who lives separate and apart from their spouse due to the breakdown of marriage. This excludes individuals in legal separation or divorce from being considered spouses under these regulations.

#### **Donor (in relation to an in vitro embryo)**

There are **two distinct categories** of donors depending on the purpose of embryo creation:

| Case | Donor Definition |
|------|------------------|
| **Embryo created for reproductive use** | - If created for an individual who has **no spouse or common-law partner** at the time of creation, then that individual is the donor, regardless of the source of reproductive material (sperm, egg).<br>- If created for a **spouse or common-law partner couple**, then the couple are jointly the donors, regardless of source of reproductive material. <br>- **Exception**: If the couple were married at creation, and later **separate**, and one individual was the sole contributor of reproductive material, then **that individual becomes the donor** under paragraph (a) of the definition — even if they were once married — **if** the couple no longer share a marital or common-law relationship. |
| **Embryo created for research or training purposes** | The individuals whose **human reproductive material** (sperm or egg) was used to create the embryo are the donors. |

> This distinction ensures that in research contexts, the original contributors retain rights and consent obligations.

#### **Designated Officer**
- Refers to the **Director General** responsible for:
  - Overseeing compliance with the AHRA;
  - Enforcing provisions of the Act;
  - Coordinating inspections and investigations.

> This role centralizes administrative and enforcement authority within the Health department.

### **3. Procedural Requirements for Notices and Court Applications**

#### **Notice to Provincial Court Judge (under section 51(1) of the AHRA)**

- A notice must be served **at least 15 clear days** before an application for an **order of restoration** is filed in a provincial court.
- The notice must include:
  1. The name of the **provincial court judge** to whom the application will be made;
  2. The **date, time, and place** of the hearing;
  3. The **seized material or information** involved;
  4. The **evidence** the applicant intends to present to prove entitlement to possession of the seized material.

> This ensures transparency, due process, and timely notice to all parties involved in enforcement actions.

---

### **4. Consent Requirements for Measures Regarding Embryos (Section 54 of the AHRA)**

These rules ensure that any action taken regarding an embryo (e.g., storage, disposal, or transfer) is consistent with the **consent of the donor(s)**.

#### **For Embryos Created for Reproductive Use**
- If the embryo was created for a **couple’s reproductive use**, any measure (e.g., transfer, destruction, or storage) must be consistent with the **consent of each spouse or common-law partner**.
- This protects both partners’ rights and ensures that decisions are made jointly, especially in cases where one partner may have withdrawn consent or is no longer in a relationship.

> **Clarification**: Subsection (1) does **not apply** to donors referred to in subsection 1(3) — i.e., those who contributed material for research or training purposes.

#### **For Embryos Created for Research or Training Purposes**
- Any measure regarding the embryo must be consistent with the **consent of each individual** whose reproductive material (sperm or egg) was used.
- This reinforces ethical standards in research involving human biological materials.

---

### **5. Requirements for Written Consent**

- **Donor consent must be in writing** and:
  - Signed by the donor;
  - Attested (witnessed) by a credible witness.
- **Exception for Anonymous Donors**:
  - If the sperm, ovum, or embryo was donated **under the condition of anonymity**, the consent must be:
    - Signed by the person who originally obtained consent from the donor;
    - Attested in a document (not necessarily by the donor themselves).

> This ensures that anonymity does not undermine legal accountability — the original consent is preserved and traceable.

---

### **6. Disposal of Forfeited Embryos or Materials**

- If it is **impossible to obtain consent** from the donor regarding forfeited sperm, ova, or in vitro embryos:
  - The **designated officer** may authorize an inspector to dispose of the material.
  - However, such disposal can **only occur after a period of 180 days** from the date of forfeiture.

> This 180-day waiting period provides a reasonable opportunity to locate or obtain consent from the donor, protecting the rights of potential donors and ensuring ethical compliance.

---

### **7. Effective Date**

- The regulations come into force on the day that **section 45 of the AHRA** comes into force.
- If the regulations are **registered after that date**, they will take effect on the **date of registration**.

> This ensures that enforcement rules are implemented in alignment with the broader legislative framework and avoids retroactive application.

---

### **8. Additional Notes**

- These regulations were **in force as of June 10, 2019**.
- They reflect a balance between:
  - Protecting individual rights (especially of donors and reproductive partners);
  - Ensuring legal accountability and transparency in embryo management;
  - Supporting both reproductive and research uses of assisted reproduction technologies.

## **Key Principles and Implications**

| Principle | Implication |
|--------|------------|
| **Consent is central** | All actions involving embryos require valid, documented, and informed consent — especially from donors and reproductive partners. |
| **Relationship status matters** | Whether a person is a spouse or common-law partner directly affects who holds consent rights. |
| **Anonymity is not a loophole** | Even anonymous donations require traceable, documented consent. |
| **Due process in enforcement** | Notice and hearing requirements ensure fairness and legal transparency. |
| **Protection of donor rights** | A 180-day grace period before disposal prevents hasty decisions without consent. |

The *Administration and Enforcement (Assisted Human Reproduction Act) Regulations* provide a comprehensive, legally grounded framework for managing the creation, storage, use, and disposition of in vitro embryos in Canada. By clearly defining key terms, establishing strict consent requirements, and ensuring procedural fairness, these regulations uphold ethical standards, protect individual rights, and support the safe and lawful operation of assisted reproduction programs — both for reproductive purposes and for research.
