**Detailed Summary of the Electronic Alternatives Regulations for the Purposes of the Federal Real Property and Federal Immovables Act**

---

### **1. Regulatory Context and Purpose**

The *Electronic Alternatives Regulations* (S.C. 2000, c. 5), made on **December 6, 2004**, by **Reginald B. Alcock**, the President of the Treasury Board, are a set of rules designed to enable the use of **electronic documents and electronic signatures** in administrative processes related to **federal real property** located in the **Province of Ontario**.

These regulations are issued under **section 50 of the Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA)**, which empowers the responsible authority to establish rules that allow for the use of electronic alternatives to paper-based records — provided they meet certain legal and security standards.

The "responsible authority" is defined in subsection 31(1) of PIPEDA as the person or body responsible for managing personal information under the Act. In this case, the **President of the Treasury Board** holds that role with respect to federal real property matters.

---

### **2. Scope of Application**

- **Applicable Jurisdiction**: These regulations apply **only to federal real property situated in the Province of Ontario**.
- **Excluded Areas**: Federal real property located outside Ontario is outside the scope of these regulations.

This limited geographic scope ensures alignment with provincial laws and administrative practices, particularly those of Ontario.

> These definitions ensure that electronic alternatives are evaluated against the legal framework established by Ontario’s land registration laws.

### **4. Application of Electronic Documents (Section 41 of PIPEDA)**

Section 41 of PIPEDA allows for electronic documents to satisfy requirements for written documents in specific statutory contexts. Under these regulations:

An **electronic document** will be accepted as valid and equivalent to a physical written document **only if it conforms to the form, format, or requirements set out in Ontario law**.

Specifically, electronic documents must meet the requirements for written documentation in the following FRPFA provisions:

| FRPFA Provision | Type of Document |
|------------------|------------------|
| Section 5 | Crown grants (e.g., land grants) |
| Section 7 | Plans related to land grants |
| Section 6 | Licences (e.g., land use or access rights) |
| Section 11 | Instruments transferring administration and control of federal property |

> **Key Principle**: Electronic documents are legally valid only if they meet the **formal requirements** established by Ontario’s land registration laws (e.g., format, authenticity, record-keeping standards). This ensures legal enforceability and consistency with provincial land administration systems.

### **5. Application of Electronic Signatures (Section 43 of PIPEDA)**

Section 43 of PIPEDA permits electronic signatures to satisfy requirements for physical signatures or countersignatures in official acts and instruments.

Under these regulations, an **electronic signature** is valid only if it meets the **formal requirements or authorizations established under Ontario law**.

This applies to the following statutory situations:

| FRPFA Provision | Context |
|------------------|--------|
| Subsection 5(5) | Instruments or acts related to Crown grants (paragraph 5(1)(b)) or under subsections 5(2) or (4) |
| Subsection 5(6) | Instruments or acts related to Crown grants (paragraph 5(1)(b)) or under subsection 5(2) |
| Section 6 | Licences |
| Subsection 7(2) | Plans under subsection 7(1) of the FRPFA |
| Subsection 11(1) | Instruments transferring administration and control of federal property |

Additionally, **officers authorized under section 3 of the FRPFA** (such as property officers or designated officials) must use electronic signatures that conform to Ontario law to satisfy any required signature or countersignature under subsection (1).

> **Key Principle**: Electronic signatures are legally binding only when they comply with Ontario’s standards for electronic authentication — such as validity, integrity, and identity verification — ensuring that electronic approvals are as reliable as handwritten ones.

- **Enhanced Efficiency and Accessibility**: Reduces administrative burden, improves access for stakeholders (e.g., applicants, officials), and supports digital transformation in government services.
