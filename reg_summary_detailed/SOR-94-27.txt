### **Regulation Summary: Certification of Equivalent Topography Protection in Switzerland**

**Regulatory Text:**  
*The Minister of Industry, Science and Technology, pursuant to subparagraph 4(1)(c)(iii) of the Integrated Circuit Topography Act, hereby certifies that Switzerland is a country that confers protection that is substantially equal to the protection conferred by that Act on nationals of Canada and individuals or legal entities that have in Canada a real and effective establishment for the creation of topographies or the manufacture of integrated circuit products.*

**Statutory Basis:**  
- **Act:** *Integrated Circuit Topography Act*  
- **Statutes Cited:**  
  - *S.C. 1993, c. 15, s. 25* (the specific section of the Act that authorizes the certification)  
  - *S.C. 1990, c. 37* (the original enactment of the Act)  
- **Date of Certification:** January 11, 1994  

---

### **Purpose of the Regulation**

This regulation serves as an **official certification** by the Canadian government that the **legal protection for integrated circuit topographies in Switzerland** is **substantially equivalent** to the protection provided under Canada’s own *Integrated Circuit Topography Act*.

This certification is a key component of **international intellectual property (IP) protection**, particularly under the framework of **mutual recognition and reciprocal protection**. It enables Canadian nationals and entities with a real and effective establishment in Canada to **enjoy the same level of protection in Switzerland** as if they were operating under Canadian law — and vice versa, under certain conditions.

---

### **Key Legal Provisions Involved**

#### **Subparagraph 4(1)(c)(iii) of the Integrated Circuit Topography Act**
This provision empowers the Minister of Industry, Science and Technology to:
- Certify that a foreign country (in this case, Switzerland) provides **protection for integrated circuit topographies** that is **substantially equivalent** to that offered under Canadian law.
- The certification is based on a comparison of the **scope, duration, enforceability, and territorial reach** of topography rights in the foreign jurisdiction.

#### **What Constitutes "Substantially Equal Protection"?**
The term "substantially equal" means that:
- The foreign country provides **protection for the topography** (i.e., the three-dimensional layout of transistors and other components in an integrated circuit).
- The protection is **sufficiently strong** to prevent unauthorized copying or use of the topography.
- The **duration of protection** is comparable (typically 10 years from the date of filing, renewable for another 5 years).
- The **rights are enforceable** in courts and administrative bodies.
- The **conditions for registration and ownership** are similar to those in Canada (e.g., ownership by the inventor or creator, and rights to prevent unauthorized reproduction).

---

### **Who Is Covered by This Certification?**

The certification applies to:
1. **Canadian nationals** – citizens of Canada.
2. **Individuals or legal entities** that have a **real and effective establishment in Canada** for:
   - The **creation** of topographies (designing the layout of integrated circuits), or  
   - The **manufacture** of integrated circuit products.

This means that if a Canadian-based company or inventor develops or manufactures integrated circuits in Canada, they can **claim protection in Switzerland** under the same conditions as if Switzerland had a direct agreement with Canada.

### **Significance and Implications**

1. **Facilitates International IP Enforcement**  
   Canadian creators and manufacturers can pursue legal remedies in Switzerland for infringement of topography rights, without needing to go through a full international treaty or bilateral agreement.

2. **Supports Trade and Innovation**  
   The certification strengthens Canada’s ability to protect its intellectual property in key technology markets, encouraging investment and innovation in the semiconductor industry.

3. **Promotes Reciprocity**  
   It reflects a broader policy of **reciprocal protection** between Canada and other countries with similar IP frameworks, especially in high-tech industries.

4. **Legal Foundation for Cross-Border Litigation**  
   The certification provides a legal basis for Canadian courts or IP offices to recognize and enforce topography rights in Switzerland, and vice versa.

### **Historical Context**

- The *Integrated Circuit Topography Act* was enacted in 1990 (S.C. 1990, c. 37) to protect the design of integrated circuits — a critical area in modern electronics and semiconductors.
- The 1993 amendment (S.C. 1993, c. 15, s. 25) introduced the power to certify foreign jurisdictions with equivalent protection.
- The certification of Switzerland on **January 11, 1994** was part of a broader effort to establish international IP protections in the rapidly growing electronics sector.

---

### **Conclusion**

This regulation is a **formal, legally binding certification** that Switzerland meets the threshold of providing **substantially equivalent protection** for integrated circuit topographies under Canadian law. It enables Canadian inventors and businesses with a real and effective presence in Canada to **protect their topography rights in Switzerland**, reinforcing international intellectual property cooperation and supporting innovation in the semiconductor industry.

---

**Note:** This certification remains valid unless revoked or amended by the Minister, and its continued relevance depends on ongoing compliance with the standards of protection set out in the Act.
