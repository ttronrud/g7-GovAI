### **1. Purpose of the Regulation**
The primary objective of these regulations is to **ensure the safety of science education sets** used in schools, homes, and educational institutions. These sets are designed to teach scientific concepts through hands-on experiments involving chemicals. The regulation sets strict limits on the types and quantities of hazardous chemicals that can be included in such sets, with the goal of **protecting children and users from exposure to dangerous substances**.

These rules apply specifically to **science education sets** and their **replacement chemicals**, ensuring that they are safe for educational use, especially when children may be involved.

---

### **2. Key Definitions**

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Science Education Set** | A collection of products, materials, or substances containing a *chemical product* (as defined in the *Consumer Chemicals and Containers Regulations, 2001*) intended to instruct users about a scientific concept. Examples include chemistry kits, biology experiment kits, etc. |
| **Replacement Chemical** | A chemical product used to restock or refill a science education set. It must meet the same safety standards as the original set. |
| **Oral LD50 Value** | The single dose of a substance administered orally to animals that is expected to cause death in at least 50% of the test population. It is a measure of toxicity. A lower value indicates higher toxicity. |

---

### **3. Prohibited Chemicals in Science Education Sets**

A chemical **must not be included** in a science education set if:

- It is **listed in the schedule** (i.e., specifically prohibited);
- Its **oral LD50 value is equal to or less than 50 mg/kg** of body weight in an animal test (indicating high toxicity);
- It is **required in any experiment** described in the instructions of the set or its replacement chemical.

### **5. Packaging Requirements**

All chemicals in science education sets and replacement chemicals must be packaged in containers that meet the requirements of the *Consumer Chemicals and Containers Regulations, 2001*, including:

- Proper labeling in both **English and French**;
- Clear, prominent, and contrasting labeling that stands out from other information;
- Readily visible to consumers at the time of purchase or during use.

Additionally, the container must display:

- The **common name** of the chemical or its name as accepted by the **International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC)**;
- If the oral LD50 is between **50 mg/kg and 2000 mg/kg**, the **hazard symbol** as specified in Schedule 2, Item 1, Column 2 of the *Consumer Chemicals and Containers Regulations*, 2001 â€” applied according to rules in paragraphs 17(b), and sections 18, 21, 22, 25, and 26 of those regulations;

### **7. Prohibited Experiments and Procedures**

Science education sets **must not contain or display** any instruction, description, or wording that suggests or promotes:

- Cultivating or demonstrating the presence of **pathogenic microorganisms** (e.g., bacteria, viruses that can cause disease);
- Cultivating any microorganism that may be present in or come from a product or substance that could contain **pathogenic organisms**;
- Demonstrating the presence of any microorganism in such substances.

> âŒ This restriction is in place to **prevent the risk of disease transmission**, such as from bacteria like *Salmonella*, *E. coli*, or *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, which could be present in certain biological samples.

> ðŸš« Examples of prohibited activities:
> - Growing bacteria from raw meat or soil;
> - Using untreated water samples to grow microbes;
> - Demonstrating the growth of mold or fungi from potentially contaminated sources.

> âœ… This protects students and educators from biological hazards that could lead to illness.

### **9. Regulatory Enforcement and Effective Date**

- These regulations **come into force on the day of registration** (i.e., upon publication in the Canada Gazette).
- They are **binding on all manufacturers, distributors, and sellers** of science education sets in Canada.
- The regulations are enforced under the authority of the *Canada Consumer Product Safety Act*, meaning compliance is mandatory, and non-compliance can result in penalties.
