## ðŸ“Š Purpose and Scope

The regulation provides detailed rules to ensure **fair and accurate comparisons** between the **price of imported goods** and the **normal value** of like goods in the country of export. This is essential for determining whether goods are being dumped (sold below fair value), which may lead to anti-dumping or countervailing duties.

It applies specifically to:
- Determining **normal value** under Sections 15, 19, and 20 of the Act.
- Calculating **cost of production**, **profit**, and **administrative/selling costs**.
- Adjusting prices to reflect differences in **quantity, quality, delivery, discounts, taxes, and sales conditions**.

## ðŸ”„ Price Adjustment Rules (for Determining Normal Value)

These rules ensure that the price of "like goods" in the country of export reflects the **true market value** of goods sold to the Canadian importer â€” accounting for commercial differences.

### 1. **Quantity Discounts**
- If the importer bought in **larger quantities**, the price of like goods must be adjusted to reflect the **discount** normally granted in such bulk sales.
- If the discount cannot be determined:
  - **Add** the estimated cost that the exporter would incur to sell in those quantities.
  - **Subtract** the estimated savings the exporter would gain from such a sale.

### 2. **Differences in Quality, Design, Warranty, Delivery, or Conditions of Sale**
If the goods sold to Canada differ from like goods in the country of export in any of the following ways:
- Quality, structure, design, or material
- Warranty or performance guarantee
- Time between order and shipment
- Other sales conditions (excluding standard ones)

ðŸ‘‰ Then the price of like goods must be adjusted:
- **If the price is higher**: Deduct the estimated difference.
- **If the price is lower**: Add the estimated difference.

> *This ensures that differences in product features donâ€™t distort the comparison.*

### 3. **Rebates, Deferred Discounts, or Cash Discounts**
- If such discounts are **commonly granted** in the country of export, they must be **deducted** from the price of like goods **if the sale to Canada would qualify** for them.

### 4. **Delivered Price Adjustments**
- If like goods are sold at a **delivered price**, deduct the **cost of delivery** to the buyer.
- If like goods are generally sold at a **common delivered price** within a **transportation zone** (e.g., a region where shipping costs are standardized), deduct the **average delivery cost** to destinations within that zone.

### 5. **Substitution of Purchasers**
- If buyers **one level below** the importer (e.g., wholesalers instead of retailers) have been substituted:
  - Deduct costs incurred due to **additional activities** required to serve those lower-tier buyers.
  - If no data exists, deduct **no more than the typical discount** offered to similar-tier buyers in the country of export.

### 6. **Taxes and Duties**
- If the **importer bears no taxes/duties** on the goods (but the like goods in the country of export do), deduct those **taxes/duties** from the price of like goods.

## ðŸ­ Cost of Production (Section 11 & 11.1)

### General Rule:
Cost of production = All costs:
- Attributable to or related to the **production** of the goods, or
- Directly attributable to the **design or engineering** of the goods.

### Special Case: Input Costs from Associated Persons
If an input is purchased from an **associated person** (e.g., a related company), and is a **significant factor** in production:

> The cost is taken as the **greater of**:
1. The price paid by the exporter or producer to the associated person,
2. The cost incurred by the associated person in producing the input (including administrative, selling, and other costs),
3. The **market price** of the same or similar input in the country of export â€” if sufficient data exists from:
   - Non-associated sales in the same or similar quantities, or
   - Published market prices.

### Exception: Market Conditions Preventing Proper Comparison
If the President determines that a **market situation** prevents a fair comparison (e.g., lack of transparency, monopoly), then the input cost is adjusted to reflect **actual cost** using one of these options:
1. Price of inputs sold to exporters or producers in the country of export.
2. Price of inputs sold from the country of export to a third country.
3. Published prices in the country of export.
4. Price of inputs produced in a third country, **adjusted** for price differences between countries.
5. Published prices outside the country of export, **adjusted** for comparability.
