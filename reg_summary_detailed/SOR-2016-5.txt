**Detailed Summary of the North American Foreign Ministers Meeting 2016 — Privileges and Immunities Order**

---

**1. Legal Authority and Issuance**

This regulation is issued under the authority of the *Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act* (FMOA), specifically pursuant to **paragraph 5(1)(c)a** of that Act. This paragraph empowers the Governor General in Council (acting on the recommendation of the Minister of Foreign Affairs) to grant privileges and immunities to representatives of foreign states participating in international meetings or conferences held in Canada.

The Order is formally published in the *Canada Gazette*, and its legal basis is cited as:
- **S.C. 2002, c. 12, s. 3(2)** – which provides the enabling power for the issuance of such orders.
- **S.C. 1991, c. 41** – the original enactment of the Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act.

---

**2. Purpose and Scope of the Order**

The primary purpose of this Order is to confer upon participating foreign government representatives the **privileges and immunities** necessary for them to carry out their official duties during the **North American Foreign Ministers Meeting (NAFMM)**, which took place in **Québec City on January 28 and 29, 2016**.

The Order applies specifically to:
- Representatives of foreign states who are attending or participating in the Meeting.
- The privileges and immunities granted are limited to the **extent required for the exercise of their functions in Canada** in connection with the Meeting.

---

**3. Definition of Key Term**

- **"Meeting"** is defined as the **intergovernmental conference known as the North American Foreign Ministers Meeting**, held in Québec City on January 28 and 29, 2016.  
  This meeting brought together foreign ministers from North American countries to discuss regional cooperation, security, trade, and other shared policy issues.

---

**4. Privileges and Immunities Granted**

The Order grants participating foreign ministers the following rights and protections, drawn from the **Convention on the Privileges and Immunities of the United Nations**, specifically:

- **Sections 11(b) and (e)** of **Article IV** of the Convention  
- **Sections 14 to 16** of Article IV of the Convention

These provisions are detailed in **Schedule III** of the *Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act*, which sets out the full text of the UN Convention.

#### Breakdown of the Privileges and Immunities:

- **Section 11(b)**:  
  Grants **immunity from civil and criminal jurisdiction** in Canada for acts performed in the course of official duties related to the Meeting. This means that participating ministers cannot be prosecuted or sued in Canadian courts for actions taken in their official capacity during the Meeting.

- **Section 11(e)**:  
  Provides **immunity from service of process** (such as legal subpoenas or court orders) in Canada for acts performed in connection with the Meeting.

- **Section 14**:  
  Confers **immunity from search, seizure, or interference** with personal property or communications (such as mail, documents, or electronic data) during the performance of official duties.

- **Section 15**:  
  Grants **immunity from interference with official communications**, including telephone, email, or other forms of communication used in the course of their official functions.

- **Section 16**:  
  Provides **immunity from liability for damages** arising from acts performed in the exercise of their official duties during the Meeting.

> These protections are **limited in scope** and apply only to actions taken **in the course of their official duties** related to the Meeting. They do not extend to personal conduct or private activities.

---

**5. Limitations and Conditions**

- The privileges and immunities are **not absolute** and are strictly limited to the **functions necessary for the conduct of the Meeting**.
- They are **not granted to non-participating officials** or to individuals attending as observers or non-ministerial delegates.
- The rights do not extend to the use of Canadian territory for purposes unrelated to the Meeting.
- The privileges are **temporary**, effective only during the dates of the Meeting (January 28–29, 2016).

---

**6. Effective Date**

The Order comes into force on the **day it is registered** in the *Canada Gazette*. This is the standard mechanism in Canadian law for the activation of statutory instruments, ensuring that the legal effects of the Order are known and enforceable upon publication.

---

**7. Legal Significance**

This Order is a **temporary, specific application** of international law principles to a particular intergovernmental event. It reflects Canada’s commitment to international norms and diplomatic practice by ensuring that foreign ministers can operate freely and without fear of legal interference when performing their official duties during multilateral meetings.

It also demonstrates how Canada implements the **principles of diplomatic immunity** under international law through domestic legislative mechanisms, consistent with the UN Convention and Canada’s international obligations.

---

**Conclusion**

The *North American Foreign Ministers Meeting 2016 — Privileges and Immunities Order* is a targeted, legally grounded instrument that ensures the smooth and effective functioning of a high-level diplomatic gathering. By granting temporary immunity and protection to foreign ministers attending the Meeting in Québec, it supports diplomatic engagement, promotes open dialogue, and upholds international norms governing diplomatic conduct. The Order is both a practical and symbolic affirmation of Canada’s role in North American regional cooperation and international diplomacy.

--- 

*Note: This Order is not a permanent or general grant of privileges. It is a specific, time-bound measure applicable only to the 2016 Meeting.*
