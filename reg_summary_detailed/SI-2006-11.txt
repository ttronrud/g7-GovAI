### **Detailed Summary of the Regulation**

**Title of Regulation:**  
Transfer of Control and Powers, and Amalgamation of Departments under the Public Service Rearrangement and Transfer of Duties Act

**Issuing Authority:**  
Her Excellency the Governor General in Council, on the recommendation of the Prime Minister.

**Legal Basis:**  
The regulation is enacted pursuant to **paragraph 2(a)a** and **paragraph 2(b)** of the *Public Service Rearrangement and Transfer of Duties Act* (S.C. 2003, c. 22, s. 207).

---

### **Key Provisions and Actions Taken (Effective February 6, 2006):**

#### 1. **Transfer of Control and Supervision**
- The **control and supervision** of all portions of the federal public administration previously managed by the **Department of International Trade (DIT)** are **transferred** to the **Department of Foreign Affairs and International Trade (DFAT)**.
- This means that DFAT assumes responsibility for overseeing all administrative functions, operations, policies, and personnel within the former DIT.

#### 2. **Transfer of Powers, Duties, and Functions**
- The **powers, duties, and functions** previously held by the **Minister for International Trade** are transferred to the **Minister of Foreign Affairs**.
- These powers and responsibilities stem from **paragraph (b) of Order in Council P.C. 2003-2049**, dated December 12, 2003.
- This includes functions related to international trade policy, trade agreements, trade promotion, and other trade-related responsibilities that were previously under the purview of the Minister for International Trade.

#### 3. **Amalgamation of Departments**
- The **Department of International Trade (DIT)** and the **Department of Foreign Affairs and International Trade (DFAT)** are **amalgamated and combined** into a single, unified department.
- This new department operates under the **Minister of Foreign Affairs** and is led by the **Deputy Minister of Foreign Affairs**.
- The amalgamation aims to streamline and consolidate trade and foreign affairs functions under one department, improving coordination, efficiency, and policy coherence.

---

### **Effective Date**
- All of the above changes take effect on **February 6, 2006**.

---

### **Purpose and Rationale (Implied from Context)**
- The regulation reflects a broader government initiative to **eliminate duplication**, **enhance coordination**, and **improve the effectiveness** of Canada’s foreign policy and international trade strategy.
- By merging the two departments, the government seeks to:
  - Ensure that trade policy is aligned with broader foreign policy objectives.
  - Reduce bureaucratic overlap and administrative costs.
  - Strengthen the capacity of the Department of Foreign Affairs and International Trade to manage both diplomatic and trade-related responsibilities in a unified and strategic manner.

---

### **Significance**
- This regulation marks a significant structural change in the Canadian federal government’s administrative framework.
- It represents a shift from a dual-department model (separate departments for foreign affairs and international trade) to a **single, integrated department** responsible for both foreign relations and international trade.
- It also reflects a strategic realignment of ministerial responsibilities, placing trade policy under the broader authority of the Minister of Foreign Affairs.

---

### **Conclusion**
This regulation effectively **reorganizes** the federal public administration by:
1. Transferring control of the Department of International Trade to the Department of Foreign Affairs and International Trade,
2. Transferring the Minister for International Trade’s powers to the Minister of Foreign Affairs,
3. Amalgamating the two departments into a single entity under the Minister of Foreign Affairs and Deputy Minister of Foreign Affairs.

The change, effective February 6, 2006, consolidates trade and foreign affairs functions to improve policy coherence, administrative efficiency, and strategic decision-making in Canada’s international engagements.
