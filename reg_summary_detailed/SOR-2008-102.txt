**Detailed Summary of the Conference of Defence Ministers of the Americas (CDMA) Privileges and Immunities Order, 2008**

---

### **1. Legal Basis and Authority**
The regulation is issued under the authority of **section 5a of the *Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act* (FMOA)**.  
- It is made by **Her Excellency the Governor General in Council**, acting on the **recommendation of the Minister of Foreign Affairs**.  
- This reflects the Canadian government’s practice of granting privileges and immunities to international organizations and their representatives through delegated legislative power.

This Order specifically establishes the **privileges and immunities** for representatives of member states participating in the **Conference of Defence Ministers of the Americas (CDMA)**.

---

### **2. Applicable Definitions**
The Order includes the following key definitions to ensure clarity and consistency:

- **Convention**: Refers to the *Convention on the Privileges and Immunities of the United Nations*, as set out in **Schedule III** of the *Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act*.  
  - This is a foundational international treaty that provides a legal framework for the privileges and immunities of UN missions and officials.  
  - The CDMA Order applies these principles to a non-UN intergovernmental body.

- **Meeting**: The official meeting of the CDMA scheduled to take place in **Banff, Alberta**, from **September 2 to 6, 2008**.

- **Organization**: The **Conference of Defence Ministers of the Americas (CDMA)** — an intergovernmental forum composed of defense ministers from North American and Caribbean countries, aimed at enhancing cooperation in defense and security matters.

- **Preparatory Meeting**: The planning and preparatory session of the CDMA held in **Banff, Alberta**, from **May 14 to 16, 2008**.

---

### **3. Scope of Privileges and Immunities**
The Order grants representatives of member states of the CDMA the following **privileges and immunities** in Canada during two specific time periods:

#### **Period 1: May 11 – May 19, 2008**  
- Covers the period leading up to and including the **Preparatory Meeting**.

#### **Period 2: August 31 – September 9, 2008**  
- Covers the period leading up to and including the **Meeting**.

During these periods, representatives of member states will have in Canada the **same privileges and immunities** as those provided under **Sections 11, 12, and 14 to 16 of Article IV of the UN Convention**.

These specific sections of the Convention include:

- **Section 11**: Immunity from civil and criminal jurisdiction (except for certain offences such as fraud, theft, or acts committed in bad faith).
- **Section 12**: Immunity from arrest and detention, and from service of process.
- **Sections 14–16**: Immunity from search, seizure, and interference with personal property; protection of official communications and documents; and immunity from taxation and other financial obligations.

> ⚠️ **Important Note**: These privileges and immunities are **limited to the exercise of official functions** related to the Preparatory Meeting and the Meeting. They do not extend to private activities or personal conduct.

---

### **4. Purpose and Rationale**
The Order enables the CDMA to operate effectively in Canada by:

- Ensuring that defense ministers and their staff can carry out their official duties without undue interference from Canadian authorities.
- Facilitating diplomatic and security-related discussions in a neutral and secure environment.
- Providing legal protection for sensitive information, communications, and official documents.

This is consistent with international norms where intergovernmental meetings — particularly those involving defense and security — are granted formal privileges and immunities to promote open dialogue and prevent diplomatic friction.

---

### **5. Legal Citation and References**
- The Order is referenced in relation to:
  - **S.C. 2002, c. 12, s. 3 and 10**: Likely relates to the enabling provisions of the *Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act* regarding the granting of privileges.
  - **S.C. 1991, c. 41**: Refers to the original enactment of the FMOA, which provides the legislative foundation for such orders.

These references confirm that the Order is within the scope of existing Canadian law and is not an innovation beyond established legal principles.

---

### **6. Effective Date**
- The Order **comes into force on the day it is registered** with the *Canada Gazette*.  
- This is standard practice in Canadian regulatory law, ensuring immediate applicability upon official publication.

---

### **7. Key Limitations and Conditions**
- The privileges and immunities are **not absolute**.
- They are **strictly limited** to the performance of official duties related to the Preparatory Meeting and the Meeting.
- They **do not apply** to:
  - Personal activities or private conduct.
  - Violations of Canadian law unrelated to official functions (e.g., criminal acts, fraud, or illegal activities).
- Canadian authorities may still enforce laws if a representative is found to have committed a crime outside the scope of official duties.

---

### **8. Significance**
This Order is a **practical application of international diplomatic law** in Canada. It demonstrates:
- Canada’s commitment to multilateral cooperation in defense and security.
- The adaptability of the UN Convention’s framework to non-UN intergovernmental bodies.
- The importance of legal clarity and consistency in diplomatic operations.

It also reflects Canada’s role as a host country for regional security dialogues and its willingness to support international cooperation through legal and institutional frameworks.

---

### **Conclusion**
The *Conference of Defence Ministers of the Americas (CDMA) Privileges and Immunities Order, 2008* provides a clear, legally grounded, and internationally aligned set of protections for CDMA representatives during two key meetings in Banff, Alberta. By drawing upon the UN Convention on Privileges and Immunities, the Order ensures that defense ministers can operate freely and securely in Canada, promoting effective regional security dialogue. The scope is strictly time-bound and function-specific, maintaining balance between diplomatic needs and Canadian legal sovereignty.
