**Detailed Summary of the Cellulose Fibre Insulation Regulations (S.C. 2010, c. 21)**

---

### **Overview**
The *Cellulose Fibre Insulation Regulations*, made under Section 37 of the *Canada Consumer Product Safety Act* (CCPSA), establish specific safety and labeling requirements for cellulose fibre insulation used in buildings. These regulations were introduced to ensure that cellulose fibre insulation products meet consistent, high-quality safety standards‚Äîparticularly in terms of fire resistance, structural integrity, and material stability‚Äîbefore being sold to consumers in Canada.

The regulations apply to **loose-fill cellulose fibre insulation** used in the **interior of new and existing buildings**.

---

### **Key Definitions**

1. **Cellulose Fibre Insulation**  
   - Refers to cellulose fibre specifically manufactured and intended for use as **loose-fill thermal insulation** within building walls, attics, or other interior spaces.  
   - Excludes other types of insulation (e.g., fiberglass, rock wool) and applications outside of building interiors.

2. **Insulation Standard**  
   - The primary performance benchmark is **CAN/ULC-S703**, titled *Standard for Cellulose Fibre Insulation for Buildings*.  
   - This standard is **subject to periodic amendments**; any updates to the standard are automatically incorporated into these regulations.

---

### **Performance and Safety Requirements**

#### 1. **Critical Radiant Flux Test (Fire Resistance)**
- **Requirement**:  
  When tested according to **Section 6.3.9** of CAN/ULC-S703, **all three test specimens** must have a **critical radiant flux (CRF) ‚â• 0.12 W/cm¬≤**.  
  - *Critical radiant flux* measures how well a material resists ignition under fire exposure. A higher value indicates better fire resistance.

- **Exception (if one specimen fails)**:  
  If **only one** of the three test specimens has a CRF **less than 0.12 W/cm¬≤**, then **three additional specimens** must be tested.  
  - All **three of these new specimens** must meet the CRF requirement (‚â• 0.12 W/cm¬≤).  
  - This ensures that even if a single test fails, the product still meets the required fire performance threshold.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è This rule enhances product reliability by reducing the risk of a product failing due to a single flawed sample.

---

#### 2. **Flaming Combustion and Mass Loss**
- **No flaming combustion** is allowed under **Sections 6.3.3 and 6.3.12** of the standard.  
  - The product must not ignite or sustain a flame when exposed to a controlled fire source.
- **Mass loss limit**:  
  The insulation must not lose more than **15% of its original mass** during testing.  
  - This ensures that the material does not degrade significantly under heat, which could compromise insulation effectiveness or structural integrity.

> üî• These requirements are critical for ensuring occupant safety in case of fire.

---

#### 3. **Separation of Non-Cellulosic Components**
- The insulation must not exhibit **any separation of non-cellulosic components** exceeding **1.5% by mass** when tested per **Section 6.3.11**.  
  - Non-cellulosic components may include binders, additives, or other materials added during manufacturing.  
  - Excessive separation could indicate poor material cohesion, leading to reduced performance or potential release of harmful substances.

> üí° This ensures the product remains structurally sound and chemically stable over time.

---

#### 4. **Perforation of Metal Components**
- If a test specimen contains **aluminum, copper, or steel**, the cellulose fibre insulation must **not cause perforation** when tested under **Section 6.3.2**.  
  - This refers to the insulation‚Äôs ability to maintain integrity when in contact with metal components (e.g., ducts, framing).  
  - Perforation could lead to air leakage, reduced thermal performance, or potential fire hazards.

> üõ†Ô∏è This requirement protects both the building envelope and safety systems involving metal elements.

---

### **Labeling and Consumer Information Requirements**

Cellulose fibre insulation sold to consumers must clearly display the following information on its **container**, or, if no container exists, on **packaging**, **bill of sale**, or **other document provided at time of sale**:

1. **Name and Principal Place of Business in Canada** of:
   - The **manufacturer**, if the product is made in Canada; **or**
   - The **importer**, if the product is imported.

2. **Date of Manufacture**:
   - The **day, month, and year** when the insulation was manufactured.

> ‚úÖ The information must be:
> - **Legible**
> - **Prominently displayed**
> - Easily accessible to consumers

> üì¶ Example: A label on a bulk container might read:  
> *"Manufactured by: GreenWall Insulation Inc. ‚Äì 123 Oak Street, Toronto, ON M5V 3L7 ‚Äì Manufactured on: June 15, 2024"*

> ‚ùå Failure to provide this information constitutes a violation of the regulations.

---

### **Repeal Clause**
- The phrase "[Repeal]" appears in the regulation, indicating that **prior or conflicting regulations** related to cellulose fibre insulation are being **replaced or withdrawn** by these new rules.  
- This ensures legal clarity and prevents confusion from outdated provisions.

---

### **Enforcement and Effective Date**

- **Effective Date**:  
  The regulations come into force on the **day they are registered** in the Canada Gazette (official publication).  
  - Registration date is typically shortly after approval by the Governor in Council, based on the Minister of Health‚Äôs recommendation.

- **Enforcement Authority**:  
  The regulations are enforced by the **Consumer Product Safety Act**, with oversight from the **Canadian Consumer Product Safety Act (CCPSA)** and its enforcement body, the **Consumer Product Safety Directorate (CPSC)**.

---

### **Purpose and Public Benefit**

These regulations aim to:
- Protect public health and safety by ensuring that cellulose fibre insulation performs reliably in real-world building environments.
- Promote product consistency and quality across manufacturers and importers.
- Provide transparency to consumers through clear labeling.
- Align Canadian standards with internationally recognized safety benchmarks (e.g., ULC standards).
