## üìö **Overview of the Agreement**

This is a **bilateral social security agreement** between **Canada and Italy**, signed originally in **Toronto on November 17, 1977**, and updated through a formal entry into force on **October 1, 2017**. The agreement establishes rules for the **recognition, portability, and calculation of social security benefits** (such as pensions, survivor benefits, and family allowances) for individuals who have worked or lived in both countries.

It is governed by the **Old Age Security Act (OAS Act)** and the **Canada Pension Plan (CPP)** in Canada, and by Italy‚Äôs social security legislation, particularly its mandatory insurance schemes for employees, self-employed workers, and family benefits.

The agreement entered into force on **October 1, 2017**, following mutual notification from both governments that they had fulfilled all domestic statutory requirements under their respective laws.

## üîç Key Provisions and Detailed Summary

### 1. **Entry into Force**
- The Agreement entered into force on **October 1, 2017**.
- This date follows the **first day of the fourth month after the month** in which each party received written notification from the other confirming compliance with domestic legal requirements.
- The notification was exchanged in **June 2017**, leading to the April 2017‚ÄìOctober 2017 timeline.
- The Order in Council (P.C. 2017-1064) was issued on **August 14, 2017**, and formally proclaimed on **September 14, 2017**.

> ‚úÖ **Key Takeaway**: The agreement is not retroactive; it only applies to individuals and situations occurring *on or after* October 1, 2017.

### 2. **Scope of Application**

#### For **Italy**:
Applies to:
- General compulsory insurance for employees (disability, old age, survivors)
- Special schemes for self-employed workers (related to the risks covered under employee schemes)
- Compulsory insurance for tuberculosis
- Family benefits for pension beneficiaries

#### For **Canada**:
Applies to:
- The **Old Age Security Act (OAS)** and its regulations
- The **Canada Pension Plan (CPP)** and its regulations

> ‚úÖ **Note**: The agreement also applies to **amendments** or **extensions** of these laws‚Äî**provided** no objection is raised by one party within **three months** of the amendment‚Äôs enactment.

> ‚ùå **Exception**: Extensions to new beneficiary categories are only valid if no objection is made within 3 months of entry into force.

### 3. **Who Is Covered?**

The agreement applies to:
- Any person who has **worked or resided** in either country
- Their **dependants and survivors** (spouses, children, etc.), as defined under applicable legislation

> ‚úÖ **Core Principle**: A person who is or has been subject to the social security legislation of either country is eligible for benefits from the other country **under the same conditions as a citizen of that country**.

---

### 4. **Benefit Portability and Payment Rules**

#### ‚úÖ Benefits Are Paid in the Territory of the Other Party
- Unless otherwise restricted, benefits acquired under one country‚Äôs system are **paid in the territory of the other party**.
- **No reduction, suspension, or cancellation** of benefits due solely to the fact that the beneficiary resides in the other country.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è Exception: Benefits for persons who are subject to both countries‚Äô legislation are paid in a **third state** (e.g., if someone lives in a third country, such as the U.S.).

### 5. **Residency and Work Status Rules**

#### A. Employed Persons
- An employed person working in the territory of one country is subject **only** to the legislation of that country.
- **Exception**: If the employee is assigned by employer to work in the other country for **up to 24 months**, they are subject only to the legislation of the **first country** (where employment began).
- This 24-month period **can be extended** if both competent authorities agree that the reasons justify it (e.g., long-term assignments, project-based work).

#### B. Self-Employed Persons
- A self-employed person who works in both countries and has a permanent home in one country is subject **only** to the legislation of that country (the "first Party").
- **Residency Rule**: A person is deemed to reside in the country where they have a **permanent home**.
- If homes exist in both countries, residency is determined by **centre of vital interests** (e.g., family, work, personal life).

#### C. Government Employment
- A person working in government employment (e.g., public agencies, military, police) in one country is subject **only** to the legislation of that country, even if assigned to the other country.
- **Exception**:
  - A person who **ordinarily resides in Canada** and is hired by an Italian public agency is subject to **Canada‚Äôs** legislation, **unless** they are a **citizen of Italy**, in which case they may **choose** to be covered by Italy.
  - A person who **ordinarily resides in Italy** and is hired by a Canadian government entity may **choose** to be covered by Canada if they are a **citizen of Canada**.

> ‚è±Ô∏è **Election Deadline**: Any such election must be made **within 3 months of the start of employment** or, for those already employed on October 1, 2017, **within 3 months of that date**.

### 6. **Special Cases ‚Äì Maritime and Diplomatic Staff**

#### A. Seagoing Crew Members
- If a ship flies the **Italian flag**, the crew member is subject **only to Italy‚Äôs** legislation.
- If the ship flies the **Canadian flag**, the crew member is subject **only to Canada‚Äôs** legislation.

#### B. Flight Crews (International Airlines)
- A flight crew member is subject to the legislation of the **country where the airline has its head office**.
- **Exception**: If the crew member is a **permanent resident** of the other country, they may **choose** to be covered by that country‚Äôs legislation.

#### C. Diplomatic and Consular Staff
- The **Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (1961)** and **Vienna Convention on Consular Relations (1963)** govern the social security coverage of:
  - Private servants of diplomats
  - Members of private staff of consular officials

> ‚úÖ These rules **supersede** the general provisions of the agreement for these categories.

### 7. **Creditable Periods and Periods of Residence (Key to Benefit Entitlement)**

#### What Are Creditable Periods?
- A period of **contributions** or **residence** used to qualify for a pension or allowance.
- In Italy: includes periods deemed as contributions under Italian law.
- In Canada: includes periods during which a disability pension is payable under the CPP.

> üîó **Totalization Rules** (crucial for pension eligibility):

|-------|-------------------|
| **OAS Act (Old Age Security)** | A period of residence in Italy during which a person was covered by CPP or provincial pension plans counts as **residence in Canada**. |

| **CPP (Canada Pension Plan)** | A calendar year with **at least 13 weeks** of creditable periods under Italian law counts as **one year of CPP contributions**. |
| **Italian Legislation** | A calendar year of CPP contributions counts as **52 weeks** of Italian contributions. A week of OAS creditable periods (not in CPP) counts as **one week** of Italian contributions. |

> üìå **Totalization Principle**: If a person has not completed the required minimum periods under one country‚Äôs system, the **total periods** from both countries are combined to determine eligibility.

### 8. **Eligibility and Benefit Calculation**

#### A. When Totalization Applies
- If a person is **not entitled** to a benefit due to insufficient periods under one country, entitlement is determined by **totalizing**:
  - Creditable periods under Canada and Italy
  - Creditable periods under a **third country** (if both Canada and Italy have a social security agreement with that country)

> ‚úÖ Totalization applies **only** if no right exists under one country‚Äôs legislation alone.

#### B. Minimum Period Requirement
- If the total creditable periods (from both countries) are **less than one year (52 weeks)**, and no right exists under either country‚Äôs law:
  - The competent institution **does not have to pay benefits** for those periods.
  - However, those periods **are still considered** when determining entitlement to benefits under the **other country‚Äôs legislation**.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è This prevents "partial credit" from being used to justify full benefits.
