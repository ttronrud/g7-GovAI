The regulation you provided is a **provincial tax prescription regulation** under the **Excise Tax Act** of Ontario, issued by His Excellency the Governor General in Council on the recommendation of the Minister of Finance. #### b) *Specified Tax Rate of a Province*
This is the **maximum tax rate** that may be imposed on certain goods or services, calculated as:

> **Specified Tax Rate = Max(12%, General Sales Tax Rate + 4%)**

This ensures that **no single provincial tax (on goods/services) exceeds 12% above the base rate**, and that **the total of all such percentage-based taxes does not exceed this cap**.

> Example:  
> If Manitoba‚Äôs general sales tax is 8%, then the specified tax rate is 12% (since 8% + 4% = 12%).  
> If the general rate is 10%, the specified rate is still 14% ‚Üí capped at 12% ‚Üí so the specified tax rate is **12%**.

Thus, **the specified tax rate acts as a ceiling** to prevent excessive taxation on goods and services.

#### ‚úÖ Category 2: Percentage-Based Taxes on Goods or Services (imposed by provincial Acts)

A tax is included if it meets **all of the following conditions**:

1. The tax is calculated as a **percentage of the value or price** of the property or service.  
2. The tax is **payable by the recipient** of the supply (not the seller).  
3. The tax is **not included** in the value or price used to calculate any other tax.  
4. The **sum of all such percentage-based taxes** under the provincial Act **does not exceed the specified tax rate of the province**.

> üîç This provision ensures **tax compatibility** ‚Äî no province can impose multiple percentage-based taxes that collectively exceed the specified tax rate (e.g., 12% or general rate + 4%).

> Example:  
> If a province has a general sales tax of 8%, the specified tax rate is 12%.  
> If a service is taxed at 5% under one law and 6% under another, total = 11% ‚Üí allowed.  
> If total = 13% ‚Üí **not allowed**.

> This prevents **tax cascading** and ensures **provincial tax systems remain within a defined limit**.
