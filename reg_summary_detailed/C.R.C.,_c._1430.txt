**Detailed Summary of the *Home-Trade, Inland and Minor Waters Voyages Regulations***

---

### **1. Regulation Title and Citation**
The regulations are formally titled:  
**"Home-Trade, Inland and Minor Waters Voyages Regulations"**  
They may be cited as such and serve as a legal framework for classifying and certifying steamships operating in specific domestic maritime routes within Canada.

---

### **2. Definition of "Miles"**
- The term **"miles"** used throughout the regulations refers to **international nautical miles**, each equal to **1,852 meters**.  
- This standardization ensures consistency in measuring distances for voyage classification and safety assessments.

---

### **3. Classification of Voyages**
The regulations divide voyages into **eight distinct classes** based on geographic scope, route safety, distance from shore, and risk level. These classes are:

| Class | Type of Voyage |
|------|----------------|
| 1 | Home-trade voyage, Class I |
| 2 | Home-trade voyage, Class II |
| 3 | Home-trade voyage, Class III |
| 4 | Home-trade voyage, Class IV |
| 5 | Inland voyage, Class I |
| 6 | Inland voyage, Class II |
| 7 | Minor waters voyage, Class I |
| 8 | Minor waters voyage, Class II |

Each class reflects a different level of risk, operational complexity, and navigational requirements.

---

### **4. Detailed Definitions of Each Voyage Class**

#### **A. Home-Trade Voyage, Class I**
- A voyage **within the limits of a home-trade voyage** as defined in the *Canada Shipping Act*.
- This is the broadest category, allowing steamships to travel anywhere within Canada’s designated home-trade zones (e.g., between major ports on the Atlantic or Pacific coasts).
- No specific restrictions on distance from shore or port spacing are imposed.

> *Purpose:* Allows flexibility for vessels operating across a wide range of domestic routes.

---

#### **B. Home-Trade Voyage, Class II**
- Must meet **all of the following conditions**:
  1. On the **Atlantic coast**, the vessel does **not go south of New York**.
  2. On the **Pacific coast**, the vessel does **not go south of Portland, Oregon**.
  3. The vessel remains **no more than 120 nautical miles offshore** at any time.
  4. The **distance between suitable ports of refuge** on the route does not exceed **200 nautical miles**.

> *Purpose:* Establishes a moderate-risk route with defined geographic boundaries and safety buffers. Designed for vessels operating in predictable, less remote areas.

---

#### **C. Home-Trade Voyage, Class III**
- Operates within the **limits specified in the vessel’s inspection certificate**.
- Conditions:
  1. On the **Atlantic coast**, does **not go south of New York**.
  2. On the **Pacific coast**, does **not go south of Portland, Oregon**.
  3. The vessel is **no more than 20 nautical miles offshore** at any time.
  4. The **maximum distance between suitable ports of refuge** on the route does **not exceed 100 nautical miles** at any point.

> *Purpose:* A safer, more restricted route intended for vessels with limited range and high navigational predictability. The tighter constraints reflect lower risk and greater safety margins.

> ⚠️ *Note:* Subject to **Section 7** of the regulations (which likely relates to safety exceptions or Board discretion).

---

#### **D. Home-Trade Voyage, Class IV**
- A voyage within **certain sheltered waters** as specified in the vessel’s inspection certificate.
- **Exception**: In **fine weather** and on **short voyages**, the vessel may go beyond these limits **between May 1 and September 30** of any year.

> *Purpose:* Designed for vessels operating in protected, low-risk environments (e.g., bays, estuaries, or lakes). The seasonal exception allows for limited expansion during favorable weather conditions.

> ⚠️ *Note:* This class is restricted to sheltered waters and is only valid under specific conditions.

---

#### **E. Inland Voyage, Class I**
- A voyage within the **limits of inland waters** as defined in the *Canada Shipping Act* (e.g., rivers, lakes, canals within Canada’s interior).
- No specific restrictions on distance from shore or refuge spacing.

> *Purpose:* Broad classification for inland navigation, including major rivers like the St. Lawrence or the Mississippi (within Canadian jurisdiction).

---

#### **F. Inland Voyage, Class II**
- Must meet:
  1. The vessel is **no more than 20 nautical miles offshore** at any time.
  2. The **maximum distance between suitable ports of refuge** on the route does **not exceed 100 nautical miles**.

> *Purpose:* A safer inland route with strict safety parameters. Intended for vessels operating in confined, predictable inland waterways.

> ⚠️ *Note:* Subject to **Section 7** (Board discretion for safety considerations).

---

#### **G. Minor Waters Voyage, Class I**
- A voyage within the **limits of minor waters** as defined in the *Canada Shipping Act* (e.g., small lakes, rivers, or coastal inlets).
- No specific distance or refuge restrictions beyond the defined boundaries.

> *Purpose:* For very small, low-risk waterways with minimal navigational complexity.

---

#### **H. Minor Waters Voyage, Class II**
- Operates in **specific lakes or rivers** specified in the vessel’s inspection certificate.
- The **maximum width** of the waterway must not exceed **two miles**.
- Alternatively, it may be a voyage within **certain sheltered waters** specified in the inspection certificate, with the **exception**:
  - In **fine weather** and on **short voyages**, the vessel may go beyond those limits **between May 1 and September 30**.
- **Exception Clause**:  
  If a lake or river has a **width exceeding two miles for a short distance only**, and the **Board determines it would be unreasonable** to classify such a voyage as Class I, the Board may **classify it as Class II**.

> *Purpose:* To allow for flexibility in small waterways while maintaining safety standards. The Board has discretion to assess risk and make exceptions.

> ⚠️ *Note:* This class is designed for very limited and low-risk operations.

---

### **5. Board Discretion and Risk Assessment (Section 7)**
- The **Board** (likely the *Board of Marine and Shipping*) has the authority to:
  - Evaluate the **degree of risk** associated with certain voyages.
  - Decide, **from time to time**, whether a voyage classified as:
    - Home-trade voyage, Class III  
    - Home-trade voyage, Class IV  
    - Inland voyage, Class II  
    - Minor waters voyage, Class II  
    should be **elevated to the next higher class** (e.g., Class II → Class I), if deemed safe and appropriate.

> *Purpose:* Provides flexibility to adapt classifications based on evolving operational conditions, vessel capabilities, or environmental factors. Ensures safety remains the primary concern.

---

### **6. Certification and Naming of Steamships**
- Steamships certified for specific voyage classes are given **official designations**:
  - **Home-trade steamships** (Class I, II, III, or IV)
  - **Inland steamships** (Class I or II)
  - **Minor waters steamships** (Class I or II)

> *Purpose:* Enables clear identification of vessel capabilities, regulatory compliance, and operational scope for licensing, insurance, and maritime oversight.

---

### **7. Key Principles and Objectives**
| Principle | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Risk-Based Classification** | Voyages are tiered based on risk (distance from shore, exposure, route complexity). |
| **Safety as Priority** | Strict limits on distance from shore and refuge spacing reflect safety concerns. |
| **Geographic Boundaries** | Clear demarcations (e.g., New York, Portland, 20-mile limits) ensure predictable operations. |
| **Flexibility with Oversight** | The Board retains authority to reassess classifications based on risk. |
| **Seasonal Exceptions** | Limited expansion allowed during favorable weather (May–Sept) in sheltered areas. |
| **Low-Risk Focus** | Minor waters and inland routes emphasize safety in confined, predictable environments. |

---

### **8. Regulatory Context**
These regulations operate under the **Canada Shipping Act**, which defines:
- Home-trade voyages
- Inland voyages
- Minor waters voyages

They provide the **operational framework** for classifying steamship voyages to determine eligibility for:
- Certification
- Insurance
- Regulatory oversight
- Licensing

They are particularly relevant for **pre-1970s Canadian maritime operations**, where steamships were common and safety standards were based on route risk and physical constraints.

---

The *Home-Trade, Inland and Minor Waters Voyages Regulations* establish a **structured, risk-based classification system** for domestic steamship voyages in Canada. The inclusion of **Board discretion** and **seasonal allowances** provides necessary flexibility while maintaining safety and operational integrity.
