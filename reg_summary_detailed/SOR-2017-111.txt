### **Detailed Summary of the Microbeads in Toiletries Regulations (Canada)**

The **Microbeads in Toiletries Regulations** are a set of federal regulations under the **Canadian Environmental Protection Act, 1999 (CEPA 1999)**, enacted to protect human health and the environment by banning the use of plastic microbeads in personal care products. These regulations were finalized and came into force on **January 1, 2018**, with specific provisions for implementation and compliance.

---

## **1. Legal Basis and Regulatory Process**

- **Authority**: The regulations were made under **subsection 93(1)** of the *Canadian Environmental Protection Act, 1999*, which allows the Governor in Council (i.e., the federal Cabinet) to adopt regulations to control substances that pose a risk to the environment or human health.
  
- **Proposal and Public Consultation**:
  - On **November 5, 2016**, the Minister of the Environment published a **proposed version** of the Microbeads in Toiletries Regulations in the *Canada Gazette, Part I*, initiating a public comment period.
  - Stakeholders (including industry, environmental groups, and the public) were given the opportunity to:
    - Submit written comments on the proposed regulations, or
    - File a **notice of objection** requesting a **board of review** to assess the regulation's validity, including reasons for objection.

- **Advisory Input**:
  - The **National Advisory Committee on Chemicals** (NAC) was consulted under **subsection 93(3)** of CEPA 1999 to provide expert advice on the scientific and environmental implications of regulating microbeads.

- **No Conflict with Other Legislation**:
  - The Governor in Council concluded, under **subsection 93(4)**, that the proposed regulations **do not duplicate or conflict** with existing environmental or health regulations. They provide **sufficient protection** to human health and the environment, thus justifying their adoption.

---

## **2. Definitions**

Two key terms are defined in the regulations:

- **Microbeads**:
  - Defined as the plastic microbeads listed in **Item 133 of Schedule 1 to CEPA 1999**, under the *List of Toxic Substances*.
  - These are typically **plastic particles smaller than 1 millimeter** (i.e., 1 mm or less), commonly used in exfoliating products like facial scrubs or toothpaste.
  - The term "microbilles" is the French equivalent used in the regulation.

- **Toiletries**:
  - Refers to any **personal care product** used for cleansing or hygiene, including:
    - Hair care (e.g., shampoos, conditioners)
    - Skin care (e.g., face washes, lotions)
    - Oral care (e.g., toothpaste, mouthwash)
    - Exfoliants (e.g., scrubs)
  - This definition explicitly includes products that contain exfoliating agents.

## **3. Key Prohibitions and Compliance Timeline**

### **Manufacturing and Importation**
- **Prohibition**: No person may **manufacture or import** toiletries containing microbeads.
- **Exception**: The ban applies **only to products that are not natural health products or non-prescription drugs**.
- **Effective Date for Exceptions**:
  - For **natural health products** or **non-prescription drugs**, the prohibition on microbeads:
    - Applies to **manufacturing and importation on or after July 1, 2018**.

### **Sale and Distribution**
- **Prohibition**: No person may **sell** toiletries containing microbeads.
- **Exception**: For natural health products or non-prescription drugs:
  - The ban on sale applies **on or after July 1, 2019**.

> ðŸ“Œ **Important Note**: The prohibition applies **only to products sold or distributed within Canada**. It does not apply to products **in transit** through Canada between foreign locations.

### **Exemption for Transit**
- **No contravention** occurs if toiletries containing microbeads are **in transit through Canada**, from a place **outside Canada to another place outside Canada**.
- This exemption supports international trade and prevents penalties for temporary movement of goods.

## **4. Laboratory Requirements for Testing**

To ensure scientific integrity and consistency in enforcement, the regulations specify requirements for **laboratory testing** to detect microbeads:

### **Accredited Laboratories Only**
- Any analysis to determine the presence of microbeads must be conducted by a laboratory that:
  1. Is **accredited** under:
     - The **ISO/IEC 17025** standard (General requirements for the competence of testing and calibration laboratories), **provided** that:
       - The accrediting body is a **signatory to the International Laboratory Accreditation Cooperation (ILAC) Mutual Recognition Arrangement** (a global recognition framework for lab accreditation), OR
     - The **Quebec Environmental Quality Act (CQLR, c. Q-2)** (a provincial standard applicable in Quebec).
  2. Has a **scope of accreditation** that specifically includes the analysis of microbeads.

## **5. Effective Date and Registration**

- The regulations **come into force on January 1, 2018**.
- However, if the regulations are **registered (officially published) after January 1, 2018**, they will take effect on the **date of registration**.

> This provision provides flexibility in the legislative process, allowing for delays due to administrative or legal review without undermining the original timeline.

---

## **6. Purpose and Environmental & Health Rationale**

- **Environmental Risk**: Microbeads, when washed down the drain, do not break down easily and can enter waterways, where they are ingested by aquatic organisms, leading to bioaccumulation and ecosystem disruption.
- **Human Health Risk**: While direct human exposure is low, microbeads can contribute to **microplastic pollution**, which may have long-term health impacts, especially through ingestion or inhalation.
- The regulation aims to **eliminate a significant source of plastic pollution** and promote more sustainable, eco-friendly alternatives (e.g., biodegradable or natural exfoliants).
