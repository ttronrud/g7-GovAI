---

#### **1. Regulatory Context and Legal Authority**

These regulations were issued by **Her Excellency the Governor General in Council**, on the recommendation of the **Minister of Finance**, under specific provisions of the **Trust and Loan Companies Act (TLC Act)**:

- **Section 459a** â€“ Authority to make regulations relating to activities involving information technology.
- **Section 531b** â€“ Power to regulate certain business activities, particularly those involving financial services and information systems.

The regulations were enacted under the authority of:
- **S.C. 2001, c. 9, s. 550** and **s. 569** (provisions authorizing the regulation of IT-related activities),
- **S.C. 1991, c. 45** (the original framework of the TLC Act).

These regulations provide a **framework for trust and loan companies** (including their subsidiaries) to engage in **information technology (IT) activities** that are materially related to the provision of financial products or services â€” while imposing **strict limits on ownership and investment** in such entities to ensure financial stability and regulatory oversight.

#### **3. Permitted IT Activities (Section 1)**

A trust or loan company may engage in the following **IT-related activities**, provided they are **materially related** to the provision of financial products or services:

- Developing, designing, holding, managing, manufacturing, selling, or otherwise dealing with:
  - Data transmission systems
  - Information sites (e.g., websites)
  - Communication devices
  - Information platforms or portals

âœ… **Conditions:**
- The activity must be **materially related** to the companyâ€™s or a member of its groupâ€™s financial services.
- Approval must be obtained under **paragraph 410(1)(c.1)** of the Act before engaging in such activities.

#### **4. Restrictions on Investment in IT Entities (Section 2)**

A company **may not acquire control of, or increase a substantial investment in**, an entity engaged in the IT activities described in Section 1, **if the sum of the following exceeds 5% of the companyâ€™s regulatory capital**:

1. **Aggregate balance sheet value** of shares/ownership interests the company and its subsidiaries would acquire in the entity (under paragraph 453(2)(f) of the Act).  
2. **Aggregate balance sheet value** of shares/ownership interests held by the company and its subsidiaries in **other entities** engaged in such IT activities (that the company controls or has a substantial investment in).  
3. **Aggregate value of outstanding loans** made by the company and its subsidiaries to such entities.

ðŸ“Œ **Key Points:**
- The 5% threshold applies to the **total exposure** across all such IT-related entities.
- The calculation is based on **unconsolidated balance sheet values**, not consolidated financial statements.
- This cap is designed to prevent **over-concentration of risk** in IT ventures that are tied to financial services.

> ðŸš¨ *Example:* If a trust company owns a digital banking platform, it cannot invest in another fintech platform (e.g., a credit scoring site) such that the total exposure (shares + loans) exceeds 5% of its regulatory capital.

#### **5. Prohibited Activities in IT Entities (Section 3)**

A company **cannot** acquire control of or increase a substantial investment in an entity that engages in any of the following activities:

| Prohibited Activity | Explanation |
|--------------------|-----------|
| **Accepting deposit liabilities** | Entities that take deposits (e.g., savings accounts) are excluded. This prevents IT companies from becoming de facto deposit-taking institutions. |
| **Dealing in securities** | Except as permitted under: <br> - Paragraph 453(2)(e) of the Act (limited securities trading), or <br> - Paragraph 409(2)(c) of the Act (specific exceptions). |
| **Dealing in goods, wares, or merchandise** | Not permitted unless explicitly allowed under subsection 410(2) of the Act. <br> â†’ This prevents IT companies from engaging in retail or commercial trading. |
| **Acting as executor, administrator, or guardian** | Prohibited unless under specific legal roles. |
| **Being a trustee for a trust** | Not allowed. |
| **Engaging in activities prohibited under Section 416** | Especially if the entity is a finance entity or falls under a prescribed category in paragraph 453(3)(e). |
| **Acquiring control of or holding a substantial investment in another entity** | Only permitted if: <br> - The company itself is authorized under **Part IX of the Act** to invest in that entity, or <br> - The company is authorized under **subsection 451(2), paragraph 451(3)(b) or (c), subsection 451(4), or subsections 453(1) or (2)** to invest in the other entity. |

> âš ï¸ *Exception:* The regulations explicitly **exclude** certain prescribed activities under **paragraph 453(3)(g)** â€” meaning some IT-related activities may be allowed if specifically authorized.

#### **6. Exemption from Subsection 453(5) (Section 4)**

For the purpose of determining whether a company can acquire a substantial investment in another entity under certain subsections (e.g., 451(2), 451(3)(b)/(c), 451(4), 453(1)/(2)):

- **Subsection 453(5) of the Act does not apply** when the company acquires control of or increases investment in an entity whose business is **limited to the activities described in subsection 3(1)**.

ðŸ“Œ This means:
- If the IT entity only engages in **basic, non-prohibited IT services** (e.g., a secure data transmission system used solely for financial product delivery), then the stricter investment rules (e.g., those under 453(5)) are **not triggered**.
- This provides **flexibility** for smaller or narrowly focused IT operations.

> âœ… *Benefit:* Encourages innovation in IT services without exposing companies to excessive risk.

### **Purpose and Regulatory Objectives**

These regulations aim to:

1. **Encourage innovation** in financial technology (fintech) by allowing trust and loan companies to develop digital tools for delivering financial services.
2. **Maintain financial stability** by limiting exposure to high-risk IT ventures through a clear 5% capital threshold.
3. **Prevent regulatory overlap or conflicts** by excluding entities that engage in non-financial or prohibited activities (e.g., securities trading, retail goods).
4. **Ensure transparency and accountability** by using balance sheet values and requiring prior approvals.
