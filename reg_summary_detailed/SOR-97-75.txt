## üîç **Detailed Summary of the CIFTA Verification of Origin Regulations**

### üìö **Legal Background and Context**

- These regulations implement a specific provision of the **Canada-Israel Free Trade Agreement (CIFTA)**.
- They are **not subject to publication requirements** under the *Customs Act* because they are a direct implementation of a provision of a bilateral trade agreement (as per **paragraph 164(4)(a.03)a of the Customs Act**).
- The regulations were made by **His Excellency the Governor General in Council**, on the recommendation of the **Minister of National Revenue**, under:
  - **Section 42.5c** of the *Customs Act*,
  - **Subsection 42.6(1)c**,
  - And **paragraph 164(1)(i)d** of the *Customs Act*.

> This means the regulations are an official, legally binding instrument issued to support the enforcement of preferential tariff treatment under CIFTA.

---

### üìÑ **Purpose of the Regulations**

The primary purpose is to establish **clear procedures for verifying the origin of goods** that are eligible for **preferential tariff treatment under CIFTA**.

- Ensures that goods claiming preferential treatment (e.g., lower tariffs) actually originate in a CIFTA beneficiary country (such as Israel or another member country).
- Prevents fraud or misrepresentation in origin claims.
- Provides a formal, transparent process for customs authorities to verify whether goods meet the origin requirements.

### üìå Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Act** | Refers to the *Customs Act* (Canada). |
| **Customs Administration** | The competent authority responsible for administering customs laws in Canada or in a CIFTA beneficiary country (e.g., Israel). |
| **Goods** | Goods for which preferential tariff treatment under CIFTA is being claimed. |
| **Verification Questionnaire** | A formal document sent to an exporter or producer asking for information about the origin of goods. |
| **Verification Visit** | An on-site inspection conducted by a customs officer to verify the origin of goods. |

> **Note**: A verification of origin can be conducted either through a **verification visit** or via a **verification questionnaire**.

### üè¢ **Prescribed Premises for Verification Visits**

- The **premises of an exporter or producer** in Israel or another CIFTA beneficiary country are considered **prescribed premises** for verification visits.
- This means that if a customs officer wants to inspect a business in Israel, they may do so at the exporter‚Äôs or producer‚Äôs physical location.

### üö® Conditions for Conducting a Verification Visit

A verification visit may only be conducted if **all of the following conditions are met**:

1. **Written Notice to the Customs Administration**  
   - The officer must send a written notice to the **customs administration of Israel or the relevant CIFTA beneficiary country**.

2. **Written Notice to the Exporter/Producer**  
   - The officer or agent must send a written notice to the **person whose premises are being visited**.

3. **Written Consent from the Exporter/Producer**  
   - The person whose premises are being visited must **provide written consent** to the visit.

> Without this consent, the visit is not legally valid.

### üìù Contents of the Verification Visit Notice

The notice must include the following details:

- Identity of the customs administration sending the notice  
- Name of the person whose premises are being visited  
- Date and place of the visit  
- Object and scope of the visit (specifically referencing the goods being verified)  
- Name and title of the officer conducting the visit  
- Legal authority under the *Customs Act* justifying the visit  

> This ensures transparency and provides a clear legal basis for the inspection.

### ‚è≥ Postponement of a Verification Visit

- The **receiving customs administration** (in Israel or another CIFTA country) may **postpone** the visit if it deems necessary.
- It must send a **written request for postponement within 15 days** of receiving the notice.
- The **maximum postponement period is 60 days** from the date the notice was received.
- However, **a longer period** can be agreed upon **if both Canada‚Äôs customs administration and the CIFTA beneficiary country agree**.

> This provides flexibility for logistical or operational reasons.

### üëÄ Observers During a Verification Visit

- Any person who receives the notice may **designate two observers** to be present during the visit.
- Observers **may not participate in the verification process** other than as passive observers.
- The observer must be **identified in advance** to the officer conducting the visit.

> This promotes transparency and accountability, allowing third parties (e.g., trade associations or legal representatives) to monitor the process.

### üìÑ Verification Questionnaire Requirements

When a verification questionnaire is sent:

1. The officer must send a **copy of the questionnaire** to the **customs administration of Israel or the CIFTA beneficiary country**.

2. The questionnaire must include:
   - Identity of the customs administration sending it  
   - Description of the goods being verified  
   - A deadline for completion and return: **not less than 30 days** from the date of sending  
   - Name and title of the officer or agent sending the questionnaire  

> This ensures that both Canadian and foreign authorities are informed and can review the origin data.

### ‚ö†Ô∏è Grounds for Withdrawing Preferential Tariff Treatment

If an exporter or producer fails to meet any of the following conditions, **preferential tariff treatment under CIFTA may be withdrawn**:

1. **No written consent** to a verification visit is provided within **30 days** of receiving the notice.
2. The **verification questionnaire is not completed and returned** within the 30-day deadline (or the information provided is insufficient).
3. The exporter or producer is **required to maintain records** under local laws (in the CIFTA country) and:
   - Fails to maintain those records properly, or
   - Denies access to those records to the customs officer.

> These are strict compliance requirements. Failure to comply results in the loss of preferential treatment.

> This means the rules were fully implemented on January 1, 1997, and have been in force since then.
