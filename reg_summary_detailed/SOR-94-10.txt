## üîç **Detailed Summary of the Marking of Imported Goods Regulations**

### üìå **1. Regulatory Background and Purpose**
- These regulations **revise and replace** the previous *Marking of Imported Goods Regulations* made on August 19, 1985 (SOR/85-837).
- The new regulations are issued under **subsection 63.1(2) of the Customs Tariff**.
- The primary purpose is to ensure **transparency and consumer protection** by requiring clear, accurate, and visible **country-of-origin markings** on imported goods.
- The changes reflect the evolving trade framework under the **Canada‚ÄìUnited States‚ÄìMexico Agreement (CUSMA)**, which replaced NAFTA in 2020, and aligns marking requirements with modern trade practices and consumer expectations.

> ‚úÖ **Effective Date**: The regulations come into force on the day that **Section 133 of the CUSMA Implementation Act** (S.C. 1993, c. 44, s. 133) becomes effective ‚Äî December 22, 1993.

### üìù **3. Mandatory Country of Origin Marking Requirements**

#### ‚úÖ For CUSMA Goods:
- Must indicate the **country of origin** to the **ultimate purchaser**.
- Marking must be in **English, French, or Spanish**.

#### ‚úÖ For Non-CUSMA Goods:
- Must indicate the **country of origin** to the **ultimate purchaser**, or if there is no purchaser, to the **ultimate recipient**.
- Marking must be in **English or French**, **except**:
  - **Honduras-origin goods**: May be marked in **English, French, or Spanish**.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Note**: The marking must be:
> - **Legible**
> - **Sufficiently permanent** (remains until final use or delivery)
> - **Easily visible** during normal handling or container use

### ‚ö†Ô∏è **4. Prohibition of Misleading Markings**

If any of the following appear on goods **outside** the country-of-origin marking:
- ‚ÄúCanada‚Äù, ‚ÄúCanadian‚Äù, ‚Äúcanadien‚Äù, or any abbreviation of these
- The name of another country or place

‚Üí **This may mislead** the purchaser or recipient.

‚û°Ô∏è **Requirement**:
- The **country-of-origin marking must be placed in close proximity** to such a word.
- It must include one of the following expressions:
  - ‚Äúmade in‚Äù
  - ‚Äúproduced in‚Äù
  - ‚Äúprinted in‚Äù
  - Or any equivalent expression in the applicable language(s)

> This ensures that consumers are not misled into thinking the product was made in Canada or another country when it was not.

### üñãÔ∏è **5. Methods of Marking Goods**

#### General Rule:
Marking must be **visible, legible, and permanent**.

#### Specific Methods by Product Type:

| Product Type | Allowed Marking Methods |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Iron or steel pipes/tubes** | - Paint stencilling<br>- Die stamping<br>- Cast-in-mould lettering<br>- Etching or engraving |

#### For **printed or lithographed paper products**:
- Marking must be:
  - **At least 1.6 mm (0.06 inch)** in size
  - In a **bold colour** ‚Äî as bold as any other printed or lithographed elements

### üì¶ **6. Marking of Containers**

#### Case 1: **Empty Usual Container**
- If the container is imported **empty** and required to be marked under CUSMA rules:
  - The **container itself must bear the country of origin** of the **usual container** (i.e., the container used to ship the goods normally).

#### Case 2: **Filled Usual Container**
- If the container is imported **filled**:
  - Marking of the **contents** is sufficient **if**:
    1. The contents are marked with their country of origin
    2. The container is **readily openable** for inspection
    3. The marking is **clearly visible through** the container

> Otherwise, the **container must be marked** with the country of origin of the contents.

### üìÖ **7. Timing of Marking (When and How)**

#### ‚úÖ General Rule:
- Goods **must be marked before importation** into Canada.

#### ‚úÖ Exceptions (Marking After Importation):
Marking **may occur after importation** but **before release**, **only if**:

1. The goods are **not imported as mail**
2. **No notice** has been served under subsection 35.02(2) of the *Customs Act* regarding:
   - Similar goods
   - Identical goods
   (OR the notice was served **less than 30 days or more than 2 years** before importation)
3. The importer provides **written notice** to a customs officer **before importation** that marking will be done in Canada, **OR**
   - At the time of importation, provides notice **with proof** that prior notice was not possible
4. The importer **complies with any instructions** given under **Section 15**

> üîç **Section 15 ‚Äì Officer Instructions**  
After receiving such a notice, the customs officer must provide written instructions specifying:
- The **customs office, bonded warehouse, or sufferance warehouse** where marking will occur
- The **time limit** for completing the marking
- The **location** where the goods must be made available for inspection after marking

> üìù Importers must **present a copy of these instructions** to an officer at:
- Time of **reporting under Section 12** of the Act
- Time of **accounting under subsection 32(1) or (2)** of the Act

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **If goods are delivered to a bonded warehouse before marking**:
- Marking must be completed **before** a transfer document is submitted under Section 18 of the *Customs Bonded Warehouse Regulations*

---

### üìú **8. Application to Prior Imports**

> The **old marking rules** (as they existed immediately before the coming into force of these regulations) **apply retroactively** to goods imported **before** the coming into force of **Section 183 of the Canada‚ÄìUnited States‚ÄìMexico Agreement Implementation Act (2020)**.

This ensures **legal continuity** for goods already imported and helps avoid retroactive penalties or enforcement actions.

> üìå Note: Section 183 (which replaced earlier provisions) was introduced in 2020 to further refine country-of-origin marking under CUSMA. The 1993 regulations remain in force **for pre-2020 imports**.
