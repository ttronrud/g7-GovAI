## üîç **Detailed Summary of the Regulation: S.C. 1986, c. 1**

### üìå **1. Background and Legal Basis**
- This regulation was **issued by the Governor in Council** (on the recommendation of the Minister of National Revenue) under:
  - **Section 40(1)** and **paragraph 164(1)(i)** of the *Customs Act*.
- It came into effect upon the coming into force of those provisions in the *Customs Act*.
- A **proposed version** of these regulations was published in *Part I of the Canada Gazette* on **March 15, 1986**, giving interested parties a reasonable opportunity to make representations to the Minister.
- The regulation was **repealed in 2005** (SOR/2005-384, s. 2), meaning it is **no longer in force** as of that date. However, this summary focuses on its **original content and intent**.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Important Note**: While this regulation was historically significant, it has been **replaced or superseded** by more modern regulations, particularly under the *Accounting for Imported Goods and Payment of Duties Regulations* and other updated record-keeping rules. ## üìö **Core Record-Keeping Requirements**

### ‚úÖ General Record-Keeping Obligations (Applicable to All Importers of Commercial Goods)

Every person required to keep records under subsection 40(1) of the *Customs Act* must retain the following records **for six years after the importation** of the commercial goods:

1. **Origin, marking, purchase, importation, costs, and value** of the commercial goods  
   ‚Üí Includes details such as country of origin, labeling, purchase price, import cost, and valuation data.

2. **Payment for the commercial goods**  
   ‚Üí Records of invoices, payment receipts, bank statements, or other proof of payment.

3. **Sale or other disposal of the goods in Canada**  
   ‚Üí Sales invoices, delivery notes, receipts, or records showing how and when the goods were sold or disposed of.

4. **Applications for advance rulings**  
   ‚Üí Any requests made under *section 43.1* of the *Customs Act* regarding duty treatment of goods.

---

### ‚úÖ Additional Specific Records (Required for CSA Importers)

In addition to the above, **CSA importers** must also keep:

- **Description and quantity** of the commercial goods  
  ‚Üí E.g., product names, SKUs, unit counts, packaging details.

- **Records of reception** of the goods  
  ‚Üí Documentation showing when and how goods arrived at the port or warehouse.

- **Credits and adjustments** used to determine the final amount paid  
  ‚Üí Includes deductions, discounts, allowances, or adjustments made during cost calculation.

- **Accounting records** for the commercial goods  
  ‚Üí Financial records showing how goods were tracked and accounted for in the business books.

- **Refunds, drawbacks, or re-determination of duties**  
  ‚Üí Records of any duty refunds, reductions, or reassessments.

- **List of vendors and consignees**  
  ‚Üí Names and addresses of suppliers and parties to whom goods were delivered.

- **Records needed to complete the ‚ÄúCSA Revenue Summary‚Äù form**  
  ‚Üí Required to support duty payments and ensure compliance with revenue reporting obligations.

### ‚úÖ Special Requirements for Goods Released Free of Duty or at Reduced Duty

When goods are released **free of duty or at a reduced rate** due to:
- Their **intended use**, or  
- Being intended for a **specific person**,

then the importer must maintain **additional records** for **six years** after importation:

#### Option 1: Goods Released Based on Intended Use
- A **certificate or record signed by the actual user** must be kept, including:
  - User‚Äôs name, address, and occupation
  - Clear evidence of the **actual use** made of the goods

#### Option 2: Goods Released Under Tariff Item 9948.00.00
- If the goods were released under **tariff item 9948.00.00** (often related to specific non-commercial or exempt uses), the importer must provide:
  - An **attestation** signed by the importer stating:
    - Name, address, and occupation
    - The **intended use** of the goods
    - Confirmation that the goods fall within the description of that tariff item

#### Option 3: Goods Diverted from Original Use or Sold to Unauthorized Party
- If the goods are **diverted** from their original use or sold to someone **not entitled** to receive them at reduced duty:
  - Records must show that **full applicable duties have been paid**  
    ‚Üí This ensures compliance and prevents fraud or misuse.

> üí° Purpose: To verify that the conditions for duty exemption were met and that no duty was improperly avoided.

## üìÇ **How Records Must Be Kept**

The regulation sets strict standards for **how records are stored and preserved**:

1. **Accessibility for Audits**  
   ‚Üí Records must be kept in a **manner enabling detailed audits** by customs officers.  
   ‚Üí Officers must be able to verify all information used in duty calculations (paid, payable, refunded, or deferred).

2. **Acceptable Formats**
   - **Photographic, microfilm, or electronic imaging** is allowed **if compliant with National Standard CAN/CGSB-72.11-93** (published by the Canadian General Standards Board, November 1993, as amended).
   - **Electronic records** are permitted **if**:
     - They can be **traced back to original source documents**
     - They are stored on **machine-sensible media**
     - The system supports **accessible, readable copies** at any time

> ‚úÖ This ensures flexibility while maintaining integrity and auditability.

## ‚öñÔ∏è **Consequences of Non-Compliance**

Failure to maintain required records can lead to **loss of preferential tariff treatment** under international trade agreements:

- **Under NAFTA (now USMCA)**  
- **Under CCFTA (Canada-China Free Trade Agreement)**  
- **Under CCRFTA (Canada-Central America Free Trade Agreement)**

### Key Provisions:
- If a person **(not covered under section 3.1)** fails to keep records as required:
  - Preferential tariff treatment **may be denied or withdrawn** for the affected commercial goods.
- If a person **fails to produce records under subsection 43(1)** of the *Customs Act*:
  - Preferential tariff treatment **may also be denied or withdrawn**.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è This is a critical enforcement mechanism ‚Äî it ties compliance with record-keeping directly to access to lower tariffs and trade benefits.
