**Detailed Summary of the Regulation: "Direction to the CRTC (Reservation of Cable Channels)"**

---

### **Overview**
This regulation, formally titled *Direction to the CRTC (Reservation of Cable Channels)*, establishes a framework under which the **Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC)** may be directed to reserve at least one channel on a cable transmission facility for use by a **provincial authority** in the context of **educational broadcasting**. It was issued on or after **March 19, 1970**, and remains a foundational piece of Canadian broadcasting policy aimed at ensuring access to educational content through cable networks.

---

### **Key Provisions and Interpretation**

#### **1. Licensing Restrictions on Cable Facilities**
- **Prohibition on New or Renewed Licenses**:  
  The CRTC is **prohibited from issuing or renewing a licence** for a broadcasting receiving undertaking (i.e., a cable system) located in a province **if**:
  - The **provincial authority** of that province has formally notified the CRTC in writing that it wishes to **reserve cable transmission facilities** for educational broadcasting.
  - The reservation includes:
    - Specific **times** during which the facilities will be used.
    - Specific **types of programming** (as defined in the appendix).
    - A demonstrated **intention to use the facilities within a reasonable period of time**.

> In such cases, the CRTC must **stipulate** that **at least one channel** of the cable system be reserved for the provincial authority.

> *This means that without such a reservation, cable operators cannot legally operate in provinces where a provincial authority has made a formal request.*

---

#### **2. Definition of "Provincial Authority"**
- The term **"provincial authority"** refers to a body or entity designated by the **Lieutenant Governor in Council** of a province.
- This designation is made specifically for the purposes of this regulation and may include government departments, education ministries, or other public institutions responsible for education.
- The authority must have a clear mandate and legal standing to manage educational broadcasting within the province.

---

#### **3. Purpose of Reserved Channel Use**
When the CRTC approves a reservation of a channel for a provincial authority, the reserved time must be used exclusively for **educational programming**, specifically:

##### **(a) Programming Aimed at Continuity of Learning**
- Must provide a **continuous learning opportunity** for audiences.
- Designed to:
  - Acquire or improve knowledge.
  - Enlarge understanding among viewers.
- Must be **subject to supervision or assessment** by the provincial authority through appropriate means (e.g., teacher evaluations, viewer feedback, academic review).

> This ensures that programming is not merely informational or entertainment-based but is structured and monitored to meet educational standards.

##### **(b) Information on Educational Programs and Events**
- Must include:
  - Information about **available courses of instruction**.
  - Broadcasts of **special education events** (e.g., school assemblies, teacher workshops, student performances, academic conferences).

> This component supports transparency and engagement within the educational system.

---

#### **4. Distinction from General Broadcasting**
- The regulation emphasizes that the programming reserved for provincial authorities must be:
  - **Distinct from general public or private channel content**.
  - Specifically designed to serve **educational objectives**, not entertainment, news, or commercial purposes.
- The **overall intent** is to ensure that the programming:
  - Is **educationally meaningful**.
  - Is **accessible and relevant** to students, educators, and parents.
  - Is **monitored and accountable** to the provincial authority.

---

### **Governor in Council Role**
- The CRTC may only make a reservation **on the direction of the Governor in Council**.
- This reflects the federal government’s oversight role in broadcasting policy, ensuring that such decisions align with national interests and regulatory standards.

---

### **Policy Objectives and Rationale**
This regulation was designed to:
1. **Promote educational access** in rural and underserved areas where access to formal education might be limited.
2. **Empower provincial governments** to directly manage educational content delivery via cable infrastructure.
3. **Ensure accountability** through supervision and assessment of educational programming.
4. **Prevent commercialization** of educational content by reserving specific channels for public, non-commercial use.
5. **Support the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation (CBC)** when it acts as an agent of Her Majesty in right of Canada — allowing CBC to provide transmission facilities to provincial authorities for educational purposes.

---

### **Significance and Legacy**
- This regulation laid the groundwork for **provincial control over educational broadcasting** in Canada.
- It established a **legal and procedural mechanism** for cable companies to cooperate with provincial education bodies.
- It remains relevant in discussions about **public access to education**, **media equity**, and **regulatory oversight** in broadcasting.

> While updated by later legislation and policy shifts (e.g., the *Cable Television Act* and modern digital broadcasting), this 1970 direction remains a foundational precedent in Canadian media regulation.

---

### **Conclusion**
The *Direction to the CRTC (Reservation of Cable Channels)* ensures that cable systems in Canada serve not only as entertainment platforms but also as tools for **educational development**. By requiring that at least one channel be reserved for provincial authorities under strict educational criteria, the regulation promotes **public interest**, **accountability**, and **equitable access to learning opportunities** across all provinces. It reflects a balanced approach between federal regulatory oversight and provincial educational autonomy.

--- 

✅ **In essence**:  
*If a province wants to use a cable system for educational broadcasting, it must notify the CRTC. If approved, the CRTC must reserve at least one channel for educational programming — content that is learning-focused, supervised, and tied to actual educational activities — ensuring that such programming is distinct from general entertainment.*
