**Detailed Summary of the Regulations Implementing United Nations Security Council Resolution 2206 (2015) on South Sudan**

---

### **Background and Purpose**

These regulations were enacted by the Government of Canada under **section 2 of the United Nations Act**, based on the recommendation of the Minister of Foreign Affairs. They implement **United Nations Security Council Resolution 2206 (2015)**, adopted on **March 3, 2015**, which addresses concerns regarding armed conflict, the proliferation of weapons, and the involvement of armed mercenaries in South Sudan.

The primary objective of these regulations is to **prohibit certain transactions and activities** in Canada that support or facilitate the use of arms, military activities, and armed mercenaries in South Sudan — particularly those linked to violations of international law or destabilizing actions — while **preserving essential humanitarian, diplomatic, and development activities**.

---

## **Key Definitions**

The regulations establish precise definitions of key terms to ensure clarity and enforceability:

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Armed Mercenary** | A person who is not part of South Sudan’s armed forces, is not serving a state officially, and is motivated primarily by private gain; uses or recruits to use arms in South Sudan. |
| **Arms and Related Material** | Includes weapons, ammunition, military equipment (including vehicles), paramilitary gear, and spare parts. |
| **Canadian** | A citizen of Canada or any entity established, incorporated, or continuing under Canadian or provincial law. |
| **Committee of the Security Council** | The body established under paragraph 16 of Resolution 2206 to monitor and make decisions on measures related to South Sudan. |
| **Designated Person** | A person designated by the Committee of the Security Council under paragraph 6 of Resolution 2206. |
| **Entity** | Includes corporations, trusts, partnerships, funds, unincorporated associations, or foreign states. |
| **Military Activities** | Any actions by state or non-state armed forces or armed mercenaries that support operational capabilities of armed groups. |
| **Minister** | The Minister of Foreign Affairs. |
| **Official** | A person employed, holding a position, or engaged by Her Majesty in right of Canada or a province. |
| **Person** | An individual or entity. |
| **Property** | (Repealed – no longer applies) |
| **Security Council** | The United Nations Security Council. |
| **Security Council Resolution 2206** | The UN resolution establishing measures against armed mercenaries and weapons trade in South Sudan. |
| **South Sudan** | The Republic of South Sudan, including its political subdivisions, government bodies, agencies, and departments. |
| **Technical Assistance** | Includes training, consulting, instruction, know-how transfer, or advice related to military or operational capabilities. |
| **Working Day** | A day that is not a Saturday or a statutory holiday. |

> ⚠️ **Note**: Several definitions (e.g., *Focal Point for De-listing*, *Panel of Experts*, *Property*) have been **repealed** as of **SOR/2020-121, section 2**, and are no longer in force.

## **Prohibited Activities (Section 2)**

It is **prohibited** for any person in Canada or any Canadian abroad to:

1. **Deal in property** owned, held, or controlled directly or indirectly by a **designated person** or a person acting on their behalf or at their direction.
2. **Enter into or facilitate transactions** related to such dealings.
3. **Provide or acquire financial or related services** in connection with such dealings.
4. **Make available property** to or for the benefit of a designated person, a person acting on their behalf, or an entity owned or controlled by them.
5. **Provide financial or related services** to such persons or entities.

> ❌ These prohibitions apply **unless** explicitly exempted under specific humanitarian or authorized exceptions.

---

## **Exemptions to Section 2 (Humanitarian and Authorized Activities)**

Section 2 **does not apply** to the following activities, which are essential for humanitarian and peace-building efforts:

- **Humanitarian Assistance** provided by:
  - The United Nations (including its programs, funds, specialized agencies, and related bodies);
  - International organizations;
  - Humanitarian organizations with UN observer status;
  - Bilateral or multilateral NGOs participating in UN humanitarian response plans (e.g., refugee or disaster appeals), including their employees, grantees, subsidiaries, and partners, when acting in those roles.

- **Payment of interest or earnings** arising from transactions that occurred **before** a person became a designated person — though such payments become subject to the prohibition once the designation takes effect.

> ✅ These exemptions ensure that life-saving aid and essential services can continue without disruption.

---

## **Prohibited Export and Transfer of Arms and Technical Assistance (Section 3 & 5)**

It is **prohibited** to:

1. **Export, sell, supply, or transfer** arms and related material **to South Sudan or any person in South Sudan**, directly or indirectly.
2. **Provide technical assistance or financial assistance** related to:
   - The sale, supply, transfer, manufacture, maintenance, or use of arms and related material in South Sudan.
3. **Provide technical or financial assistance** related to:
   - Military activities (including training, equipment, logistics, etc.).
   - The recruitment, deployment, or operation of **armed mercenaries**.

> ❌ These prohibitions are broad and apply to all Canadian individuals, businesses, and institutions.

---

## **Prohibited Use of Canadian Vessels and Aircraft (Section 4)**

- **Canadian vessel owners, masters, or operators** of aircraft registered in Canada **cannot carry, cause to be carried, or permit** the carriage of arms and related material destined for South Sudan or any person in South Sudan.

> ⚠️ This includes both commercial and government-operated vessels or aircraft.

---

## **Exceptions to Arms and Technical Assistance Prohibitions (Sections 3 & 5)**

Despite the general prohibitions, the following activities are **exempt** if certain conditions are met:

1. **Non-lethal military equipment** intended solely for **humanitarian or protective use** — if **advance notification** is given to the Committee of the Security Council.
2. **Protective clothing** (e.g., flak jackets, helmets) temporarily exported **to UN personnel, media representatives, and humanitarian workers** for **personal use only**.
3. **Arms and materials** intended solely for:
   - The use of **state forces** protecting or evacuating nationals under international law (e.g., consular responsibility).
   - The **African Union Regional Task Force** combating the Lord’s Resistance Army (LRA).
4. **Activities supporting the implementation of the Peace Agreement** — specifically, the **Khartoum Declaration of June 27, 2018** — or any other activity **if the Committee of the Security Council has given prior approval**.

> These exceptions are designed to allow legitimate, peaceful, and internationally recognized efforts to maintain stability and protect civilians.

---

## **Prohibition on Facilitation or Assistance (Section 6)**

Any person in Canada or abroad who **knowingly causes, facilitates, or assists** in any activity prohibited under sections 2 to 5 is **also prohibited**.

> This includes indirect support, coordination, or financial facilitation — even if not directly involved.

---

## **Obligations of Certain Entities (Section 9)**

Specific types of Canadian financial and business entities must **continuously monitor** their operations to determine whether they hold or control property owned, held, or controlled by or on behalf of a **designated person**. These include:

- Authorized foreign banks and banks regulated under the Bank Act;
- Cooperative credit societies, credit unions, caisses populaires, and associations under provincial law;
- Foreign companies (insurance businesses);
- Insurance companies, provincial companies, and societies;
- Fraternal benefit societies (insurance activities);
- Trust and loan companies;
- Loan companies;
- Entities engaged in securities dealing or portfolio management (if they open client accounts);  
- Provincial entities authorized to offer investment or portfolio management services.

> These entities have a **duty of vigilance** to detect and report suspicious property links.

---

## **Disclosure Obligations (Section 10)**

All persons and entities listed in section 9 must **disclose without delay** to either:

- The **Commissioner of the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP)**, or  
- The **Director of the Canadian Security Intelligence Service (CSIS)**

the following:

1. The existence of property in their possession or control that they **believe is owned, held, or controlled** by or on behalf of a designated person.
2. Any **transaction or proposed transaction** involving such property.

> ✅ **No liability** arises for disclosures made in **good faith** under this section.

---

## **Exemptions for Specific Activities (Section 11)**

A person wishing to engage in a prohibited activity must apply in writing to the **Minister of Foreign Affairs** for a **certificate of exemption**.

### Conditions for Issuance of Exemption Certificate:

- The **Security Council did not intend** the activity to be prohibited; OR
- The **Security Council or the Committee of the Security Council has approved** the activity in advance.

> This allows for flexibility in rare cases where international actors or humanitarian actors need to proceed with specific activities.
