## üîç **OVERVIEW OF THE REGULATION**

This document is a **formal Canadian Proclamation** that officially brings into force the **Agreement on Social Security between Canada and the Eastern Republic of Uruguay**, signed on June 2, 1999. The agreement establishes a framework for mutual recognition of social security rights, particularly for pensions and related benefits, between the two countries. It ensures that individuals who work or reside in either country can accumulate creditable periods of contributions that count toward eligibility for retirement, disability, or survivor benefits in both jurisdictions ‚Äî without facing reductions, suspensions, or loss of benefits due to cross-border residency.

The agreement is grounded in international social security cooperation principles and is aligned with the **Vienna Conventions on Diplomatic and Consular Relations**, which remain applicable even under this agreement.

### 4. **Scope of Application**

- Applies to:
  - All individuals who have been or are currently subject to the legislation of either country.
  - Individuals who derive benefits from such persons (e.g., spouses, dependents).
- **Equal treatment**: All persons described in the agreement must be treated equally under the legislation of each country regarding rights and obligations.

### 5. **Non-Reduction of Benefits (Core Principle)**

> **Article 6** ‚Äî A key pillar of the agreement.

Unless otherwise stated:
- Pensions and benefits **shall not be reduced, suspended, canceled, or confiscated** simply because the beneficiary resides in the other country.
- Benefits may be paid **in the territory of the other country or a third state**, if requested by the beneficiary.

> ‚úÖ This protects retirees and workers from being penalized for living abroad.

### 6. **Special Rules for Employees (Article 7)**

#### a) **Temporary Detachments (‚â§36 months)**
- Employees temporarily sent by employer to work in the other country for up to **36 months** are subject **only to the legislation of the home country**.
- Can be extended by **24 additional months** with **prior express consent** from both competent authorities.

#### b) **Seafarers (Crew Members)**
- If a seafarer resides and is hired in **Canada**, they are subject **only to Canadian legislation**.
- If they reside and are hired in **Uruguay**, they are subject **only to Uruguayan legislation**.
- If the ship flies the **Uruguayan flag**, the seafarer is subject to Uruguayan law.

#### c) **Harbour Work**
- Work related to loading, unloading, or repairing ships at a port is governed by the **territorial jurisdiction** of that port (i.e., only the local country‚Äôs law applies).

#### d) **Government Employees**
- Employees posted to work in another country for a government employer are subject **only to the legislation of the home country**.
- If the employee is working for the other country‚Äôs government, they may **elect** (within 6 months of start or entry into force) to be subject to the **employing country‚Äôs legislation**, **only if they had previously contributed** under that country‚Äôs system.

> üîç This exception applies to employees in personal service of diplomats or consular officials under the Vienna Conventions.

#### e) **Common Exceptions**
- Both parties may, by mutual agreement, make **additional exceptions or modifications** to these rules for specific categories of employees.

### 7. **Creditable Periods and Totalization (Article 8)**

This section governs how periods of contribution or residence in one country are counted toward eligibility in the other.

#### a) **Residence and Contribution Periods in Uruguay**
- If a person contributes to the **Canada Pension Plan (CPP)** or a provincial comprehensive pension plan during a period of residence in Uruguay:
  - That period is counted as **residence in Canada** for the person and their spouse/dependents **not employed in Uruguay**.

- If a person is subject to **Uruguayan legislation** during a period of residence in Canada:
  - That period is **not counted** as residence in Canada for the person or their dependents **unless they are employed in Canada**.

> ‚öñÔ∏è *Only employment or self-employment leads to creditable periods.*

#### b) **Eligibility Determination (Totalization Rules)**
- If a person does **not meet the required creditable periods** under one country‚Äôs legislation:
  - The competent organization may **totalize** creditable periods from **both countries**, provided the periods do not overlap.
- If totalization still fails to meet eligibility:
  - The person may be assessed against **third-party agreements** where both countries have social security arrangements allowing totalization.

> üí° *This ensures that people do not lose access to benefits due to gaps in contribution history.*

### 8. **Old Age Security (OAS) ‚Äì Special Rules (Article 9)**

#### a) **Residence Requirements for OAS Outside Canada**
- A person outside Canada is eligible for an OAS pension **only if** the total residence periods (Canada + Uruguay) meet or exceed the **minimum residency requirement** set by Canadian law for pensions outside Canada.

- Spouse‚Äôs allowance and Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) are paid **only to the extent permitted** by OASA.

> üìå *This ensures that OAS is not available without sufficient residency, but allows for cross-border credit.*

#### b) **Calculation of OAS Pension**
- If a person is eligible **only through totalization**, the amount is calculated:
  - Based **only on periods of residence in Canada**.
  - Using rules for **partial pensions** or spouse allowances under OASA.

> üìâ *No benefit is paid if total creditable periods under one country are less than one year and no right exists under that country‚Äôs law ‚Äî but such periods may still count toward eligibility in the other country.*
