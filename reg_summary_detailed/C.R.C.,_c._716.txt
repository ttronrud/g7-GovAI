**Detailed Summary of the National Defence Official Mementos Regulations**

---

**1. Title and Citation**  
The regulation is officially titled the *National Defence Official Mementos Regulations* and may be cited as such. It establishes the rules governing the transfer of official mementos by the Department of National Defence (DND) and the Canadian Forces.

---

**2. Key Definitions**

- **Minister**: Refers to the Minister of National Defence, who holds overall responsibility for the implementation and oversight of these regulations.  
- **Official Memento**: Defined broadly as *any item of public property of a distinctly Canadian character and manufacture*. This includes:
  - Contemporary arts and crafts
  - Items representative of the Canadian Forces
  - Items reflecting Canadian culture
  - Items produced in Canada (emphasizing Canadian manufacturing)

This definition ensures that only items that are distinctly Canadian in origin, design, or cultural significance are eligible for transfer.

---

**3. Purpose and Scope of Transfer**

The regulations allow for the **transfer of official mementos** to the following individuals or entities, **subject to specific conditions**:

- **Foreign dignitaries**
- **Military representatives** of foreign armed forces
- **Visiting lecturers** or other **visiting officials**

Such transfers are permitted **in appreciation of**:
- Professional assistance
- Social hospitality

These acts must occur in support of programs conducted by:
- The Department of National Defence (DND)
- The Canadian Forces

The transfer may take place **either inside or outside Canada**, indicating flexibility in diplomatic and international engagement.

---

**4. Approval Authority**

The transfer of an official memento requires formal approval from one of the following:

- The **Minister of National Defence**
- The **Chief of the Defence Staff (CDS)**
- An **officer designated by the Chief of the Defence Staff**

This establishes a tiered approval process, with the CDS or his designated officer having primary oversight over such transfers.

---

**5. Value-Based Restrictions (Key Limitation)**

To prevent misuse or excessive spending, the regulations impose **value-based limits** on transfers:

- **Chief of the Defence Staff (CDS)** shall **not approve** a transfer of an official memento if its **value exceeds $200**.
- An **officer designated by the CDS** shall **not approve** a transfer where the **value exceeds $75**.

These thresholds are designed to:
- Ensure fiscal responsibility
- Prevent the distribution of high-value items that could be seen as inappropriate or symbolic of undue influence
- Protect the integrity and value of official mementos as public property

> Note: The Minister retains authority to approve transfers beyond these limits, suggesting that exceptional or high-level diplomatic circumstances may be accommodated with ministerial discretion.

---

**6. Overall Principles**

- **Public Property**: All official mementos are considered public property and are not personal items.
- **Cultural Significance**: The emphasis on Canadian character, culture, and manufacturing ensures that transfers promote national identity and pride.
- **Diplomatic Use**: Transfers are intended to strengthen international relations through respectful and symbolic gestures.
- **Accountability and Oversight**: The structured approval process and value caps ensure transparency and accountability in the use of public resources.

---

**Conclusion**

The *National Defence Official Mementos Regulations* provide a clear, balanced framework for the appropriate and responsible use of Canadian-made cultural and military items in international diplomatic and professional engagements. By defining what constitutes an official memento, setting strict value thresholds, and establishing a clear approval hierarchy, the regulations ensure that such transfers serve legitimate purposes—such as fostering goodwill and supporting national defense programs—without compromising fiscal discipline or public trust.

This regulation reflects a careful balance between diplomatic engagement and the stewardship of public property.
