### **Detailed Summary of the Shipping Casualties Reporting Regulations, C.R.C., c. 1478**

---

#### **1. Purpose and Legal Authority**
The *Shipping Casualties Reporting Regulations* are issued under **section 543 of the Canada Shipping Act** (Act), by **Her Excellency the Governor General in Council**, on the recommendation of the **Minister of Transport**. These regulations replace the previous *Shipping Casualties Reporting Regulations*, C.R.C., c. 1478, with updated and more comprehensive reporting requirements for maritime incidents.

These regulations establish a clear and enforceable framework for the **immediate reporting of shipping casualties, accidents, dangerous occurrences, deaths, and disappearances of persons on board ships**. The primary goal is to ensure timely notification to Canadian authorities, support investigations, and enhance maritime safety and environmental protection.

---

#### **2. Key Definitions**

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Accident** | An event on board a ship resulting in: <br> - Loss of life; <br> - Belief that a person is missing overboard; <br> - Serious bodily injury (preventing resumption of duties within 24 hours after medical treatment); <br> - Temporary asphyxiation; or <br> - Electrocution leading to temporary loss of consciousness. |
| **Dangerous Occurrence** | An event involving: <br> - Breakage or malfunction of rigging, structure, or machinery that could have caused serious injury or loss of life; or <br> - A situation that could have led to a casualty or accident. |
| **Shipping Casualty** | As defined in **section 541 of the Canada Shipping Act** ‚Äî refers to any incident involving loss of life, injury, damage to property, or environmental harm resulting from a maritime event. |

---

#### **3. Scope of Application**
These regulations apply to:
- **All ships operating in Canadian waters**, including those on voyages to or from Canadian ports;
- **All Canadian-flagged ships**, regardless of where they are located in the world.

This means that whether a vessel is within Canadian territorial waters or abroad, any incident involving a Canadian ship or a foreign ship operating in Canadian waters must be reported according to these rules.

---

#### **4. Reporting Obligations**

##### **A. Immediate Radio Report (Within 24 Hours or As Soon As Possible)**  
**Who must report?**  
The **master**, any **certificated officer**, **crew member**, **pilot**, or **person responsible** for the ship (or the vessel being towed) must report the incident **without delay**.

**Reporting Methods:**
- **In Canadian waters or on a voyage to/from a Canadian port or alongside a Canadian port facility:**  
  ‚Üí Report **by radio** to a **Canadian radio ship reporting station** (e.g., Canadian Coast Guard stations, Vessel Traffic Service Centres, St. Lawrence Seaway Authority stations, or harbour radio stations).

- **Outside the range of a Canadian radio station:**  
  ‚Üí Report via radio to **any other radio ship reporting station**;  
  ‚Üí Or, if the incident occurs in a **Commonwealth country (excluding Canada)**: report to the **chief officer of customs** or **superintendent of a mercantile marine office**;  
  ‚Üí Or, if in a **non-Commonwealth country**: report to a **Canadian consular officer**.

**When radio communication is not possible while in a Canadian port:**  
‚Üí The incident must be reported **by telephone** to:
- The **local Canadian radio ship reporting station**, or  
- The **Canadian Coast Guard Operations Centre** in Ottawa.

##### **B. Content of the Initial Radio Report**
The report must include the following information:
1. Identity of the ship making the report;
2. Nature of the incident (e.g., collision, fire, grounding);
3. Identity of the ships involved;
4. Date, time, and precise location of the incident;
5. Number of persons killed, missing, or injured;
6. Whether a pilot is on board;
7. Whether the incident has caused or is likely to cause:
   - An obstruction to navigation or other serious hazard; or  
   - Pollution of waters.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Note**: If a report under section 642(1) of the Canada Shipping Act already contains all required information, **no additional report is needed** under section 5 of these regulations.

---

#### **5. Written Report (Within 24 Hours or As Soon As Possible)**

After the initial radio report, a **formal written report** must be submitted **within 24 hours** of the incident (or as soon as practicable).

##### **Who must submit it?**  
The **master**, any **certificated officer**, **crew member**, or **person responsible** for the ship.

##### **Content of the Written Report**
- Must include a **statement of the probable cause** of the incident.
- Must be submitted **by air mail** without delay to:  
  **Chief, Marine Casualty Investigations, Department of Transport, Ottawa, K1A 0N7.**

##### **Formal Requirements for Completion**
The report must be:
- Completed on the **form specified in the Schedule**;
- Signed by the master, a certificated officer, crew member, or person responsible for the ship or vessel being towed.

##### **Completion in the Presence of a Witness (Mandatory in Certain Jurisdictions)**

To ensure accuracy and accountability, the form must be completed **in the presence of a designated official**, depending on the location:

| Location | Required Witness |
|--------|------------------|
| **In Canada** | Chief Officer of Customs, Marine Casualty Investigations Officer, Ship Safety Officer, or any other Coast Guard officer. If none available, a Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) officer. |
| **In another Commonwealth country** | Chief Officer of Customs or Superintendent of a Mercantile Marine Office. |
| **In any other country (non-Commonwealth)** | Canadian Consular Officer. |

> ‚úÖ **Exception**: If **none of the required witnesses is available within 24 hours** of the first landing after the incident, the form may be **forwarded directly to the Chief, Marine Casualty Investigations, Transport Canada, Ottawa**, **without the signature of a witness**.

---

#### **6. Language and Bilingual Compliance**
The regulations are presented in **both English and French**, reflecting Canada‚Äôs official bilingual status. The French version is fully equivalent and legally binding, ensuring compliance across all Canadian jurisdictions.

---

#### **7. Enforcement and Compliance**
- Failure to report a shipping casualty, accident, or dangerous occurrence **without reasonable excuse** may result in penalties under the Canada Shipping Act.
- The Department of Transport, through its **Marine Casualty Investigations** unit, uses these reports to:
  - Investigate root causes;
  - Identify trends and safety risks;
  - Recommend improvements to maritime safety standards;
  - Prevent future incidents.

---

#### **8. Key Provisions and Practical Implications**

| Feature | Practical Significance |
|--------|------------------------|
| **Dual reporting (radio + written)** | Ensures both immediate response and detailed investigation. |
| **Time-bound reporting (within 24 hours)** | Promotes rapid response and data collection. |
| **Witness requirement** | Enhances credibility and transparency of reports. |
| **Geographic coverage** | Applies globally to Canadian ships and incidents in international waters. |
| **Environmental and navigational hazard reporting** | Supports pollution control and maritime traffic safety. |
| **Exemption for full Act reports** | Prevents redundant reporting if the Canada Shipping Act already covers the incident. |

---

#### **Conclusion**

The *Shipping Casualties Reporting Regulations* provide a **structured, timely, and legally enforceable system** for reporting maritime incidents involving loss of life, injury, danger, or environmental risk. By requiring immediate radio notification followed by a formal, witnessed written report, the regulations ensure:
- Rapid emergency response;
- Accurate data collection;
- Accountability and transparency;
- Support for safety investigations and regulatory improvements.

These rules are essential for maintaining **maritime safety, protecting human life, preventing environmental damage, and upholding Canada‚Äôs regulatory authority over its shipping operations**, both domestically and internationally.
