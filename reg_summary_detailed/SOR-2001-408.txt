**Detailed Summary of the Name Use by Non-Financial Businesses (Excluded Entities) Regulations**

---

### **1. Regulatory Authority and Basis**

The **Name Use by Non-Financial Businesses (Excluded Entities) Regulations** were issued by **Her Excellency the Governor General in Council**, acting on the **recommendation of the Minister of Finance**, under **section 983(16)a of the Bank Act**.

This regulatory instrument is part of Canada’s broader framework designed to enhance transparency and consumer protection in financial markets by regulating how certain entities use names in connection with financial services.

---

### **2. Purpose of the Regulations**

The primary objective of these regulations is to **prevent misleading or deceptive name usage** by non-financial businesses that are **associated with or have substantial financial ties to banks or financial institutions**.

Specifically, the regulations aim to ensure that non-financial businesses do not use names that could **mislead the public** into believing they are a bank, a financial institution, or otherwise engaged in banking or financial services — when they are not.

This helps protect consumers from **misrepresentation**, **fraud**, and **confusion** in the marketplace.

---

### **3. Scope and Applicability**

The regulations apply to **non-financial businesses** that fall within a defined category of "excluded entities" — entities that are **prescribed** under subsection **983(5.1)** of the *Bank Act*. These entities are subject to restrictions on name usage because of their **financial connection to banks or financial institutions**.

#### **Entities Prescribed Under Subsection 983(5.1)**:

The following types of entities are explicitly included in the list of excluded entities:

1. **An entity in which a bank has a substantial investment**  
   → If a bank holds a significant ownership stake (e.g., 10% or more), the entity is considered to be associated with a bank.

2. **A financial institution**  
   → Includes entities like credit unions, trust companies, insurance companies, etc., that are legally recognized as financial institutions.

3. **An authorized foreign bank**  
   → A foreign bank that is authorized to operate in Canada.

4. **Any other foreign bank**  
   → Includes foreign banks that are not authorized to operate in Canada but are still operating within the Canadian market.

5. **An entity affiliated with a bank or a bank holding company**  
   → Entities that have a formal or operational relationship (e.g., parent, subsidiary, joint venture, or sister company) with a Canadian bank or bank holding company.

6. **An entity in which a bank holding company has a substantial investment**  
   → Similar to point 1, but focused on investments in bank holding companies (which may own multiple banks).

7. **An entity associated with a foreign bank**  
   → Entities linked to a foreign bank through ownership, control, or operations.

8. **An entity in which a foreign bank has a substantial investment**  
   → A domestic entity that is owned or controlled by a foreign bank.

9. **An entity in which an entity associated with a foreign bank has a substantial investment**  
   → A tiered relationship: if a foreign-linked entity (e.g., a foreign bank or affiliate) owns a significant stake in a domestic entity, that domestic entity is included.

10. **An entity that has a substantial investment in a bank or a bank holding company**  
   → A non-financial business that owns a significant portion (e.g., 10% or more) of a bank or bank holding company.

11. **An entity affiliated with an entity that has a substantial investment in a bank or a bank holding company**  
   → A business linked (e.g., through control, shared management, or operations) to an entity that itself holds a significant stake in a bank or bank holding company.

> **Note**: These definitions are designed to capture both **direct and indirect financial relationships** with banks, ensuring that even complex ownership structures are considered.

---

### **4. Prohibited Name Use**

While the regulations do not explicitly state the full list of prohibited name uses in this summary, they are **intended to support the broader requirement under section 983(5.1)** of the *Bank Act*, which prohibits non-financial businesses from using names that:

- Could **mislead the public** into believing the entity is a bank or financial institution;
- Could **imply financial services** (such as lending, deposit-taking, or insurance) that the entity does not offer;
- Could **create a false impression of safety, reliability, or regulatory status**.

Thus, **name usage must be transparent and truthful** — for example, a non-financial business cannot use a name like “Bank of Canada” or “First National Financial Services” if it is only a retail store or a real estate firm with no banking operations.

---

### **5. Effective Date**

The regulations **come into force on the day that subsection 983(2) of the Bank Act** comes into force, as **enacted by section 183 of the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada Act (S.C. 2001, c. 9, s. 183)**.

This effective date is **October 24, 2001** (as noted in the reference to SI/2001-102).

> Therefore, the regulations became effective on **October 24, 2001**.

---

### **6. Legal Context and Background**

- The *Bank Act* (specifically section 983) governs the conduct of non-financial businesses in relation to name use and consumer protection.
- Section 983(5.1) establishes a **prescribed list** of entities that are subject to name use restrictions due to their financial connections with banks.
- The 2001 amendment (S.C. 2001, c. 9, s. 183) expanded the scope of the name use rules to include more entities, especially those with **indirect or foreign financial ties** to banks.
- This regulation was part of a broader effort by the Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC) to **enhance financial literacy and consumer trust** by reducing misleading branding in the marketplace.

---

### **7. Key Implications**

- **Transparency**: Businesses must ensure that their names do not imply financial services or affiliations they do not possess.
- **Consumer Protection**: Helps prevent consumers from being misled into thinking a non-financial business is a bank or financial institution.
- **Enforcement**: The regulations are enforced by the **Financial Consumer Agency of Canada (FCAC)**, which can take action against businesses found to be in violation.
- **Compliance Requirement**: Any non-financial business falling under one of the 11 prescribed categories must review its name and branding to ensure compliance.

---

### **8. Conclusion**

The **Name Use by Non-Financial Businesses (Excluded Entities) Regulations** are a critical component of Canada’s financial consumer protection regime. They establish a clear, comprehensive list of entities that are considered "excluded" due to their financial connections with banks or financial institutions. By restricting the use of names that could mislead the public, the regulations promote **market transparency**, **consumer trust**, and **ethical business practices** in the financial services ecosystem.

These rules reflect a proactive approach to preventing **misrepresentation** and ensuring that the public can distinguish between legitimate financial institutions and non-financial businesses that may exploit similar branding.
