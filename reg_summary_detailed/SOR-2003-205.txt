**Detailed Summary of the Regulation: *Regulations Respecting the Possession of Non-duty-paid Packaged Alcohol* (S.C. 2002, c. 22)**

---

### **1. Regulatory Authority and Legal Basis**
- The regulation was issued by **Her Excellency the Governor General in Council**, acting on the recommendation of the **Minister of National Revenue**.
- It is grounded in **paragraph 304(1)(o) of the Excise Act, 2001**, which grants the Minister the authority to make regulations concerning the possession of non-duty-paid packaged alcohol.
- This regulation specifically addresses the **conditions under which individuals may lawfully possess non-duty-paid packaged alcohol** in Canada.

---

### **2. Scope and Purpose**
The regulation establishes clear, legally binding conditions under which a person may possess **non-duty-paid packaged alcohol**—alcohol that has not been subject to excise duty and is not intended for general retail sale. The goal is to ensure that such possession is legitimate, traceable, and compliant with customs and excise laws, particularly in contexts involving transportation, trade, or specific authorized operations.

---

### **3. Eligibility to Possess Non-duty-paid Packaged Alcohol (Under Subparagraphs 88(2)(a)(iv), (c)(ii), and (d)(ii) of the Excise Act, 2001)**

A person may legally possess non-duty-paid packaged alcohol **only if one of the following conditions is met**:

#### **Condition 1: Authorized Transportation by Customs Officer**
- The person must be **authorized by an officer under Section 19 of the Customs Act** to transport non-duty-paid packaged alcohol.
- The alcohol must have been **reported under Section 12 of the Customs Act** (i.e., formally declared and documented).
- The person must be **acting in accordance with that authorization**.

> *This allows customs officers or authorized personnel to carry alcohol during authorized transport operations, such as cross-border movement or supply chain logistics, provided all reporting and compliance requirements are met.*

#### **Condition 2: Possession of Valid Documentation**
The person must have **documentary evidence** proving they are transporting the alcohol on behalf of one of the following:

- An **excise warehouse licensee**  
- A **licensed user**  
- A **registered user**  
- A **duty-free shop licensee**  
- **Any person** if the alcohol is designated as **ships’ stores** under the *Ships’ Stores Regulations*  
- An **accredited representative**

> *This provision ensures that only legitimate business entities or authorized individuals involved in regulated supply chains can possess such alcohol. The documentation must be sufficient to verify the legitimate purpose and chain of responsibility for the alcohol.*

> **Note on Ships’ Stores**: Alcohol designated as "ships’ stores" is exempt from duty and is permitted for use on vessels, provided it meets the specific criteria set out in the *Ships’ Stores Regulations*.

---

### **4. Eligibility to Possess a Non-duty-paid Marked Special Container (Under Paragraph 88(3)(b) of the Excise Act, 2001)**

A person may possess a **non-duty-paid marked special container of alcohol** (a specific type of container used for certain purposes, such as testing or distribution) **if**:

#### **Condition 1: Authorized Transportation by Customs Officer**
- The person is authorized by a customs officer under **Section 19 of the Customs Act** to transport the marked special container.
- The container must have been **reported under Section 12 of the Customs Act**.
- The person must be **acting in accordance with that authorization**.

#### **Condition 2: Possession of Valid Documentation**
The person must have documentation proving they are transporting the container on behalf of:

- An **excise warehouse licensee**, or  
- A **registered user**

> *This is a more limited scope than the general packaged alcohol rule, focusing only on special containers and specific authorized parties. It excludes duty-free shops, licensed users, and others who are eligible for general packaged alcohol.*

---

### **5. Key Provisions and Exclusions**
- The regulation **does not permit general public possession** of non-duty-paid alcohol.
- Possession is strictly limited to:
  - Authorized transport personnel
  - Individuals acting on behalf of specific licensed or regulated entities
  - Cases involving ships’ stores (a narrow exception)
- The presence of **valid documentation** is a mandatory requirement in most cases.
- The alcohol must have been **previously reported** under the Customs Act (Section 12), ensuring traceability and compliance with customs procedures.

---

### **6. Effective Date**
- These regulations **take effect on July 1, 2003**.
- All provisions apply from that date onward, and prior practices or unregulated possession of non-duty-paid alcohol are no longer lawful under this framework.

---

### **7. Regulatory Objectives and Implications**
- **Compliance and Accountability**: The regulation strengthens oversight by requiring documentation and formal authorization for the possession of non-duty-paid alcohol.
- **Prevention of Fraud and Unauthorized Trade**: By limiting possession to authorized actors and requiring reporting, the regulation reduces the risk of smuggling, illegal distribution, or misuse of non-dutied goods.
- **Support for Legitimate Trade Flows**: It enables legitimate operations such as:
  - Transportation of alcohol for duty-free shops
  - Supply to excise warehouses
  - Shipboard provisions (ships’ stores)
  - Specialized testing or distribution containers

---

### **Conclusion**
The *Regulations Respecting the Possession of Non-duty-paid Packaged Alcohol* (S.C. 2002, c. 22) provide a **clear, structured, and legally enforceable framework** for when individuals may possess non-duty-paid alcohol. It ensures that such possession is limited to authorized, documented, and compliant activities, reinforcing Canada’s customs and excise regulatory integrity. The rules emphasize accountability, traceability, and the prevention of unauthorized or illicit use of alcohol not subject to duty.

These regulations are a key component of Canada’s broader framework for managing excise goods and ensuring that non-dutied alcohol is only handled through legitimate, regulated channels.
