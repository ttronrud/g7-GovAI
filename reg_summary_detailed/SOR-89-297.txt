**Detailed Summary of the Newfoundland Rules of Practice Respecting Reduction in the Number of Years of Imprisonment Without Eligibility for Parole**  
*Issued by the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court of Newfoundland, Trial Division, May 31, 1989*  

---

### **Overview**
These rules establish the procedural framework for applications under **subsection 745(1) of the Criminal Code** of Newfoundland and Labrador, which allows a person sentenced to imprisonment without eligibility for parole to apply for a reduction in the number of years of their sentence. The rules govern the filing, service, hearing, and disposition of such applications, ensuring a fair and structured judicial process while respecting the constitutional and statutory rights of the applicant and the state.

The rules are formally titled:  
**"Newfoundland Rules of Practice Respecting Reduction in the Number of Years of Imprisonment Without Eligibility for Parole"**  
and may be cited as such.

---

### **Key Definitions**

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Applicant** | A person who makes an application, or, in context, counsel acting on their behalf. |
| **Application** | A formal request made by an applicant under subsection 745(1) of the Criminal Code seeking a reduction in the length of imprisonment. |
| **Attorney General** | The Attorney General of Newfoundland, including counsel acting for the Attorney General. |
| **Chief Justice** | The head of the Supreme Court of Newfoundland, Trial Division. |
| **Clerk** | The clerk of the Registry of the Supreme Court in the judicial district where the original sentence was imposed. |
| **Judge** | A judge of the Supreme Court designated by the Chief Justice under subsection 745(2) of the Criminal Code to empanel a jury for the hearing. |

---

### **Filing and Content Requirements of an Application**

An application must be:
- **In writing** and submitted to the **clerk** of the Registry of the Supreme Court in the relevant judicial district.
- Include the following information:
  1. Applicantâ€™s full name (given names, surname, and any other names used), and date of birth.
  2. Name and location of the institution where the applicant is currently detained.
  3. The offence, date of conviction, and the original sentence imposed.
  4. The length of time already served for that offence.
  5. Grounds supporting the application (e.g., changed circumstances, rehabilitation, etc.).
  6. Relief sought (e.g., reduction of sentence by X years).
  7. Applicantâ€™s address for service of legal documents.
  8. An **outline of any evidence** the applicant intends to present at the hearing.

### **Service of the Application**

The applicant must serve the application on three parties:
1. **The Attorney General of Newfoundland**  
2. **The Solicitor General of Canada**  
3. **The officer in charge of the institution** where the applicant is detained.

#### Service Method:
- Must be sent by **registered mail**.
- Service is deemed effective on the **tenth day after mailing**.

#### Proof of Service:
- The applicant or their counsel may file with the clerk:
  - An **affidavit** sworn by the person who effected service.
- Alternatively, any other method acceptable to the clerk may be used.

> ðŸ” **Important Note**: Service on the Solicitor General of Canada or the institution officer is **for information only**.  
> These parties are **not parties to the application** and have **no right to participate or respond** in the proceedings.

---

### **Processing by the Clerk and Chief Justice**

Upon receiving proof of service:
- The **clerk** delivers the application and proof of service to the **Chief Justice**.
- The Chief Justice forwards the application to the **designated judge** for hearing.

---

### **Dismissal of Applications**

The judge may **dismiss the application** if they determine, on their own initiative or at the request of the Attorney General, that **subsection 745(1) of the Criminal Code does not apply** to the applicant.

> This may occur if the applicant is not eligible for a reduction under the law (e.g., due to the nature of the offence, the sentence type, or the time served).

---

### **Hearing Procedures and Judicial Directions**

The judge has broad discretion to ensure a fair and efficient hearing and may:
- Arrange the **date and venue** of the hearing.
- Fix the **empanelling of a jury** (in accordance with Part XX of the Criminal Code, with necessary modifications).
- Make orders regarding:
  - **Detailed outlines of evidence** (applicant or Attorney General).
  - **Admissibility of affidavits** (proof of facts).
  - **Attendance of deponents** for cross-examination (if an affidavit is used).
  - **Bringing the applicant before the court** (if necessary).

#### Specific Orders:
- **Order for detailed evidence outline**: If the judge deems it necessary, the applicant or the Attorney General must submit a more detailed plan of evidence.
- **Order for Attorney Generalâ€™s evidence outline**: The Attorney General must provide a detailed outline of their intended evidence.
- **Permit affidavit evidence**: The judge may allow the use of affidavits to prove facts.
- **Require applicantâ€™s appearance**: The judge may order the applicant to appear personally before the court (e.g., for cross-examination or to confirm facts).

> If the judge orders an affidavit to be presented, **section 527 of the Criminal Code applies**, with adjustments based on the circumstances.

> The jury is empanelled under **Part XX of the Criminal Code**, with **modifications as required** by the specific case.

---

### **Evidence Rules at the Hearing**

- **Only the applicant and the Attorney General** may present evidence.
- The applicant presents evidence **first**.
- The applicant may, **if permitted by the judge**, present **rebuttal evidence** after the Attorney General has presented their case.

> No third parties (e.g., witnesses, experts, or family members) may testify.

---

### **Admissibility of Records**

A **duly certified transcript** of the original trial and sentencing proceedings is **admissible in evidence** at the hearing of the application.

> This ensures that the judge and jury have access to the full factual and procedural history of the case.

---

### **Judicial Ruling on Evidence**

At the hearing, the judge has the authority to:
- Rule on the **admissibility** of any evidence presented.
- Determine whether any evidence is relevant, reliable, or properly authenticated.

---

### **Dismissal Based on Non-Applicability of the Code**

At any point before or during the hearing, if the judge determines that **subsection 745(1)** of the Criminal Code **does not apply** to the applicant:
- The application is **dismissed**.
- The **jury is discharged** immediately.

> This prevents unnecessary or invalid proceedings and ensures compliance with the statutory basis for reduction.

---

### **Addressing the Jury**

After evidence is presented:
- The **applicant** addresses the jury first.
- Then, the **Attorney General** may address the jury (if they choose to do so).
- Finally, the **judge addresses the jury** after both parties have spoken.

> This structure ensures that the jury receives a balanced presentation of the applicantâ€™s case and the governmentâ€™s position.

---

### **Judicial Discretion and Additional Directions**

The judge may issue any orders or directions necessary for the **fair and proper conduct** of the hearing and its final disposition. These may include:
- **Extending or shortening the time period** for the hearing.
- Determining the **sufficiency** of the application or any affidavits.
- Directing how **documents are served or proven**.
- **Adjourning** the hearing if necessary (e.g., due to scheduling, evidence issues, or complexity).

> These powers reflect the judgeâ€™s role in ensuring procedural fairness and judicial efficiency.

---

### **Key Principles and Objectives**

1. **Fairness and Due Process**: The rules ensure that applicants have a meaningful opportunity to present their case before a jury, with full access to records and the right to rebut evidence.
2. **Procedural Clarity**: Clear requirements for filing, service, and evidence help prevent procedural errors and ensure consistency.
3. **Judicial Oversight**: The judge maintains control over the hearing process, including jury empanelment, evidence rules, and final decisions.
4. **Limitation on Participation**: Only the applicant and the Attorney General may participate, preserving the adversarial nature of the process and preventing undue influence.
5. **Statutory Compliance**: The rules are designed to strictly adhere to the provisions of **section 745 of the Criminal Code**, ensuring that reductions are only granted where legally permitted.

---

### **Conclusion**

These rules represent a well-structured, balanced, and legally grounded procedural regime for handling applications for sentence reductions in cases where the original sentence was imposed without eligibility for parole. They provide:
- A clear path for applicants to seek relief.
- A fair, transparent, and jury-based hearing process.
- Judicial discretion to manage procedural and substantive matters effectively.
- Protection against misuse or overreach by ensuring statutory compliance and procedural integrity.

The rules reflect a commitment to both **justice** and **practicality**, allowing for meaningful consideration of an applicantâ€™s circumstances while maintaining the integrity of the criminal justice system.
