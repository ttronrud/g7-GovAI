**Detailed Summary of the Regulation**

**Regulatory Authority and Context:**

The regulation is issued by Her Excellency the Governor General in Council, acting on the recommendation of the Prime Minister, under **paragraph 2(a) of the Public Service Rearrangement and Transfer of Duties Act**. This legislative authority enables the federal government to reorganize public service structures by transferring specific responsibilities, control, and supervision between departments.

The purpose of this transfer is to improve the efficiency, coherence, and strategic alignment of Canada’s international trade and investment functions. By consolidating key functions into the **Department of International Trade (DIT)**, the government aims to strengthen Canada’s ability to promote trade, attract foreign investment, and support Canadian businesses in global markets.

---

**Effective Date:**

The transfer of control and supervision becomes effective on **December 12, 2003**.

---

**Departments Involved:**

- **Source Department:** Department of Industry  
- **Receiving Department:** Department of International Trade  

---

**Specific Units Transferred:**

The following portions of the public service within the Department of Industry are transferred to the Department of International Trade:

1. **Investment Partnerships Canada**  
   - This unit was responsible for facilitating and managing public-private partnerships in international investment.  
   - It focused on identifying and supporting investment opportunities that align with Canada’s economic interests, particularly in emerging markets.  
   - The transfer ensures that investment promotion efforts are integrated within a more cohesive international trade framework.

2. **Trade Integration Directorate**  
   - This directorate was responsible for analyzing and promoting the integration of Canadian industries into global supply chains and trade networks.  
   - It supported policy development related to trade agreements, market access, and the alignment of domestic industries with international standards.  
   - Its transfer enhances the Department of International Trade’s capacity to develop and implement integrated trade and investment strategies.

3. **International Business Opportunities Centre (within the Industry Sectors Branch)**  
   - This center provided market intelligence, business development support, and opportunities for Canadian companies to access international markets.  
   - Its integration into the Department of International Trade strengthens the delivery of targeted business services to Canadian exporters and investors.

4. **International Trade Centres (within the Operations Branch)**  
   - These centres operated as regional or country-specific hubs offering support services such as market research, trade missions, and business matchmaking.  
   - Their transfer ensures that regional trade promotion activities are aligned with the broader international trade strategy and are better coordinated under a centralized department.

5. **Industrial Trade Policy Division (within the International Trade and Investment Policy Branch)**  
   - This division developed policy frameworks related to industrial trade, including sector-specific trade strategies and competitiveness initiatives.  
   - It contributed to shaping trade policies that support Canadian manufacturing and industrial sectors in global markets.

6. **Investment Portions of the International Investment and Services Division (excluding Investment Canada Act administration)**  
   - This portion focused on promoting foreign direct investment (FDI) into Canada, including investment facilitation, sectoral analysis, and investment promotion.  
   - **Important Exclusion:** The administration of the *Investment Canada Act* is explicitly **not** transferred. This exclusion ensures that regulatory oversight of foreign investments under the Investment Canada Act remains within the Department of Industry, preserving the legal and policy framework governing national security and public interest assessments.

---

**Key Objectives of the Transfer:**

- **Enhanced Coordination:** By consolidating trade and investment functions, the government aims to reduce duplication and improve coordination between trade promotion and investment policy.
- **Strategic Alignment:** The transfer supports a more unified approach to international economic engagement, ensuring that trade and investment policies are mutually reinforcing.
- **Improved Service Delivery:** Placing these units under the Department of International Trade enables better access to resources, expertise, and funding to serve Canadian businesses globally.
- **Policy Integration:** It allows for a more integrated approach to policy development, where trade policy and investment promotion are co-designed and implemented.

---

**Significance and Implications:**

- This reorganization reflects a broader shift in Canada’s approach to international economic engagement, emphasizing the interdependence of trade and investment.
- It signals a strategic realignment of federal responsibilities to better respond to global economic trends and business needs.
- The exclusion of the *Investment Canada Act* administration preserves the Department of Industry’s role in safeguarding national interests in foreign investment decisions, ensuring that regulatory oversight remains distinct from promotional activities.

---

**Conclusion:**

This regulation represents a significant reorganization within the Canadian federal public service. It transfers key functions related to international trade promotion and investment facilitation from the Department of Industry to the Department of International Trade, effective December 12, 2003. The move strengthens the government’s ability to promote Canadian businesses abroad and attract foreign investment, while maintaining critical regulatory oversight through the continued administration of the Investment Canada Act. The transfer underscores a strategic commitment to aligning trade and investment policy under a single, cohesive department.
