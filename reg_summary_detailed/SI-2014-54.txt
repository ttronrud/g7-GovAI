### üîπ **1. Legal Framework and Source**
This document is a **proclamation issued by the Governor General of Canada** on **June 3, 2014**, officially notifying the public and legal entities of the **entry into force** of a **supplementary convention** between **Canada and the Republic of Austria**.

- The original Convention was signed in **Vienna on December 9, 1976**, and amended by a Protocol on **June 15, 1999**.
- The **2012 Supplementary Convention** (also known as the "Second Protocol") amends the 1976 Convention to strengthen **information exchange mechanisms** between Canada and Austria in tax matters.
- The **interpretative protocol** clarifies the application of the information-sharing provisions, especially regarding the standard of "foreseeable relevance."

> ‚úÖ **Entry into Force**: October 1, 2013  
> ‚úÖ **Effective Date for Taxable Periods**: January 1 of the calendar year following the entry into force (i.e., January 1, 2014)

### üîπ **2. Purpose of the Supplementary Convention**
The primary objective is to **enhance cooperation between Canada and Austria** in the **exchange of tax-related information**, particularly to:
- Prevent **double taxation** of income and capital gains.
- Prevent **fiscal evasion** through the use of tax avoidance strategies.
- Improve **tax administration and enforcement** through mutual information sharing.

This amendment expands and refines the existing information-sharing rules under **Article 26** of the original Convention.

- The scope of relevant information,

- The procedural requirements for requests.

#### ‚úÖ **Paragraph 1 (Scope of Information Exchange)**
> *The competent authorities of the Contracting States shall exchange such information as is "foreseeably relevant" for carrying out the provisions of this Convention or for administering or enforcing domestic tax laws ‚Äî provided that such taxation is not contrary to the Convention.*

- **"Foreseeably relevant"** means information that could reasonably assist in assessing, collecting, enforcing, or prosecuting tax obligations.
- The exchange is **not restricted** by Articles 1 and 2 (which may otherwise limit information sharing, e.g., on personal privacy or national security).

> üîç This broadens the scope of information sharing beyond strictly financial data to include any data that could be useful in tax investigations.

#### ‚úÖ **Paragraph 2 (Treatment of Information as Secret)**
> *Information received under Paragraph 1 shall be treated as secret in the same manner as information obtained under domestic laws.*

- The receiving country (e.g., Canada receiving data from Austria) must treat the information as **confidential**.
- It may only be disclosed to:
  - Tax assessors,
  - Revenue enforcement agencies,
  - Courts,
  - Administrative bodies,
  - Or entities involved in tax appeals or oversight.

> ‚öñÔ∏è **Use Limitation**: Disclosures are strictly limited to tax-related purposes. No broader use (e.g., for commercial or political purposes) is permitted.

> üèõÔ∏è **Public Proceedings**: Information may be disclosed in **public court proceedings or judicial decisions**, as long as it is relevant and necessary.

#### ‚úÖ **Paragraph 3 (Limitations on Information Sharing)**
> *In no case shall the provisions of Paragraphs 1 and 2 be construed to require a Contracting State to:*
> 1. Carry out administrative measures inconsistent with its own laws or administrative practices;
> 2. Supply information not obtainable under domestic law or normal administrative procedures;
> 3. Disclose trade, business, industrial, commercial, or professional secrets, or information contrary to public policy (ordre public).

> üîê These are **critical safeguards**:
> - No country can be forced to take actions that violate its domestic legal framework.
> - No country must provide data that is not legally accessible or routinely gathered.
> - No country can be compelled to reveal information that would violate **commercial confidentiality** or **public policy** (e.g., national security, privacy, or trade secrets).

> ‚ö†Ô∏è **Important**: These limitations are **not absolute** ‚Äî they are exceptions to the general rule of information sharing.

#### ‚úÖ **Paragraph 4 (Mandatory Information Gathering ‚Äì Even Without Domestic Interest)**
> *If a Contracting State receives a request under this Article, the other State must use its own information-gathering measures to obtain the requested information ‚Äî even if it has no domestic tax interest in that information.*

- This means **Austria must gather information** about a Canadian taxpayer even if it is not needed for its own tax assessments.
- **Example**: If Canada requests details about a foreign bank account held by a Canadian resident, Austria must investigate and provide it ‚Äî even if it has no tax liability or interest in that account.

> ‚öñÔ∏è **Exception**: This obligation is **subject to Paragraph 3**, which limits disclosure based on legal, administrative, or public policy grounds.

> üö´ **No Excuse for Lack of Domestic Interest**: A country **cannot refuse** to provide information simply because it does not have a domestic tax interest in it.

#### ‚úÖ **Paragraph 5 (Clarification of Public Policy and Fiduciary/Ownership Issues)**
> *The provisions of Paragraph 3 shall not allow a Contracting State to decline to supply information solely because:*
> - The information is held by a **bank, financial institution, nominee, or fiduciary**;
> - Or because it relates to **ownership interests** in a person.

> üîç This ensures that **ownership structures** (e.g., trusts, nominee accounts, offshore holdings) are not used as a shield to block information sharing.

> üìå **Significance**: This prevents tax authorities from using **fiduciary or nominee arrangements** as a legal excuse to withhold information.

> üí° Example: If a Canadian resident holds shares through a nominee in Austria, Austria must still provide information about ownership, even if the nominee is acting in a fiduciary capacity.

### üîπ **4. Interpretative Protocol ‚Äì Clarifying the "Foreseeable Relevance" Standard**

This is a **separate but integral** part of the convention that provides **practical guidance** on how to interpret the information-sharing rules.

#### ‚úÖ Key Provisions:

| Requirement | Purpose |
|-----------|--------|
| **1. Identity of person under examination** | Ensures clarity about who the request is targeting (e.g., a specific individual or entity). |
| **2. Nature and form of information sought** | Specifies what data is being requested (e.g., bank statements, property records) and in what format (e.g., digital, paper). |
| **3. Tax purpose** | Explains why the information is needed (e.g., to verify income, assess capital gains). |
| **4. Grounds for belief that information exists** | The requesting country must demonstrate it has reasonable grounds to believe the information is held in Austria. |
| **5. Names and addresses of persons in possession** | If known, this helps the requested country locate the data. |
| **6. Exhaustion of domestic means** | The requesting country must show it has tried all reasonable steps within its own jurisdiction to obtain the data ‚Äî unless those steps would cause disproportionate difficulty. |

> üéØ **Purpose of "Foreseeable Relevance"**:
> - To **maximize effective tax administration**.
> - To **prevent "fishing expeditions"** ‚Äî i.e., requests that are vague, speculative, or unrelated to actual tax obligations.

> üìù **Liberal Interpretation**:
> - The standard of "foreseeable relevance" is to be interpreted **liberally** to avoid blocking legitimate tax investigations.
> - However, **requests must still be reasonable and tied to a specific tax purpose**.

> ‚ùå **No automatic exchange**: The Convention does **not require** spontaneous or automatic sharing of information. Requests must be made formally and based on a legitimate tax purpose.
