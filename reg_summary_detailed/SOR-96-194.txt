**Detailed Summary of the Base Metal Coins Regulations, 1996**

---

**Regulatory Authority and Legal Basis**  
The *Base Metal Coins Regulations, 1996* (R.S., c. 35 (3rd Supp.), s. 4) were enacted by His Excellency the Governor General in Council, acting on the recommendation of the Minister of Supply and Services. This action is grounded in **paragraph 5.2(3)(a)** of the *Royal Canadian Mint Act*. This provision grants the Minister the authority to authorize the issuance of base metal coins that deviate from the standard compositions specified in **Part II of the Schedule** to the Royal Canadian Mint Act.

This regulatory instrument serves as a formal mechanism to allow flexibility in the composition of certain Canadian coins—specifically, those made from base metals (such as copper-nickel or other non-gold or silver alloys)—when such deviations are necessary for economic, practical, or production-related reasons.

---

**Purpose of the Regulations**  
The primary purpose of these regulations is to **authorize the issuance of base metal coins with compositions different from those prescribed in the Royal Canadian Mint Act’s Schedule**. This enables the Mint to adapt coin production to market demands, cost considerations, or technological improvements without requiring a full amendment to the foundational legislation.

---

**Key Provisions**

1. **Authorized Denominations and Compositions**  
   The regulations authorize the issue of base metal coins for specific denominations listed in **Column I** of the Schedule. Each item in the Schedule specifies:
   - **Denomination** (e.g., 1 cent, 5 cents, 10 cents, etc.)
   - **Composition** (e.g., 92% copper, 8% zinc or other alloy)
   - **Standard weight** (in grams)
   - **Margin of tolerance** (the allowable deviation from the standard weight)

   These specifications define the exact parameters under which coins may be issued with non-standard compositions.

2. **Definition of Standard Weight**  
   A coin is deemed to meet the standard weight if its actual weight **does not deviate by more than the margin of tolerance** specified in Column IV of the relevant Schedule item. This tolerance allows for minor variations in manufacturing processes without disqualifying the coin from being considered valid or acceptable in circulation.

3. **Circulation and Weight Diminution Due to Use**  
   A critical provision ensures that coins in circulation **do not fall below the standard weight merely due to wear and abrasion**. Even if a coin has lost weight through normal use (e.g., handling, vending, or daily transactions), it is **not considered to have fallen below the required standard weight**. This provision protects the integrity of coins in circulation and prevents the devaluation or invalidation of coins based solely on natural wear.

---

**Effective Date**  
These regulations came into force on **July 1, 1996**. This date marks the beginning of the legal authority to issue base metal coins with non-standard compositions as outlined in the Schedule.

---

**Regulatory Citation**  
The regulations are officially cited as:  
**The Base Metal Coins Regulations, 1996**  
(R.S., c. 35 (3rd Supp.), s. 4)

---

**Significance and Context**  
This regulation reflects a practical adaptation within Canada’s monetary framework. While the *Royal Canadian Mint Act* originally prescribes specific compositions for coins (e.g., silver or gold for certain denominations), the 1996 regulations allow for the use of base metal alloys—such as copper-nickel or zinc-copper—where cost efficiency, durability, or manufacturing feasibility justifies deviation. This flexibility is essential for maintaining a modern, cost-effective currency system.

The inclusion of a tolerance margin and the protection against wear-related weight loss also demonstrates a balance between technical precision and real-world usability, ensuring that coins remain functional and acceptable in everyday commerce.

---

**Conclusion**  
The *Base Metal Coins Regulations, 1996* provide a legally sound and operationally practical framework for issuing coins with non-standard compositions. By authorizing specific denominations, compositions, weights, and tolerances, and by protecting coins from being invalidated due to normal wear, these regulations support both the operational efficiency of the Royal Canadian Mint and the stability of Canada’s currency system. They remain a key component of Canada’s monetary policy, enabling adaptability while preserving public confidence in coinage.
