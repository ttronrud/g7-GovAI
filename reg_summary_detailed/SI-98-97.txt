**Detailed Summary of the Regulation:**

**Title:**  
Proclamation Declaring "Police and Peace Officers’ National Memorial Day"

**Issuing Authority:**  
Her Majesty Queen Elizabeth II, by and with the advice of the Privy Council for Canada, acting through the Governor General of Canada.

**Date of Proclamation:**  
September 24, 1998  
**Effective Date:** September 24, 1998  
**Location:** Government House, Ottawa, Canada

---

### **Purpose and Background:**

This proclamation establishes an official national day in Canada to honor and recognize the service, dedication, and sacrifices of Canadian police and peace officers. The regulation is rooted in the following key reasons:

1. **Recognition of Service:**  
   Canadian police and peace officers play a vital role in maintaining public safety, upholding the law, and serving communities. The proclamation acknowledges their significant contributions to national well-being and public order.

2. **Commemoration of Fallen Officers:**  
   Each year, memorial services are held across Canada on the last Sunday of September to honor police and peace officers who have died in the line of duty. This day serves as a formal and national tribute to their bravery, professionalism, and ultimate sacrifice.

3. **Legal Foundation:**  
   The proclamation builds upon earlier administrative action—specifically, Order in Council P.C. 1998-1703, issued on September 24, 1998—by the Governor General in Council. This order formally directed the issuance of a proclamation to designate the last Sunday in September as a national memorial day.

---

### **Key Provisions of the Proclamation:**

- **Designation of the Day:**  
  The last Sunday in September of each year is officially declared as **"Police and Peace Officers’ National Memorial Day"**.

- **Purpose of the Day:**  
  The day is intended to:
  - Honor and pay tribute to the hard work, dedication, and personal sacrifices made by police and peace officers.
  - Commemorate those officers who have lost their lives while serving in the line of duty.
  - Promote public appreciation for the role of law enforcement in Canadian society.

- **Legal Effect:**  
  The proclamation is binding on all persons and entities within Canada. It requires all Canadians and relevant institutions to take notice of the day and to govern themselves accordingly in observance of it.

---

### **Legal and Constitutional Context:**

- The proclamation is issued under the authority of the **Governor General in Council**, who acts on the advice of the federal Cabinet (Privy Council). This reflects the constitutional framework of Canada, where the Governor General serves as the ceremonial head of state and executes legislative and ceremonial duties on the advice of the elected government.

- The use of the Great Seal of Canada affixes the proclamation with formal legal and symbolic weight, signifying its official status as a national policy.

---

### **Signatory and Seal:**

- **Signed by:**  
  Roméo A. LeBlanc, Governor General of Canada at the time.  
  He held several honors, including:
  - Member of the Privy Council (PC)
  - Chancellor of the Order of Canada
  - Commander of the Order of Military Merit

- **Seal:**  
  The Great Seal of Canada is affixed, confirming the authenticity and legal standing of the proclamation.

---

### **Significance and Impact:**

- This day serves as a **national moment of reflection**, reinforcing public respect for law enforcement.
- It strengthens community ties through shared remembrance and provides a structured opportunity for ceremonies, educational initiatives, and public outreach.
- The day is not a statutory holiday, but it is widely observed through events, memorials, and official statements by government and police services.

---

### **Conclusion:**

This proclamation is a formal, constitutionally grounded, and respectful recognition of the essential role of police and peace officers in Canadian society. By designating the last Sunday in September as "Police and Peace Officers’ National Memorial Day," Canada establishes a permanent, annual tradition to honor both the living and the fallen, fostering a culture of gratitude, respect, and remembrance.

It reflects the nation’s commitment to acknowledging the courage and service of public servants who protect the safety and peace of Canadians every day.
