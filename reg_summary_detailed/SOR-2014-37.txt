## ðŸ“š **Detailed Summary of the Transportation Safety Board Regulations (2014)**

### ðŸ”¹ **Overview**
These regulations, adopted by the **Canadian Transportation Accident Investigation and Safety Board (CTAISB)** and approved by the Governor in Council on January 10, 2014, establish mandatory reporting obligations for key transportation sectors: **aviation, marine (shipping), and pipeline operations**. The purpose is to ensure timely and comprehensive information is available to the Board to investigate incidents and accidents that pose risks to safety, human life, property, or the environment.

The regulations were developed following a public consultation process, with a proposed version published in the *Canada Gazette, Part I*, on September 3, 2011, allowing stakeholders to submit written representations.

## ðŸ›©ï¸ III. Aviation Occurrence Reporting Requirements

### âœ… Who Must Report?
The following individuals must report aviation occurrences:
- Owner
- Operator
- Pilot-in-command
- Any crew member
- Any person providing air traffic services with direct knowledge of the occurrence

### âœ… When Must Reporting Occur?
Reporting is required **if the occurrence results directly from the operation of an aircraft** and meets one of the following criteria:

#### ðŸ”¹ **Accidents** (Require immediate reporting)
1. A person is killed or sustains a **serious injury** due to:
   - Being on board the aircraft
   - Coming into direct contact with any part of the aircraft (including detached parts)
   - Direct exposure to jet blast, rotor downwash, or propeller wash
2. The aircraft sustains **structural failure or damage** that:
   - Adversely affects structural strength, performance, or flight characteristics
   - Requires major repair or replacement of components  
   *(Exemptions apply for engine failure limited to engine, cowlings, accessories; or minor damage to propellers, tires, fairings, skin dents/punctures)*
3. The aircraft is **missing or inaccessible**

#### ðŸ”¹ **Incidents** (Require reporting for aircraft >2,250 kg or operating under Part VII of Canadian Aviation Regulations)
These include:
- Engine failure or precautionary shutdown
- Power train transmission gearbox malfunction
- Smoke or fire detected on board
- Difficulties in controlling the aircraft due to:
  - System malfunctions
  - Weather phenomena
  - Wake turbulence
  - Uncontrolled vibrations
  - Operations outside flight envelope
- Aircraft fails to remain within intended landing/take-off area
- Lands with landing gear retracted or drags wing tip, engine pod, or other part
- Crew member physically incapacitated and unable to perform duties (threatens safety)
- Depressurization requiring emergency descent
- Fuel shortage requiring diversion or priority landing
- Refueling with incorrect or contaminated fuel
- Collision, risk of collision, or loss of separation
- Crew declares emergency requiring priority handling
- Unintentional or precautionary release of slung load
- Any **dangerous goods released** in or from the aircraft
