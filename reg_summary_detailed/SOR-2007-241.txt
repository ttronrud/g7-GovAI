**Detailed Summary of the First Nations Assessment Appeal Regulations (S.C. 2005, c. 9)**

---

### **Overview**
The *First Nations Assessment Appeal Regulations* (S.C. 2005, c. 9) were made by Her Excellency the Governor General in Council, on the recommendation of the Minister of Indian Affairs and Northern Development, under specific provisions of the *First Nations Fiscal and Statistical Management Act*. These regulations establish a clear, structured, and fair process for property assessment appeals within First Nations communities in Canada.

The regulations ensure that First Nations have the right to appeal property assessments in a manner consistent with provincial standards while maintaining independence, transparency, and procedural fairness. They provide a legal framework that aligns First Nations property assessment practices with those of provincial governments, while respecting the unique governance and self-determination of First Nations.

---

## **Key Definitions**

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Assessable Property** | Includes reserve land, interests in reserve land, or rights to occupy, possess, or use such land that are subject to assessment under a property assessment law. |
| **Assessor** | The individual designated by a First Nation to conduct assessments of assessable property. |
| **Complainant** | A person who initiates an appeal under Section 7 of the regulations. |
| **Party** | Refers to any of the following: the complainant, the assessor, or the tax administrator. |
| **Property Assessment Law** | A law enacted under subparagraph 5(1)(a)(i) of the *First Nations Fiscal Management Act*. This law governs how property is assessed and appealed. |
| **Tax Administrator** | The official responsible for administering the property assessment laws within a First Nation. |

---

## **Core Provisions and Key Requirements**

### **1. Incorporation of Provincial Assessment Procedures**
- A First Nation’s property assessment law **must incorporate procedures** similar to those used in the province where the assessable property is located, specifically those outlined in **Sections 3 to 13** of the provincial assessment legislation.
- This ensures consistency and fairness by aligning First Nation assessment practices with those of the broader provincial system.

> *Purpose:* Promotes equity and comparability across jurisdictions.

---

### **2. Right to Request Reconsideration**
- Any person listed on the assessment roll may request that the **assessor reconsider** the assessment of their property.
- The reassessment process must include a **minimum period of 21 days** for the assessor to review the matter.

> *Implication:* Provides a mechanism for correction of errors before formal appeal, promoting early resolution and reducing the burden on review boards.

---

### **3. Notification of Modified Assessments**
- If the assessor modifies an assessment after reconsideration:
  - The **notice of the modified assessment** must be sent to:
    - The **tax administrator**
    - Any other person who originally received the notice of the initial assessment

> *Purpose:* Ensures transparency and timely communication of changes to all relevant parties.

---

### **4. Right to Appeal to an Assessment Review Board**
- Any person (the complainant) may appeal an assessment or a reconsideration of an assessment to a **First Nation-established Assessment Review Board**.
- Appeals must be initiated by delivering a **notice of appeal** to the assessor.

> *Grounds for Appeal:* Must include:
> - Full name and mailing address of the complainant and any representative
> - Description of the assessable property (including assessment roll number)
> - Specific grounds for the appeal

> *Fees:* Any required fee must be paid with the notice of appeal.

---

### **5. Composition of the Assessment Review Board**
- The board must consist of **at least three members**, one of whom is the **chairperson**.
- **Mandatory qualifications**:
  - At least **one member must be a lawyer** (practising or non-practising) in good standing with the **law society of the province** where the property is located.
  - At least **one other member must have experience in assessment appeals** within that province.
- **Exclusion criteria**:
  - No member may have a **personal or financial interest** in the assessable property involved in the appeal.
  - No member may be a **chief or member of the council of the First Nation**.
- *Note:* Membership in the First Nation itself does **not** constitute a personal or financial interest.

> *Purpose:* Ensures impartiality, legal expertise, and local knowledge while preventing conflicts of interest.

---

### **6. Time Limits for Appeals**
- If a property assessment law sets a time limit for appeals, that period ends **no earlier than 45 days after the notice of assessment is mailed** to the person on the roll.

> *Purpose:* Protects the complainant’s right to appeal without undue delay.

---

### **7. Commencement of Appeal**
- An appeal is formally commenced when the **notice of appeal is delivered to the assessor** at the address listed in the assessment notice.

---

### **8. Scheduling of Hearings**
- The **chairperson of the review board**, in consultation with the assessor, must schedule a hearing.
- A **written notice of the hearing date, time, and place** must be delivered to:
  - All parties (complainant, assessor, tax administrator)
  - All persons named on the assessment roll for the property in question
- This notice must be sent **at least 10 days before the hearing begins**.

> *Purpose:* Ensures timely, transparent, and accessible proceedings.

---

### **9. Timeline for Hearing Commencement**
- The review board must begin the hearing **within 45 days** of receiving the notice of appeal.
- This deadline can be extended **only with mutual consent** of all parties.

> *Purpose:* Prevents unreasonable delays in resolving appeals.

---

### **10. Court Intervention During Hearings**
If a **tax liability proceeding** is ongoing in a court of competent jurisdiction regarding the same property:

- The hearing is **deferred** until the court decision is made.
- The hearing is **adjourned** during the court proceeding.
- If the hearing has concluded but the court decision has not yet been rendered, the **decision of the review board is deferred** until the court ruling.

> *Purpose:* Resolves conflicts between administrative and judicial processes and avoids contradictory outcomes.

---

### **11. Consolidated Hearings**
- The review board may conduct a **single hearing** for **two or more appeals** related to the same assessment roll **if the issues raised are the same or substantially similar**.

> *Purpose:* Increases efficiency and reduces administrative costs.

---

### **12. Document Sharing**
- The **chairperson must promptly deliver** any document submitted by a party to all other parties involved in the appeal.

> *Purpose:* Ensures transparency and equal access to information.

---

### **13. Delivery of Final Decision**
- After the hearing, the review board must deliver a **written decision** to all parties **at the earliest opportunity** after the hearing concludes.
- The assessor must then **update the assessment roll** accordingly.

> *Purpose:* Ensures timely and enforceable outcomes.

---

### **14. Decision Period (Time Limit for Review Board Rulings)**
- Despite general principles, a property assessment law may establish a **time limit** for the review board to issue a decision.
- This period must end **no earlier than 90 days after the hearing concludes**.

> *Purpose:* Provides a reasonable deadline for resolution, balancing efficiency with due process.

---

### **15. Methods of Document Delivery**
Documents may be delivered in several ways:

| Method | Delivery Rule |
|-------|---------------|
| **Personal Delivery** | - To individuals: left with the person or with a person ≥18 years old residing at the individual’s home.<br> - To First Nations: left with the person in charge of administrative office or legal counsel.<br> - To corporations: left with person in charge of head office/branch or officer/director or legal counsel. |
| **Registered Mail** | Considered delivered on the **fifth day after mailing**. |
| **Fax** | Considered delivered at the time indicated in the **transmission confirmation**. |
| **Email** | Considered delivered at the time the email is **opened** (confirmed). |

> *Exception:*  
> If delivery occurs on a **non-business day** or **after 17:00 on a business day**, it is considered delivered at **09:00 on the next business day**.

> *Purpose:* Ensures reliable, legally recognized delivery of documents.

---

### **16. Right to Judicial Review**
- A party may appeal a **decision of the assessment review board** to a **court of competent jurisdiction** on a **question of law**.
- This appeal must be filed **within 30 days** of the decision being delivered.

> *Purpose:* Provides a final check on legal errors and ensures that decisions are grounded in sound legal principles.

---

### **17. Effective Date**
- These regulations **come into force on the day they are registered** in the Canada Gazette.

---

## **Key Principles and Objectives**

| Principle | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Equity & Fairness** | Aligns First Nations assessment procedures with provincial standards to ensure fairness and consistency. |
| **Transparency** | Requires public notice of hearings, sharing of documents, and clear timelines. |
| **Impartiality** | Requires board members to have legal and assessment experience and to avoid conflicts of interest. |
| **Access to Justice** | Provides clear pathways for appeal, including judicial review. |
| **Self-Governance**
