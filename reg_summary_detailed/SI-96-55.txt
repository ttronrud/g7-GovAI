**Detailed Summary of the Canadian Regulation: Proclamation of National Aboriginal Day (1996)**

---

### **1. Legal and Official Context**
This document is an **official proclamation** issued under the authority of the **Governor General in Council**, acting on the recommendation of the **Minister of Indian Affairs and Northern Development**. It was formally published on **June 13, 1996**, and came into effect on **June 21, 1996**, as the first official observance of the newly established holiday.

The proclamation is issued under the **Constitution of Canada**, which recognizes the rights of Aboriginal peoples. It reflects a constitutional commitment to acknowledging and honoring Indigenous peoples as distinct and integral to Canadian identity.

---

### **2. Key Provisions and Objectives**

#### **a. Recognition of Aboriginal Peoples**
- The proclamation explicitly identifies **Aboriginal peoples of Canada** as including:
  - **Indigenous (Indian) peoples**
  - **Inuit**
  - **Métis**
- This classification aligns with the constitutional recognition of Aboriginal rights and title established in the **Constitution Act, 1982**, particularly under Section 35, which recognizes and affirms the existing rights of Aboriginal peoples.

#### **b. Purpose of the Proclamation**
The primary objectives are:
- To **mark and celebrate** the valuable contributions of Aboriginal peoples to Canadian society.
- To **recognize and honor** the diverse cultures, traditions, and histories of Indigenous communities.
- To promote **cultural awareness, respect, and reconciliation** between Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal Canadians.

#### **c. Selection of June 21 as National Aboriginal Day**
- The date, **June 21**, was chosen because it corresponds to the **summer solstice** — the longest day of the year in the Northern Hemisphere.
- The summer solstice holds **significant cultural and spiritual importance** in many Indigenous traditions across Canada, symbolizing renewal, strength, and the connection between people, land, and nature.
- This date serves as a meaningful and unifying moment to celebrate Indigenous heritage and values.

---

### **3. Legal Authority and Process**
- The proclamation was approved by **His Excellency the Governor General**, who is the representative of the Canadian Crown and exercises executive authority on behalf of the Queen.
- The decision was made **on the recommendation of the Minister of Indian Affairs and Northern Development**, reflecting the government’s responsibility for Indigenous affairs.
- The Governor General in Council has the constitutional authority to issue proclamations that establish national observances, especially when they align with constitutional values and national identity.

---

### **4. Scope and Application**
- The proclamation applies to **all persons and institutions in Canada**.
- It is a **national observance**, meaning that all levels of government, schools, businesses, and the public are encouraged to participate in activities that celebrate Aboriginal culture.
- The proclamation does **not impose legal obligations** but rather encourages **cultural recognition, education, and public engagement**.

---

### **5. Historical and Cultural Significance**
- This proclamation was a significant step in Canada’s journey toward **Indigenous reconciliation**.
- It marked the **first official recognition of a national day dedicated to Aboriginal peoples**, setting a precedent for future initiatives such as **National Day for Truth and Reconciliation** (established later in 2021).
- It acknowledged the **historical contributions** of Aboriginal peoples — including their roles in shaping Canadian laws, economies, arts, and governance — and sought to correct historical marginalization.

---

### **6. Language and Tone**
- The tone is formal, respectful, and inclusive, reflecting the dignity and importance of Indigenous peoples in Canadian society.
- The use of terms such as “valuable contributions,” “different cultures,” and “spiritual symbolism” emphasizes both the practical and cultural value of Aboriginal peoples.

---

### **7. Limitations and Later Developments**
- While this 1996 proclamation established **National Aboriginal Day**, it was **not widely observed** at the federal level in the same way as other national holidays.
- In **2001**, the federal government formally recognized **National Aboriginal Day** as a **non-statutory observance** (not a public holiday).
- In **2015**, the federal government renamed and restructured the observance as **National Day for Truth and Reconciliation**, in response to the legacy of residential schools and the need for deeper reconciliation.
- As of today, **June 21 is recognized as a day of cultural celebration**, but it is **not a public holiday** in Canada. It is often observed in schools, communities, and events that highlight Indigenous culture, language, and history.

---

### **8. Conclusion**
The 1996 Proclamation of National Aboriginal Day was a landmark moment in Canadian history. It:
- Legally recognized Aboriginal peoples as central to Canada’s identity.
- Established a national day of cultural celebration rooted in Indigenous traditions.
- Provided a foundation for ongoing efforts toward reconciliation and cultural respect.

Although the original observance has evolved, the proclamation remains a **symbolic and foundational document** in Canada’s commitment to honoring and integrating Indigenous peoples into the national narrative.

---

**Note**: This proclamation is a **historical document** and should be understood in the context of evolving Indigenous rights and reconciliation efforts in Canada. It reflects the state of Indigenous recognition in the 1990s and laid the groundwork for future policy developments.
