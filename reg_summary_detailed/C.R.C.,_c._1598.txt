### **Detailed Summary of the Visiting Forces Regulations**

The **Visiting Forces Regulations** are a set of administrative rules designed to govern the legal jurisdiction and enforcement of offences committed by members of **foreign armed forces** (referred to as "visiting forces") while they are stationed in Canada. These regulations operate under the broader framework of the **Visiting Forces Act (VFA)** and aim to balance the need for legal accountability with the operational and diplomatic realities of hosting foreign military personnel.

Below is a **comprehensive, structured, and detailed summary** of the key provisions, their purposes, legal mechanisms, and practical implications.

---

## **1. Title and Citation**
- The regulations are officially titled:  
  **"The Visiting Forces Regulations"**  
  and may be cited as such.

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------|
| **Act** | Refers to the *Visiting Forces Act* (Loi sur les forces visitantes) ‚Äî the enabling legislation that provides the legal basis for these regulations. |
| **Attorney General** | Defined differently depending on the jurisdiction: <br> - In the **Northwest Territories or Yukon**: The Attorney General of **Canada** or their deputy.<br> - In all other provinces: The Attorney General of the **provincial government** or their deputy. |
| **Service Court** | A military court established by a foreign country to try members of its armed forces. |
| **Civil Court** | A regular provincial or territorial court system that handles civil and criminal matters under Canadian law. |

## **3. Waiver of Civil Court Jurisdiction by the Attorney General**

### **Purpose**
To allow foreign military personnel to be tried in **service courts** (rather than Canadian civil courts) when accused of committing an offence under Canadian law.

### **When Can a Waiver Be Made?**
- **No information has been laid** against the member before the civil court:
  - The Attorney General may waive the civil court‚Äôs primary jurisdiction **by sending a signed waiver** (Form 1) to the **officer in command** of the visiting force.
- **Information has already been laid**:
  - The waiver must be sent **to the presiding judge, magistrate, or justice** of the civil court, **alongside** the original waiver (Form 1).

### **Conditions for Waiver**
- The waiver applies **only to offences** that constitute a breach of a law in force in Canada.
- **No waiver is permitted** after a civil court has already made a final adjudication (judicial decision) regarding the offence.
- **No prior request is required** to empower the Attorney General to issue a waiver.
- The officer in command of the visiting force may **submit a request** (Form 2) explaining why a waiver is needed ‚Äî though it is not mandatory.

> üîç **Key Principle**: This mechanism allows for **dual jurisdiction flexibility**, ensuring that foreign military personnel are not subjected to Canadian civilian courts for offences that may be better handled in their own military justice systems.

## **4. Waiver of Service Court Jurisdiction in Favor of Civil Courts**

### **Applicable Offences**
This waiver applies **only** to offences described in **paragraphs 6(2)(a) to (c)** of the Visiting Forces Act. These include:
- Offences involving **violence or threats to public safety**,
- Offences involving **disorderly conduct**, or
- Offences involving **breach of peace or public order**.

> ‚ö†Ô∏è This is a **limited exception** ‚Äî service courts do not have automatic jurisdiction over these specific types of offences.

### **Who Can Waive?**
- The **officer in command** of the visiting force may initiate the waiver by:
  - Completing and sending a signed form (Form 3) to the **Attorney General**.

### **Conditions**
- No formal request is required from the officer to initiate this waiver.
- However, the **Attorney General may submit a request** (Form 4) to the officer, explaining the reasons for wanting to transfer jurisdiction to a civil court.

> üí° This provision ensures that in cases involving serious public order violations, Canadian civil courts can step in to ensure accountability and public safety.

## **5. Request for Incarceration in Canadian Prisons (Section 11 of the Act)**

### **When Is This Request Made?**
When a member of a visiting force is **sentenced to incarceration** by a service court, and the officer in command requests that the sentence be served in a Canadian prison (penitentiary, civil prison, service prison, or detention barrack).

### **Requirements of the Request (Form 5)**
- Must be in **Form 5** or equivalent.
- Must include:
  1. **Reasons** for requesting incarceration in Canada.
  2. **Nature of the place** where incarceration will occur under the laws of the foreign state.
  3. A **financial undertaking** from the **government of the visiting state** to:
     - Reimburse **the Government of Canada** for rations if the inmate is held in a Canadian government-operated prison (penitentiary, service prison, or detention barrack).
     - Reimburse the **provincial government** if the inmate is held in a provincial prison.
  4. Must be **signed by the officer in command** of the visiting force.

### **Decision-Making Authority**
- The **Minister of National Defence** has **sole discretion** to decide whether to grant the request.
- The Minister may **deny, approve, or modify** the request based on national security, public interest, and diplomatic considerations.

### **Notification and Appointment as Committing Authority**
- If the Minister approves the request:
  - They issue a **written notification** (Form 6) to the officer in command.
  - The notification includes:
    - The **designated prison** where incarceration will occur.
    - The **appointment of the officer in command** as a **"committing authority"** under **section 187 of the National Defence Act**.

> üìå This gives the officer in command the legal power to **commit the individual to prison** under the authority of the Canadian government.

---

## **6. Committal Order (Form 7)**

- A formal legal document issued by the officer in command upon approval of the Minister.
- Must be in **Form 7** or equivalent.
- Serves as the **official order** authorizing the transfer of the individual into custody at the designated prison.
- It is legally binding and issued under the authority of **section 187 of the National Defence Act**.

---

## **7. Police Powers of Foreign Military Personnel**

### **Authority**
A member of a visiting force may exercise **police functions** (including the **power of arrest**) in Canada **as permitted by the laws of their home country**.

> ‚úÖ This is a **limited delegation** ‚Äî only powers granted by the foreign state‚Äôs law are recognized in Canada.

> ‚öñÔ∏è This provision respects the sovereignty of the visiting force while ensuring that such powers are exercised **within the bounds of Canadian law and public order**.

---

## **8. Subpoena for Witnesses (Form 8)**

### **When Can a Subpoena Be Issued?**
- When a **witness is likely to give material evidence** in a **court martial** of the visiting force.

### **Requirements of the Subpoena (Form 8)**
- Must be signed by a **magistrate or justice**.
- If the witness is **outside the province** where the court martial is held, the subpoena **cannot be issued without prior approval** from:
  - A **county or district court judge**, or
  - A **superior court judge of criminal jurisdiction** (as defined in the *Criminal Code*).

### **Compensation for Witnesses**
- A witness who appears before a court martial is entitled to receive:
  - The **same fees and allowances** as if they had appeared in a proceeding under the *Criminal Code* of Canada.

> üéØ This ensures **fairness and consistency** in the treatment of witnesses across both Canadian and military justice systems.

## **9. Forms and Templates (Schedules)**

The regulations include specific forms (set out in the schedule) for each procedural step. These are designed to ensure clarity, consistency, and legal validity in all actions:

| Form | Purpose |
|------|--------|
| **Form 1** | Waiver by Attorney General in favour of service courts. |
| **Form 2** | Request by officer in command for waiver of civil court jurisdiction. |
| **Form 3** | Waiver by officer in command in favour of civil courts (for specific offences). |
| **Form 4** | Request by Attorney General for waiver of service court jurisdiction. |
| **Form 5** | Request for incarceration in a Canadian prison. |
| **Form 6** | Notification by Minister of National Defence of prison designation and appointment of committing authority. |
| **Form 7** | Committal order issued by officer in command. |
| **Form 8** | Subpoena for witness attendance. |

> üìù All forms must be completed and signed appropriately to be legally valid.
