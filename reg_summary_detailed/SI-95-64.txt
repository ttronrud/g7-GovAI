**Detailed Summary of the Canada National Mining Week Proclamation (1995)**

---

### **1. Title and Legal Authority**
The document is an official **Proclamation** issued under the authority of **Queen Elizabeth II**, acting through the **Governor General of Canada**, in consultation with the **Privy Council for Canada**. It is formally titled:  
> *"A Proclamation Designating the Week Commencing on the Second Monday of May Every Year as 'National Mining Week'"*.

This proclamation was issued on **May 12, 1995**, and is legally binding under the constitutional framework of Canada, where the Governor General, acting on the advice of the federal Cabinet (Privy Council), has the authority to issue such proclamations.

---

### **2. Purpose and Rationale**
The proclamation establishes **National Mining Week** as an annual observance in Canada, grounded in several key reasons:

- **Historical and Economic Contribution**:  
  Mining has played a significant role in Canada’s development, both historically and economically. The industry has contributed to infrastructure, population growth, and national prosperity.

- **Economic Significance of Minerals and Metals**:  
  Minerals and metals extracted in Canada are vital to the country’s economy and international trade. These resources support domestic industries, export revenues, and technological advancement.

- **Sustainable Development and Job Creation**:  
  The proclamation emphasizes the importance of a **prosperous, sustainable mining industry**. Such an industry continues to be essential for:
  - Creating jobs across rural and remote communities.
  - Enhancing the economic well-being of all Canadians.
  - Promoting long-term economic resilience through responsible resource management.

---

### **3. Key Provision**
The central legal and practical component of the proclamation is the **designation of a specific week each year** as **National Mining Week**:

> The week **commencing on the second Monday of May** every year is officially designated as **National Mining Week**.

This means:
- The observance occurs annually, starting on the second Monday of May.
- It is a fixed, recurring event, ensuring consistency and predictability in public awareness and industry engagement.
- The timing aligns with the early spring season, potentially allowing for coordination with educational, community, and industry events.

---

### **4. Scope and Application**
The proclamation applies to:
- **All Canadians**, including citizens, residents, and stakeholders.
- **Government agencies**, industry representatives, educational institutions, and community organizations.
- **All persons or entities to whom these presents may concern** — meaning any individual or group affected by or involved in the mining sector or its broader economic and social impacts.

It mandates that all relevant parties **take notice** of the designation and **govern themselves accordingly**, implying a call to action for public engagement, education, and celebration of the mining sector.

---

### **5. Official Seal and Authentication**
The proclamation is authenticated with:
- The **Great Seal of Canada**, signifying its official status and legal validity.
- The signature of **Roméo A. LeBlanc**, who served as:
  - Governor General of Canada (at the time),
  - A member of the Privy Council,
  - Chancellor of the Order of Canada,
  - Commander of the Order of Military Merit.

This underscores the high-level governmental endorsement and recognition of the importance of the mining sector in Canada’s national identity and economic strategy.

---

### **6. Historical Context and Significance**
- The proclamation was issued in **1995**, a period when Canada was actively promoting economic diversification and sustainable resource development.
- It followed a prior **Order in Council P.C. 1995-722**, dated May 2, 1995, which authorized the issuance of the proclamation.
- The establishment of National Mining Week reflects a broader national effort to:
  - Recognize the contributions of the mining industry.
  - Foster public understanding and appreciation of Canada’s natural resources.
  - Encourage dialogue between industry, government, and communities on responsible mining practices.

---

### **7. Legacy and Impact**
Although the original 1995 proclamation is a historical document, its designation has had lasting significance:
- It laid the foundation for ongoing public awareness campaigns about mining.
- It has been used as a platform for:
  - Educational outreach in schools.
  - Community events, tours, and exhibitions.
  - Industry engagement and policy discussions on sustainability and innovation.
- The observance has evolved over time, with modern iterations often emphasizing **sustainability**, **indigenous rights**, **environmental stewardship**, and **community benefits**.

---

### **Conclusion**
This proclamation is a formal, constitutionally grounded declaration by the Canadian government to recognize and celebrate the vital role of the mining industry in Canada’s economic and social fabric. By designating the week beginning on the second Monday of May as **National Mining Week**, the government affirms the enduring value of mining to national prosperity, job creation, and sustainable development. It serves both as a tribute to history and as a call to future-focused, responsible resource management.

---

**Note**: While this proclamation is from 1995, it remains relevant today as Canada continues to develop its mining sector with a focus on innovation, environmental responsibility, and inclusive economic growth. National Mining Week is often observed in alignment with modern industry initiatives and public education programs.
