**Detailed Summary of the CPAFTA Verification of Origin Regulations**

---

### **Overview**

The **CPAFTA Verification of Origin Regulations** are a set of rules designed to implement and operationalize the provisions of **Customs Notice 13-006**, issued on **March 22, 2013**, under the *Customs Act* (the "Act"). These regulations provide a detailed framework for verifying the **origin of goods** that are eligible for **preferential tariff treatment under the Canada–Panama Free Trade Agreement (CPAFTA)**.

The regulations came into force on **April 1, 2013**, as specified in the public announcement and subsequent legislative action.

These rules are developed under the authority of specific sections of the *Customs Act*, including:
- Section 42.1a
- Paragraphs 164(1)(i)b and (j)
- Subsection 164(1.1)c
- Paragraph 167.1(b)d

They are also supported by relevant statutory provisions from earlier legislation (e.g., S.C. 2009, c. 6, s. 24; S.C. 1992, c. 28, s. 30(1), etc.), reflecting the legal continuity and foundation of the customs regime.

---

## **Key Definitions**

The regulations establish clear definitions of key terms used throughout:

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Act** | Refers to the *Customs Act* of Canada. |
| **Customs Administration** | The governmental body responsible for administering customs laws—either in Canada or Panama, depending on the jurisdiction. |
| **Goods** | Goods for which preferential tariff treatment under CPAFTA is claimed. |
| **Material** | A component or part used in the production of another good; includes raw materials, parts, or ingredients. |
| **Verification Letter** | A formal communication requesting information about the origin of goods under a verification process. |
| **Verification Questionnaire** | A structured form requesting origin-related details from exporters or producers. |
| **Verification Visit** | A physical inspection of premises conducted by a customs officer to verify the origin of goods under paragraph 42.1(1)(a) of the Act. |

---

## **Methods of Origin Verification**

A verification of origin may be conducted through **one or more of the following methods**:

1. **Verification Visit**  
   A physical inspection of premises where goods or materials are produced or stored.

2. **Verification Questionnaire**  
   A written form completed by:
   - The exporter or producer of the goods, or  
   - A producer or supplier of a material.

3. **Written Response to a Verification Letter**  
   A formal reply to a letter requesting origin information.

4. **Other Relevant Information**  
   Any additional documentation or data provided by the relevant party.

> **Note**: All of these methods are used to verify whether goods qualify for preferential tariff treatment under CPAFTA.

---

## **Requirements for Verification Letters and Questionnaires**

Every verification letter or questionnaire must include the following elements:

- The **name and title** of the customs officer sending it.
- A clear **description of the goods or materials** being verified.
- A **specific deadline** for response, which must be **at least 30 days** from the date of receipt.

### **Request for Extension**

- A recipient may request a **single extension** of the deadline.
- The extension is limited to **no more than 30 additional days**.
- The request must be submitted **within the specified period**.
- The extension is **at the discretion of the customs administration**.

---

## **Prescribed Premises in Panama**

The following **Panamanian entities** are designated as **prescribed premises** for verification visits:

- An **exporter or producer** of goods;
- A **producer or supplier** of a material.

> This means that inspections can be conducted at the physical locations of these parties in Panama.

---

## **Conditions for Conducting a Verification Visit**

An officer may only carry out a verification visit if **all of the following conditions are met**:

1. A **written notice** of intent to conduct the visit is sent to:
   - The person whose premises are to be inspected;
   - The **Panama customs administration**;
   - (If requested by Panama) The **Canadian Embassy in Panama**.

2. The person whose premises are to be visited must **consent in writing** to the visit.

---

## **Content of the Verification Visit Notice**

The notice must specify:

- Identity of the customs administration sending the notice;
- Name and title of the officer conducting the visit;
- Name of the person whose premises are being visited;
- Date and location of the visit;
- Object and scope of the visit (including description of goods/materials involved);
- Legal authority under which the visit is being conducted.

---

## **Right to Postpone a Verification Visit**

A person receiving the notice may:

- Request a **postponement** within **15 days** of receiving the notice;
- The postponement must not exceed **60 days** from the date of the original notice;
- The request must be submitted in writing to the officer.

> **Exception**: If the **Panama customs administration** requests a postponement within 15 days of receiving the notice, the visit is automatically postponed, subject to the same 60-day limit (or longer, only if agreed upon jointly by Canada and Panama).

---

## **Right to Designate Observers**

- A person may designate **two observers** to attend the verification visit.
- Observers are **only permitted to observe** and may not participate in the verification process.
- The observers must be **identified in writing** to the officer conducting the visit.

> This ensures transparency and allows for independent oversight of the inspection process.

---

## **Recordkeeping and Access Requirements**

Under **subsection 42.1(2)** of the *Customs Act*, the **exporter and producer** are required to:

1. **Maintain records** relevant to the origin of goods, in accordance with the laws of the country where the verification is conducted (Canada or Panama);
2. **Allow access** to those records by the customs officer during the verification process;
3. **Respond promptly** to verification letters or complete and return verification questionnaires within the timeframes specified (minimum 30 days).

> This ensures that origin information is accurate, verifiable, and traceable.

---

## **Consent to Visit**

- Written consent to the verification visit must be provided **within 30 days** of receiving the notice under paragraph 6(1)(a)(i).
- Failure to provide timely consent may result in the visit being denied or postponed.

---

## **Redetermination of Origin (Rejection of Preferential Treatment)**

If a customs officer determines that goods **do not meet the origin requirements** under CPAFTA, they must:

- Send a **written notice** to the exporter or producer;
- State that the goods are **not eligible** for preferential tariff treatment;
- Specify the **effective date** from which the preferential treatment is denied or withdrawn;
- Provide a **period during which** the exporter or producer may submit written comments or additional information regarding the origin.

> This allows for a formal appeal or clarification process before any adverse determination is finalized.

---

## **Delivery and Receipt Confirmation**

All verification letters, questionnaires, and notices (including those related to visits) must be sent via a method that provides **confirmation of receipt** (e.g., email, registered mail, courier with tracking).

> This ensures accountability and helps prevent disputes over whether a document was received.

---

## **Effective Date**

These regulations **came into force on April 1, 2013**, as stated in the original public announcement and legislative action.

---

## **Purpose and Significance**

The CPAFTA Verification of Origin Regulations serve several critical functions:

1. **Ensures Compliance**: Verifies that goods claiming preferential tariff treatment under CPAFTA are genuinely produced or sourced in Panama (or meet the required origin criteria).
2. **Promotes Fair Trade**: Prevents fraud or misuse of preferential treatment by requiring verifiable documentation and physical inspections.
3. **Strengthens Bilateral Relations**: Establishes a transparent, cooperative, and mutually respectful framework between Canada and Panama for customs administration.
4. **Provides Legal Clarity**: Offers clear procedures for all stakeholders—exporters, producers, customs officers, and embassies—on how origin verifications are conducted.
5. **Balances Flexibility and Accountability**: Allows for reasonable extensions and postponements while maintaining strict deadlines and procedural safeguards.

---

## **Conclusion**

The CPAFTA Verification of Origin Regulations represent a comprehensive, legally grounded, and practical mechanism to ensure the integrity of preferential trade benefits under the Canada–Panama Free Trade Agreement. By setting out clear procedures for verification letters, questionnaires, visits, recordkeeping, and dispute resolution, these regulations promote transparency, fairness, and compliance in cross-border trade.

They are a key component of Canada’s customs enforcement strategy in free trade agreements and reflect a commitment to **international cooperation, regulatory consistency, and accountability** in the administration of tariff preferences.

--- 

✅ **Key Takeaways for Stakeholders**:
- All origin claims must be supported by verifiable evidence.
- Physical visits are only possible with written consent and proper notice.
- Extensions and postponements are permitted under strict time limits.
- Observers can be appointed to ensure transparency.
- Recordkeeping is mandatory and must comply with local laws.
- Non-compliance may result in denial of preferential treatment.

These regulations remain in effect and are subject to future review or amendment as trade practices evolve.
